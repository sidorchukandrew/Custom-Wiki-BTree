      <p><b>Duke of Manchester</b> is a title in the <a href="/wiki/Peerage_of_Great_Britain" title="Peerage of Great Britain">Peerage of Great Britain</a>, and the current senior title of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Montagu" title="House of Montagu">House of Montagu</a>. It was created in 1719 for the politician <a href="/wiki/Charles_Montagu,_1st_Duke_of_Manchester" title="Charles Montagu, 1st Duke of Manchester">Charles Montagu, 4th Earl of Manchester</a>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duke_of_Manchester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Their ancestor was <a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Ladde&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Richard Ladde (page does not exist)">Richard Ladde</a>, grandfather of the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chief_Justice_of_England_and_Wales" title="Lord Chief Justice of England and Wales">Lord Chief Justice</a> <a href="/wiki/Edward_Montagu_(judge)" title="Edward Montagu (judge)">Sir Edward</a>, who changed his name to <a href="/wiki/Montagu_(surname)" title="Montagu (surname)">Montagu</a> around 1447. His descendants claimed a connection with the older house of Montagu or Montacute, <a href="/wiki/Baron_Montagu" title="Baron Montagu">Barons Montagu or Montacute</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Salisbury" title="Earl of Salisbury">Earls of Salisbury</a>, but there is no sound evidence that the two families were related. A case has been made out for the possibility that the Ladde alias came from a division among coheirs about 1420 of the remaining small inheritance of a line of Montagus at Spratton and Little Creton, also in Northamptonshire.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The judge Sir Edward Montagu's grandson, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Montagu,_1st_Baron_Montagu_of_Boughton" title="Edward Montagu, 1st Baron Montagu of Boughton">Edward Montagu</a>, was raised to the peerage as 1st <a href="/wiki/Baron_Montagu_of_Boughton" title="Baron Montagu of Boughton">Baron Montagu of Boughton</a>. He is the ancestor of the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Montagu" title="Duke of Montagu">Dukes of Montagu</a>. His brother, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Montagu,_1st_Earl_of_Manchester" title="Henry Montagu, 1st Earl of Manchester">Sir Henry Montagu</a> (c. 1563–1642), who served as <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chief_Justice_of_the_King%27s_Bench" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Chief Justice of the King's Bench">Lord Chief Justice</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Lord_High_Treasurer_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord High Treasurer of England">Lord High Treasurer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lord_Privy_Seal" title="Lord Privy Seal">Lord Privy Seal</a>, was in 1620 raised to the <a href="/wiki/Peerage_of_England" title="Peerage of England">Peerage of England</a> as <i>Viscount Mandeville</i>, with the additional title <i>Baron Montagu of Kimbolton</i>, of <a href="/wiki/Kimbolton,_Cambridgeshire" title="Kimbolton, Cambridgeshire">Kimbolton</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Huntingdonshire" title="Huntingdonshire">County of Huntingdon</a>. In 1626, he was made <i>Earl of Manchester</i>, of Manchester in the <a href="/wiki/County_of_Lancaster" class="mw-redirect" title="County of Lancaster">County of Lancaster</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Masters_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Masters-2">[2]</a></sup> It is said that the title referred not to the city of <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, but to <a href="/wiki/Godmanchester" title="Godmanchester">Godmanchester</a> in Huntingdonshire. The word "God" was deliberately excluded from the title, as Henry thought it would be blasphemous for him to be known as "Lord Godmanchester".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>His son, the <a href="/wiki/Edward_Montagu,_2nd_Earl_of_Manchester" title="Edward Montagu, 2nd Earl of Manchester">2nd Earl</a>, was a prominent Parliamentary General during the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">Civil War</a>, but later supported the restoration of <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a>. His son, the <a href="/wiki/Robert_Montagu,_3rd_Earl_of_Manchester" title="Robert Montagu, 3rd Earl of Manchester">3rd Earl</a>, represented <a href="/wiki/Huntingdonshire_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Huntingdonshire (UK Parliament constituency)">Huntingdonshire</a> in the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a>. His son was the 4th Earl, who in 1719 was created <i>Duke of Manchester</i>. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Descent">Descent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duke_of_Manchester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Descent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Charles_Montagu,_1st_Duke_of_Manchester" title="Charles Montagu, 1st Duke of Manchester">Charles, 1st Duke of Manchester</a>, was succeeded by his eldest son. The 2nd Duke notably served as <a href="/wiki/Captain_of_the_Yeomen_of_the_Guard" title="Captain of the Yeomen of the Guard">Captain of the Yeomen of the Guard</a> in the administration of <a href="/wiki/Sir_Robert_Walpole" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Robert Walpole">Sir Robert Walpole</a>. He was childless, and on his death, the titles passed to his younger brother, the 3rd Duke. He had earlier represented Huntingdonshire in Parliament. He was succeeded by his son, the 4th Duke. He was <a href="/wiki/British_Ambassador_to_France" class="mw-redirect" title="British Ambassador to France">Ambassador to France</a> and served as <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chamberlain_of_the_Household" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Chamberlain of the Household">Lord Chamberlain of the Household</a>. His son, the 5th Duke, was <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Jamaica" class="mw-redirect" title="Governor of Jamaica">Governor of Jamaica</a> between 1827 and 1830 also held office as <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Postmaster_General" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Postmaster General">Postmaster General</a>. He was succeeded by his son, the 6th Duke. He represented <a href="/wiki/Huntingdon_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Huntingdon (UK Parliament constituency)">Huntingdon</a> in the House of Commons as a <a href="/wiki/Tory" title="Tory">Tory</a>. </p>
      <p>His eldest son, the 7th Duke, was <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> Member of Parliament for <a href="/wiki/Bewdley_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Bewdley (UK Parliament constituency)">Bewdley</a> and Huntingdonshire. His son, the 8th Duke, briefly represented Huntingdonshire in Parliament. He was succeeded by his eldest son, the 9th Duke. He sat on the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal</a> benches in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> and served as <a href="/wiki/Captain_of_the_Yeomen_of_the_Guard" title="Captain of the Yeomen of the Guard">Captain of the Yeomen of the Guard</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Government_1905-1915" class="mw-redirect" title="Liberal Government 1905-1915">Liberal administration</a> of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Campbell-Bannerman" title="Henry Campbell-Bannerman">Sir Henry Campbell-Bannerman</a>. In the twentieth century, mismanagement and profligacy resulted in the wholesale<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="Who says this? (July 2018)">weasel&nbsp;words</span></a></i>]</sup> depletion of the Dukedom's estates. Generational instability caused further damage<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="Who says this? (July 2018)">weasel&nbsp;words</span></a></i>]</sup> to the family's honour:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="Who says this? (July 2018)">weasel&nbsp;words</span></a></i>]</sup> both the 11th and 12th Dukes had a criminal record.<sup id="cite_ref-Scriven_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scriven-4">[4]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seats">Seats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duke_of_Manchester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Seats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

      <p>The principal estate of the Dukes of Manchester was <a href="/wiki/Kimbolton_Castle" title="Kimbolton Castle">Kimbolton Castle</a>. It was sold, together with 50 acres of parkland, by the 10th Duke in 1951, and is now a private school. A remaining 3,250 acres of the estate were sold by his eldest son and heir in 1975. The other family seat was <a href="/wiki/Tandragee_Castle" title="Tandragee Castle">Tandragee Castle</a>, in <a href="/wiki/County_Armagh" title="County Armagh">County Armagh</a>, Northern Ireland. It was sold in 1955, and the remaining estate in 1975, and is now the headquarters of <a href="/wiki/Tayto_(Northern_Ireland)" title="Tayto (Northern Ireland)">Tayto (NI) Ltd.</a><sup id="cite_ref-Scriven_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scriven-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>The arms of the Duke of Manchester have the following <a href="/wiki/Blazon" title="Blazon">blazon</a>: <i>Quarterly, 1st &amp; 4th: Argent, 3 fusils conjoined in fess gules</i> (Montagu)<i>; 2nd &amp; 3rd: Or an eagle displayed vert beaked and membered gules</i> (Monthermer).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The <i>fusils</i> or diamond shapes in the Montagu arms were originally intended to represent a range of mountains,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> as the name comes from the old French <i>mont agu</i> meaning "pointed hill".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The arms represent a claim to be a cadet of the medieval Montagu (Montacute) family, <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Salisbury" title="Earl of Salisbury">earls of Salisbury</a>, for which there is no proof (see above <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Manchester#origins" title="Duke of Manchester">origins</a>). </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Titles_and_styles">Titles and styles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duke_of_Manchester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Titles and styles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The Duke of Manchester holds the subsidiary titles <i>Earl of Manchester</i>, <i>Viscount Mandeville</i>, and <i>Baron Montagu of Kimbolton</i>. </p>
      <p>The Duke of Manchester is styled <i>Your Grace</i> (spoken style) or <i>His Grace</i> (reference style), alternatively <i>Sir</i>. </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Heir_apparent" title="Heir apparent">heir apparent</a> to the Dukedom takes the <a href="/wiki/Courtesy_peer" class="mw-redirect" title="Courtesy peer">courtesy title</a> <i>Viscount Mandeville</i>, and the heir apparent's heir apparent, when such exists, is styled <i>Lord Kimbolton</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Burial_place">Burial place</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duke_of_Manchester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Burial place">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Many members of the Montagu family (Earls and Dukes of Manchester) are buried at St Andrew's Church, <a href="/wiki/Kimbolton,_Cambridgeshire" title="Kimbolton, Cambridgeshire">Kimbolton, Cambridgeshire</a>. Several Montagu monuments still exist in the South Chapel, while the Montagu vault (extended in 1853) is located beneath the North Chapel.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Angus_Montagu,_12th_Duke_of_Manchester" title="Angus Montagu, 12th Duke of Manchester">12th Duke of Manchester</a> was cremated at Bedford Crematorium in 2002. </p>

The Montagus of Boughton, Northhamptonshire, who acquired a barony in 1621, an earldom in 1689, the dukedom of Montagu in 1705, and in their younger branches the earldom of Manchester in 1626, the dukedom of Manchester in 1719, and the earldom of Sandwich in 1660, descended from Richard Montagu alias Ladde, a yeoman or husbandman, living in 1471 at Hanging Houghton, Northamptonshire, where the Laddes had been tenants since the fourteenth century. Alias names, in some respect the forerunners of modern compound (or double-barreled) name, were common in the Middle Ages. In the earliest times, when surnames were new, an alias may just mean indecision between equally attractive alternatives. Later they sometimes indicate bastardy (one name perhaps being the father's and one the mother's), but in most cases probably mark inheritance through an heiress whose name was thus perpetuated. A good case has been made out for the possibility that the Ladde alias came from a division among coheirs about 1420 of the remaining small inheritance of a line of Montagus at Spratton and Little Creton, also in Northamptonshire. This line was of knightly origin and probably a branch of the baronial Montagus (Earls of Salisbury from 1337), whose almost certain ancestor Dru de Montagud was a tenant-in-chief in 1086. Other yeoman Montagus are found in Buckinghamshire from 1354 when Roger Montagu appears as a witness to a quitclaim of land in Great Kimble, notably in Halton where a family of Montagu alias Elot held land from about 1440 to 1610. A line of Montagus found in Waddesdon from about 1540 may have branched from these. These in the eighteenth century were shepherds and drovers and one set up in Aylesbury as a wheelwright and another as a tailor. Another line, also possibly branched from Halton, is found at Boveney and Dorney in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries.
