      <p><b>Lady Jane Grey</b> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 1537</span><sup id="cite_ref-birth_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birth-3">[3]</a></sup> – 12 February 1554), also known as <b>Lady Jane Dudley</b> (after her marriage)<sup id="cite_ref-odnbJane_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-odnbJane-4">[4]</a></sup> and as "the <b>Nine Days' Queen</b>",<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> was an English noblewoman and <i><a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a></i> Queen of England and Ireland from 10 July until 19 July 1553. </p>
      <p>Jane was the great-granddaughter of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry VII</a> through his younger daughter <a href="/wiki/Mary_Tudor,_Queen_of_France" title="Mary Tudor, Queen of France">Mary</a>, and was a first cousin once removed of <a href="/wiki/Edward_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward VI">Edward VI</a>. She had an excellent <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">humanist</a> education and a reputation as one of the most learned young women of her day.<sup id="cite_ref-ascham213_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ascham213-6">[6]</a></sup> In May 1553, she married <a href="/wiki/Lord_Guildford_Dudley" title="Lord Guildford Dudley">Lord Guildford Dudley</a>, a younger son of Edward's chief minister <a href="/wiki/John_Dudley,_Duke_of_Northumberland" class="mw-redirect" title="John Dudley, Duke of Northumberland">John Dudley, Duke of Northumberland</a>. In June 1553, Edward VI wrote his will, nominating Jane and her male heirs as successors to the Crown, in part because his half-sister <a href="/wiki/Mary_I_of_England" title="Mary I of England">Mary</a> was Roman Catholic, while Jane was a committed Protestant and would support the reformed <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>, whose foundation Edward claimed to have laid. The will removed his half-sisters, Mary and <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth</a>, from the line of succession on account of their illegitimacy, subverting their claims under the <a href="/wiki/Third_Succession_Act" title="Third Succession Act">Third Succession Act</a>. </p>
      <p>After Edward's death, Jane was proclaimed queen on 10 July 1553 and awaited coronation in the <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_London" title="Tower of London">Tower of London</a>. Support for Mary grew very quickly, and most of Jane's supporters abandoned her. The <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_England" title="Privy Council of England">Privy Council of England</a> suddenly changed sides and proclaimed Mary as queen on 19 July 1553, deposing Jane. Her primary supporter, her father-in-law the Duke of Northumberland, was accused of treason and executed less than a month later. Jane was held prisoner at the Tower and was convicted in November 1553 of high treason, which carried a sentence of death—though Mary initially spared her life. However, Jane soon became viewed as a threat to the Crown when her father, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Grey,_1st_Duke_of_Suffolk" title="Henry Grey, 1st Duke of Suffolk">Henry Grey, 1st Duke of Suffolk</a>, got involved with <a href="/wiki/Wyatt%27s_rebellion" title="Wyatt's rebellion">Wyatt's rebellion</a> against Queen Mary's intention to marry <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II of Spain</a>. Both Jane and her husband were executed on 12 February 1554. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lady_Jane_Grey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Lady Jane Grey was the eldest daughter of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Grey,_1st_Duke_of_Suffolk" title="Henry Grey, 1st Duke of Suffolk">Henry Grey, 1st Duke of Suffolk</a>, and his wife, <a href="/wiki/Frances_Grey,_Duchess_of_Suffolk" title="Frances Grey, Duchess of Suffolk">Frances</a>. The traditional view is that she was born at <a href="/wiki/Bradgate_Park" title="Bradgate Park">Bradgate Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Leicestershire" title="Leicestershire">Leicestershire</a> in October 1537, while more recent research indicates that she was born somewhat earlier, possibly in London, in late 1536 or in the spring of 1537.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Frances was the elder daughter of <a href="/wiki/King_Henry_VIII" class="mw-redirect" title="King Henry VIII">King Henry VIII</a>'s younger sister, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Tudor,_Queen_of_France" title="Mary Tudor, Queen of France">Mary</a>. Jane had two younger sisters, <a href="/wiki/Lady_Catherine_Grey" class="mw-redirect" title="Lady Catherine Grey">Lady Catherine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lady_Mary_Grey" title="Lady Mary Grey">Lady Mary</a>; through their mother, the three sisters were great-granddaughters of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry VII</a>, grandnieces of Henry VIII, and first cousins once removed of <a href="/wiki/Edward_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward VI">Edward VI</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mary_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Mary I">Mary I</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth I">Elizabeth I</a>. </p>
      <p>Jane received a humanist education, studying <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> with <a href="/wiki/John_Aylmer_(bishop)" title="John Aylmer (bishop)">John Aylmer</a>, and Italian with <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo_Florio" title="Michelangelo Florio">Michelangelo Florio</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Through the influence of her father and her tutors, she became a committed Protestant and also corresponded with the <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" title="Zürich">Zürich</a> reformer <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Bullinger" title="Heinrich Bullinger">Heinrich Bullinger</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> </p>
      <p> Jane preferred book studies to hunting parties<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and regarded her strict upbringing, which was typical of the time,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> as harsh. To the visiting scholar <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ascham" title="Roger Ascham">Roger Ascham</a>, who found her reading <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>, she is said to have complained: </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p> For when I am in the presence either of father or mother, whether I speak, keep silence, sit, stand or go, eat, drink, be merry or sad, be sewing, playing, dancing, or doing anything else, I must do it as it were in such weight, measure and number, even so perfectly as God made the world; or else I am so sharply taunted, so cruelly threatened, yea presently sometimes with pinches, nips and bobs and other ways (which I will not name for the honour I bear them) ... that I think myself in hell.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
      </blockquote> 
      <p>In early February 1547, Jane was sent to live in the household of Edward VI's uncle, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Seymour,_1st_Baron_Seymour_of_Sudeley" title="Thomas Seymour, 1st Baron Seymour of Sudeley">Thomas Seymour</a>, who soon married Henry VIII's widow, <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Parr" title="Catherine Parr">Catherine Parr</a>. Jane lived with the couple until Catherine's <a href="/wiki/Death_in_childbirth" class="mw-redirect" title="Death in childbirth">death in childbirth</a> in September 1548.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contracts_for_marriage">Contracts for marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lady_Jane_Grey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Contracts for marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Lady Jane acted as chief mourner at Catherine Parr's funeral; Thomas Seymour showed continued interest to keep her in his household, and she returned there for about two months before he was arrested at the end of 1548.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Seymour's brother, the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Protector" title="Lord Protector">Lord Protector</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Seymour,_1st_Duke_of_Somerset" title="Edward Seymour, 1st Duke of Somerset">Edward Seymour, 1st Duke of Somerset</a>, felt threatened by Thomas' popularity with the young King Edward. Among other things, Thomas Seymour was charged with proposing Jane as a bride for the king.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the course of Thomas Seymour's following <a href="/wiki/Attainder" title="Attainder">attainder</a> and execution, Jane's father was lucky to stay largely out of trouble. After his fourth interrogation by the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_England" title="Privy Council of England">King's Council</a>, he proposed his daughter Jane as a bride for the Protector's eldest son, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Seymour,_1st_Earl_of_Hertford" title="Edward Seymour, 1st Earl of Hertford">Lord Hertford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Nothing came of this, however, and Jane was not engaged until the spring of 1553, her bridegroom being <a href="/wiki/Lord_Guildford_Dudley" title="Lord Guildford Dudley">Lord Guildford Dudley</a>, a younger son of <a href="/wiki/John_Dudley,_1st_Duke_of_Northumberland" title="John Dudley, 1st Duke of Northumberland">John Dudley, 1st Duke of Northumberland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The Duke, <a href="/wiki/Lord_President_of_the_Council" title="Lord President of the Council">Lord President</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Privy_council" title="Privy council">King's Council</a> from late 1549, was then the most powerful man in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> On 25 May 1553, the couple were married at <a href="/wiki/Durham_House_(London)" class="mw-redirect" title="Durham House (London)">Durham House</a> in a triple wedding, in which Jane's sister <a href="/wiki/Lady_Catherine_Grey" class="mw-redirect" title="Lady Catherine Grey">Catherine</a> was matched with the heir of the <a href="/wiki/William_Herbert,_1st_Earl_of_Pembroke_(1551_creation)" class="mw-redirect" title="William Herbert, 1st Earl of Pembroke (1551 creation)">Earl of Pembroke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Herbert,_2nd_Earl_of_Pembroke" title="Henry Herbert, 2nd Earl of Pembroke">Lord Herbert</a>, and another <a href="/wiki/Katherine_Hastings,_Countess_of_Huntingdon" title="Katherine Hastings, Countess of Huntingdon">Katherine</a>, Lord Guildford's sister, with <a href="/wiki/Henry_Hastings,_3rd_Earl_of_Huntingdon" title="Henry Hastings, 3rd Earl of Huntingdon">Henry Hastings</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Francis_Hastings,_2nd_Earl_of_Huntingdon" title="Francis Hastings, 2nd Earl of Huntingdon">Earl of Huntingdon</a>'s heir.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Claim_to_the_throne_and_accession">Claim to the throne and accession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lady_Jane_Grey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Claim to the throne and accession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       See also: 
       <a href="/wiki/Third_Succession_Act" title="Third Succession Act">Third Succession Act</a>
      </div>

      <p>The Third Succession Act of 1544 restored Henry VIII's daughters, Mary and Elizabeth, to the line of succession, although they were still regarded as illegitimate. Furthermore, this Act authorised Henry VIII to alter the succession by his will. Henry's will reinforced the succession of his three children, and then declared that, should none of them leave descendants, the throne would pass to heirs of his younger sister, Mary, which included Jane. For unknown reasons, Henry excluded Jane's mother, Frances Grey, from the succession,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and also bypassed the claims of the descendants of his elder sister, <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Tudor" title="Margaret Tudor">Margaret</a>, who had married into the Scottish royal house and nobility. </p>
      <p>Both Mary and Elizabeth had been named illegitimate by statute during the reign of Henry VIII after his marriages to <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Aragon" title="Catherine of Aragon">Catherine of Aragon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anne_Boleyn" title="Anne Boleyn">Anne Boleyn</a> had been declared void.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> When the 15-year-old Edward VI lay dying in the early summer of 1553, his Catholic half-sister Mary was still his heir presumptive. However, Edward, in a draft will ("My devise for the Succession") composed earlier in 1553, had first restricted the succession to (non-existent) male descendants of Frances Brandon and her daughters, before he named his Protestant cousin "Lady Jane and her heirs male" as his successors, probably in June 1553; the intent was to ensure his Protestant legacy, thereby bypassing Mary, who was a Roman Catholic.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Alford_2002_171–172_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alford_2002_171–172-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Edward's decision to name Jane Grey herself was possibly instigated by Northumberland.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-auto_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-30">[30]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Edward VI personally supervised the copying of his will which was finally issued as letters patent on 21 June and signed by 102 notables, among them the whole <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Privy Council">Privy Council</a>, peers, bishops, judges, and <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Aldermen" title="Court of Aldermen">London aldermen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Edward also announced to have his "declaration" passed in <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">parliament</a> in September, and the necessary <a href="/wiki/Writs" class="mw-redirect" title="Writs">writs</a> were prepared.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-30">[30]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>The King died on 6 July 1553, but his death was not announced until four days later.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> On 9 July Jane was informed that she was now queen, and according to her own later claims, accepted the crown only with reluctance. On 10 July, she was officially proclaimed Queen of England, France and Ireland after she had taken up secure residence in the <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_London" title="Tower of London">Tower of London</a>, where English monarchs customarily resided from the time of accession until coronation. Jane refused to name her husband Dudley as king, because that would require an <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act of Parliament</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ives_p._189_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ives_p._189-33">[33]</a></sup> She would agree only to make him <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Clarence" title="Duke of Clarence">Duke of Clarence</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>Northumberland faced a number of key tasks to consolidate his <a href="/wiki/Political_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Political power">power</a> after Edward's death. Most importantly, he had to isolate and, ideally, capture Mary Tudor to prevent her from gathering support. As soon as Mary was sure of King Edward's demise, she left her residence at <a href="/wiki/Hunsdon" title="Hunsdon">Hunsdon</a> and set out to <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a>, where she began to rally her supporters. Northumberland set out from London with troops on 14 July to capture Mary. The <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_England" title="Privy Council of England">Privy Council</a> switched their allegiance and proclaimed Mary queen in London, on 19 July. The historical consensus assumes that this was in recognition of overwhelming support of the population for Mary. However, there is no clear evidence for that outside Norfolk and Suffolk, where Northumberland had put down Kett's Rebellion; hence, where princess Mary sought refuge. Rather, it seems that <a href="/wiki/Henry_FitzAlan,_19th_Earl_of_Arundel" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry FitzAlan, 19th Earl of Arundel">Henry FitzAlan, 19th Earl of Arundel</a>—whom Northumberland had arrested and detained twice as an ally of Somerset, before rehabilitating—engineered a coup d'état in the Privy Council in Northumberland's absence.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Jane is often called the Nine-Day Queen, although if her reign is dated from the moment of Edward's death on 6 July 1553, her reign could have been a few days longer.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> On 19 July 1553, Jane was imprisoned in the Tower's Gentleman Gaoler's apartments, her husband in the Beauchamp Tower. The Duke of Northumberland was executed on 22 August 1553. In September, Parliament declared Mary the rightful successor and denounced and revoked Jane's proclamation as that of a usurper.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trial_and_execution">Trial and execution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lady_Jane_Grey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Trial and execution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Referred to by the court as Jane Dudley, wife of Guildford, Jane was charged with <a href="/wiki/High_treason" class="mw-redirect" title="High treason">high treason</a>, as were her husband, two of his brothers, and the former <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Cranmer" title="Thomas Cranmer">Thomas Cranmer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-37">[37]</a></sup> Their trial, by a special commission, took place on 13 November 1553, at <a href="/wiki/Guildhall,_London" title="Guildhall, London">Guildhall</a> in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a>. The commission was chaired by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_White_(merchant)" title="Thomas White (merchant)">Sir Thomas White</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lord_Mayor_of_London" title="Lord Mayor of London">Lord Mayor of London</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Howard,_3rd_Duke_of_Norfolk" title="Thomas Howard, 3rd Duke of Norfolk">Thomas Howard, 3rd Duke of Norfolk</a>. Other members included <a href="/wiki/Edward_Stanley,_3rd_Earl_of_Derby" title="Edward Stanley, 3rd Earl of Derby">Edward Stanley, 3rd Earl of Derby</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Bourchier,_2nd_Earl_of_Bath" title="John Bourchier, 2nd Earl of Bath">John Bourchier, 2nd Earl of Bath</a>. As was to be expected, all defendants were found guilty and sentenced to death. Jane's guilt, of having treacherously assumed the title and the power of the monarch, was evidenced by a number of documents she had signed as "Jane the Quene".<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-37">[37]</a></sup> Her sentence was to "be burned alive on Tower Hill or beheaded as the Queen pleases" (burning was the <a href="/wiki/Execution_by_burning#Historical_usage" class="mw-redirect" title="Execution by burning">traditional English punishment</a> for <a href="/wiki/Treason" title="Treason">treason</a> committed by women).<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/List_of_ambassadors_of_the_Kingdom_of_England_to_the_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="List of ambassadors of the Kingdom of England to the Holy Roman Emperor">imperial ambassador</a> reported to <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor</a>, that her life was to be spared.<sup id="cite_ref-odnbJane_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-odnbJane-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">

      <p>The rebellion of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Wyatt_the_Younger" title="Thomas Wyatt the Younger">Thomas Wyatt the Younger</a> in January 1554 against Queen Mary's marriage plans with <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip of Spain</a> sealed Jane's fate. Her father, Henry Grey, 1st Duke of Suffolk, and his two brothers joined the rebellion, and so the government decided to go through with the verdict against Jane and Guildford. Their execution was first scheduled for 9 February 1554, but was then postponed for three days to give Jane a chance to convert to the Catholic faith. Mary sent her chaplain <a href="/wiki/John_Feckenham" title="John Feckenham">John Feckenham</a> to Jane, who was initially not pleased about this.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Though she would not give in to his efforts "to save her soul", she became friends with him and allowed him to accompany her to the scaffold.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> </p>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">

      <p>On the morning of 12 February 1554, the authorities took Guildford from his rooms at the Tower of London to the public <a href="/wiki/Execution" class="mw-redirect" title="Execution">execution</a> place at <a href="/wiki/Tower_Hill" title="Tower Hill">Tower Hill</a>, where he was beheaded. A horse and cart brought his remains back to the Tower, past the rooms where Jane was staying. Seeing her husband's corpse return, Jane is reported to have exclaimed: "Oh, Guildford, Guildford."<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> She was then taken out to <a href="/wiki/Tower_Green" title="Tower Green">Tower Green</a>, inside the Tower, to be beheaded.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>According to the account of her execution given in the anonymous <i>Chronicle of Queen Jane and of Two Years of Queen Mary</i>, which formed the basis for <a href="/wiki/Raphael_Holinshed" title="Raphael Holinshed">Raphael Holinshed</a>'s depiction, Jane gave a speech upon ascending the scaffold: </p> 
      <blockquote>
       <p>Good people, I am come hither to die, and by a law I am condemned to the same. The fact, indeed, against the Queen's highness was unlawful, and the consenting thereunto by me: but touching the procurement and desire thereof by me or on my behalf, I do wash my hands thereof in innocency, before God, and the face of you, good Christian people, this day.<sup id="cite_ref-ladyjane_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ladyjane-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
      </blockquote> 
      <p>While admitting to action considered unlawful, she declared that "I do wash my hands thereof in innocence".<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Jane then recited <a href="/wiki/Psalm_51" title="Psalm 51">Psalm 51</a> (<i>Have mercy upon me, O God</i>) in English, and handed her gloves and handkerchief to her maid. The executioner asked her forgiveness, which she granted him, pleading: "I pray you dispatch me quickly." Referring to her head, she asked, "Will you take it off before I lay me down?", and the axeman answered: "No, madam." She then blindfolded herself. Jane then failed to find the block with her hands, and cried, "What shall I do? Where is it?" Probably <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Brydges" title="Thomas Brydges">Sir Thomas Brydges</a>, the Deputy Lieutenant of the Tower, helped her find her way. With her head on the block, Jane spoke the last words of Jesus as recounted by <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke" title="Gospel of Luke">Luke</a>: "Lord, into thy hands I commend my spirit!"<sup id="cite_ref-ladyjane_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ladyjane-43">[43]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Jane and Guildford are buried in the Chapel of <a href="/wiki/Church_of_St_Peter_ad_Vincula" title="Church of St Peter ad Vincula">St Peter ad Vincula</a> on the north side of Tower Green. No memorial stone was erected at their grave.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Jane's father, the Duke of Suffolk, was executed 11 days after Jane, on 23 February 1554.<sup id="cite_ref-CP_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CP-47">[47]</a></sup> Her mother, the Duchess of Suffolk, married her Master of the Horse and <a href="/wiki/Chamberlain_(office)" title="Chamberlain (office)">chamberlain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Stokes_(Master_of_Horse)" class="mw-redirect" title="Adrian Stokes (Master of Horse)">Adrian Stokes</a>, in March 1555 (not, as often said, three weeks after the execution of the Duke of Suffolk).<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> She was fully pardoned by Mary and allowed to live at Court with her two surviving daughters. She died in 1559. </p>

      <p>"The traitor-heroine of the Reformation", as historian <a href="/wiki/Albert_Pollard" title="Albert Pollard">Albert Pollard</a> called her,<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> was only 16 or 17 years old at the time of her execution. During and in the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Protestant_martyrs_of_the_English_Reformation" title="List of Protestant martyrs of the English Reformation">Marian persecutions</a>, Jane became viewed as a Protestant martyr for centuries, featuring prominently in the several editions of the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Martyrs" class="mw-redirect" title="Book of Martyrs">Book of Martyrs</a></i> (<i>Actes and Monuments of these Latter and Perillous Dayes</i>) by <a href="/wiki/John_Foxe" title="John Foxe">John Foxe</a>. The tale of Lady Jane grew to legendary proportions in popular culture, producing romantic biographies, novels, plays, operas, paintings, and films. </p>
      <p>Jane Grey is the only English monarch in the last 500 years (though whether her short reign was legitimate is disputed) of whom no proven contemporary portrait survives.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-telegraph_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph-50">[50]</a></sup> A painting in London's <a href="/wiki/National_Portrait_Gallery_(United_Kingdom)" class="mw-redirect" title="National Portrait Gallery (United Kingdom)">National Portrait Gallery</a> was thought to be Jane for many years, but in 1996 it was confirmed to be of Catherine Parr.<sup id="cite_ref-yale_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yale-51">[51]</a></sup> A portrait believed by some experts to be of Jane was discovered in a private home in 2005. Painted 40 to 50 years after Jane's death, the "<a href="/wiki/Streatham_portrait" title="Streatham portrait">Streatham portrait</a>" (so called after the area of London in which it resided for decades) depicts a young woman dressed in a red gown, adorned with jewels and holding a prayer book.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-1">[1]</a></sup> Historian and Tudor specialist Dr. <a href="/wiki/David_Starkey" title="David Starkey">David Starkey</a> is sceptical, "It's an appallingly bad picture and there's absolutely no reason to suppose it's got anything to do with Lady Jane Grey".<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Another portrait, a miniature, was shown to the news media in 2007 by Starkey who stated that he was "90 per cent certain" that it is of Lady Jane Grey.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> This painting had been discovered at the <a href="/wiki/Yale_Center_for_British_Art" title="Yale Center for British Art">Yale Center for British Art</a> in America.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> </p>
