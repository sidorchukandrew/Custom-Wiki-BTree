      <p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Bulgarians" title="Bulgarians">Bulgarian</a> name system</b> has considerable similarities with most other <a href="/wiki/Europea" class="mw-redirect" title="Europea">European</a> name systems, and with those of other <a href="/wiki/Slavic_peoples" class="mw-redirect" title="Slavic peoples">Slavic peoples</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Slavic_naming_customs" title="Eastern Slavic naming customs">Russian</a> name system, though it has certain unique features. </p>
      <p>Bulgarian names usually consist of a <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a>, which comes first, a <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a>, which is second (and is usually omitted when referring to the person), and a <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family name</a>, which comes last. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bulgarian_given_names">Bulgarian given names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bulgarian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Bulgarian given names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Traditionally, the Bulgarian given names are either of <a href="/wiki/Slavic_names" title="Slavic names">Slavic origin</a> or from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> when reflecting <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> faith (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Peter_(first_name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Peter (first name)">Petar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mary_(mother_of_Jesus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mary (mother of Jesus)">Maria</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="John (name)">Ivan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theodora_(given_name)" title="Theodora (given name)">Teodora</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_(given_name)" title="George (given name)">Georgi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicholas" title="Nicholas">Nikolay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael" title="Michael">Mihail</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paraskevi_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Paraskevi (disambiguation)">Paraskeva</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dimitar" title="Dimitar">Dimitar</a>). </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Slavic_names" title="Slavic names">Slavic names</a> may describe the appearance or character of the person, may constitute a wish or even stem from pre-Christian conjuring rituals and meant not to attract the <a href="/wiki/Slavic_mythology" class="mw-redirect" title="Slavic mythology">evil spirits</a>. Below are listed examples of Bulgarian <a href="/wiki/Slavic_names" title="Slavic names">Slavic names</a> and Bulgarian names of Greek, Latin, or Hebrew origin: </p>

      <p>In addition, some Bulgarian names may be of <a href="/wiki/Thracians" title="Thracians">Thracian</a> (e.g. Neno, Dako, Boto, Geto) or <a href="/wiki/Bulgars" title="Bulgars">Bulgar</a> (Boris, <a href="/wiki/Boyan_(given_name)" title="Boyan (given name)">Boyan</a>, Biser) origin. </p>
      <p>Since the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_National_Revival" title="Bulgarian National Revival">Bulgarian National Revival</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Liberation_of_Bulgaria" title="Liberation of Bulgaria">Liberation of Bulgaria</a> in 1878 names of successful <a href="/wiki/List_of_Bulgarian_monarchs" title="List of Bulgarian monarchs">medieval Bulgarian rulers</a>, like <a href="/wiki/Asen_dynasty" title="Asen dynasty">Asen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asparukh_of_Bulgaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Asparukh of Bulgaria">Asparuh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ivailo_of_Bulgaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Ivailo of Bulgaria">Ivaylo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samuil_of_Bulgaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Samuil of Bulgaria">Samuil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Simeon_I_of_Bulgaria" title="Simeon I of Bulgaria">Simeon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Krum_of_Bulgaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Krum of Bulgaria">Krum</a>, have also gained a lot of popularity. </p>
      <p>Traditionally, the parents would often name their child after an older relative, so that his/her name would live on in the family. Today, however, these are not binding conditions and are often ignored: parents often pick a name without conforming with these traditions, however it is really up to the parents as still many of them continue to observe these traditions. Traditions to name a child after the parents' <a href="/wiki/Best_man" class="mw-redirect" title="Best man">best man</a> or <a href="/wiki/Maid_of_honor" class="mw-redirect" title="Maid of honor">maid of honor</a> or the saint on whose <a href="/wiki/Name_day" title="Name day">name day</a> the child is born also exist. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bulgarian_patronymics_and_family_names">Bulgarian patronymics and family names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bulgarian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Bulgarian patronymics and family names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bulgarian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Typically, a Bulgarian person inherits the last name of his father's family (family name), as well as a patronymic based on his father's given name, with a gender-<a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">agreeing</a> <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffix</a> usually added. For example, Stoyan <i>Georgi<u>ev</u></i> <i>Draganov</i> would be the son of <i>Georgi</i> Petkov <i>Draganov</i>. The same person's daughter would bear the names <i>Georgi<u>eva</u></i> <i>Draganov<u>a</u></i>. Another way of preserving the family name and the name of a specific ancestor would be the following circulating of the names: if the father's name is Ivan Petrov Mihailov, the son is named Petar Ivanov Mihailov. Then if Petar has a son, his name will be Ivan Petrov Mihailov, and so on. </p>
      <p>Until recently, if the father's name was not a specific family name, the child would take his patronymic as a family name, so names in a chain of generations would shift. For example, the son of Petar Stoyanov Ivanov would be Georgi Petrov Stoyanov, his son would be Ivaylo Georgiev Petrov, his son would be Marin Ivaylov Georgiev. This practice often caused confusion in the past, especially when dealing for foreign institutions, since a child did not bear his father's family name. However, the recent generation has all but abandoned this practice.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>When marrying, today a woman may choose either to adopt her husband's family name, retain her <a href="/wiki/Maiden_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Maiden name">maiden name</a> or combine the two using a hyphen. For instance, when marrying Nikolay Petrov, Maria Bogdanova could become Petrova, remain Bogdanova or adopt Petrova-Bogdanova or Bogdanova-Petrova. Historically, she would adopt her husband's name. In any case, a woman retains her patronymic, which she has inherited from her father. </p>
      <p>The Family Code (Bulgarian law regulating wedlock, cohabitation and family) allows the reverse as well: the husband may also take or add his wife's family name. </p>
      <p>Sometimes a person becomes well known with his patronymic, e.g. sci-fi writer <a href="/wiki/Lyuben_Dilov" title="Lyuben Dilov">Lyuben Dilov</a>'s full name is Lyuben Dilov Ivanov, while the Bulgarian <a href="/wiki/Cosmonaut" class="mw-redirect" title="Cosmonaut">cosmonaut</a> <a href="/wiki/Georgi_Ivanov_(cosmonaut)" title="Georgi Ivanov (cosmonaut)">Georgi Ivanov</a>'s full name is Georgi Ivanov Kakalov. </p>
      <p>Among <a href="/wiki/Bulgarians_in_Serbia" title="Bulgarians in Serbia">Bulgarians in Serbia</a>, as well as in other countries throughout the world, the <i>a</i> ending of women's surnames is usually dropped, resulting in names such as Elizabeta Nikolov instead of Nikolova. Other examples of changes in the family name of Bulgarians living abroad is how Nikolov turns into Nikoloff. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bulgarian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In most cases (though by no means always), the etymology of Bulgarian patronymics and family names closely corresponds to that of given names. Many families bear the name of the family's founder, adding the patronymic Slavic suffix "–ov/–ev" (men) or "–ova/–eva" (women) (e.g. Ivanov, Radeva, Parvanov, Petrova, Asenov, Tsvetanova). Family names may indicate the occupation of the founder, his nickname or origin, in which case names of <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turkish_language" title="Ottoman Turkish language">Ottoman Turkish</a> or Greek etymology can be found in addition to those of Slavic origin (e.g. Kolarov, Kalaydzhieva, Popova, Cholakov, Kovacheva, Daskalov, Tepavicharov, Uzunova). </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Suffixes">Suffixes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bulgarian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Suffixes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Most Bulgarian surnames end with "–ov(a)" or "–ev(a)". They work the same way as the <a href="/wiki/Patrynomic" class="mw-redirect" title="Patrynomic">patrynomic</a> (and are in fact derived from them), and are possessive forms of given names. The "–ov/–ev" (masculine) and "–ova/–eva" (feminine) suffixes are also extremely popular and commonly used by <a href="/wiki/Russians" title="Russians">Russians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_Macedonians" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethnic Macedonians">Macedonians</a>. Consequently, the surnames Ivanov(a), Petrov(a) and Borisov(a) are the three most common surnames in Bulgaria. As these "–ov/–ev" surnames are indistinguishable from patronymics, it is not uncommon for people to have the same patronymic and surname; for instance the children of Petar Ivanov Petrov would be Georgi Petrov Petrov and Lyudmila Petrova Petrova. (This would also be distinguished from Russians, who would be named "Georgi Petrovich Petrov" and "Lyudmila Petrovna Petrova".) </p>
      <p>Although most popular, "–ov/–ev" and respectively "–ova/–eva" are not the only patronymic and family name suffixes. The second most popular suffix is "–ski/–ska" (sometimes "–ki/–ka") (e.g. Zelenogorski, Stoykovska, Petrinska), which, besides often being merely a version of an "–ov/–ev" or "-–ova/–eva" name, may also often indicate origin (e.g. Sofianski — "from <a href="/wiki/Sofia" title="Sofia">Sofia</a>", Stamboliyski — "from <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>"). This suffix is not only popular with Bulgarians but with most other Slavic people, such as <a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">Polish</a>, Macedonians, Russians (most often spelled as "-sky"), <a href="/wiki/Belarusians" title="Belarusians">Belarusians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukrainians" title="Ukrainians">Ukrainians</a>. </p>
      <p>Another suffix is "–in/–ina" (e.g. Kunin, Ganina, Radin). Unlike all other Bulgarian patronymics and family names, these stem from a female name (e.g. "of Kuna", "of Gana", "of Rada"). They are most common in the region of Razlog and Bansko. The "–in" suffix is also popular with Russians. </p>
      <p>For these three most popular suffixes, there is also a plural form used when referring to the family as a whole or several members of it (as opposed to a single member). For "–ov/–ova" and "–ev/–eva" it is "–ovi/–evi", for "–ski/–ska" it is "–ski" and for "–in/–ina" the form is "–ini". </p>
      <p>Historically, the universal suffix "–ovich" and "-evich" was quite popular in some regions (bearers of such names include <a href="/wiki/Gavril_Krastevich" title="Gavril Krastevich">Gavril Krastevich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hristofor_Zhefarovich" class="mw-redirect" title="Hristofor Zhefarovich">Hristofor Zhefarovich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petar_Parchevich" title="Petar Parchevich">Petar Parchevich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kiril_Peychinovich" title="Kiril Peychinovich">Kiril Peychinovich</a>, etc.), particularly among the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism_in_Bulgaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism in Bulgaria">Roman Catholic Bulgarians</a>, but has today largely fallen out of use and is more typical for the <a href="/wiki/Serbian_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Serbian name">Serbo</a>-<a href="/wiki/Croatian_name" title="Croatian name">Croatian</a> name system (where it is usually spelled "<a href="/wiki/-i%C4%87" class="mw-redirect" title="-ić">-ić</a>"). </p>
      <p>In addition, other suffixes also exist: for instance, names like Tihanek, Kozlek, Lomek (suffixed "–ek") were historically dominant in the town of <a href="/wiki/Koprivshtitsa" title="Koprivshtitsa">Koprivshtitsa</a>.<sup><a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20070827060123/http://www.koprivshtitza.com/index_eng.html">[1]</a> (Internet Archive link)</sup> </p>
      <p>Names lacking a suffix, though often foreign-sounding, have been more popular in the past, but still exist today (e.g. Beron, Tomash), despite being quite uncommon. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Most_popular_names">Most popular names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bulgarian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Most popular names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
