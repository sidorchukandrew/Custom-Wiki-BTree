      <p>A <b>house</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">building</a> that functions as a <a href="/wiki/Home" title="Home">home</a>. They can range from simple <a href="/wiki/Dwelling" title="Dwelling">dwellings</a> such as rudimentary <a href="/wiki/Hut" title="Hut">huts</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nomadic_tribes" class="mw-redirect" title="Nomadic tribes">nomadic tribes</a> and the improvised <a href="/wiki/Shack" title="Shack">shacks</a> in <a href="/wiki/Shantytown" class="mw-redirect" title="Shantytown">shantytowns</a> to complex, fixed structures of wood, brick, concrete or other materials containing plumbing, ventilation, and electrical systems.<sup id="cite_ref-schoenauer_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schoenauer-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Houses use a range of different <a href="/wiki/Roof" title="Roof">roofing</a> systems to keep precipitation such as rain from getting into the dwelling space. Houses may have <a href="/wiki/Door" title="Door">doors</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lock_(security_device)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lock (security device)">locks</a> to secure the dwelling space and protect its inhabitants and contents from burglars or other trespassers. Most conventional modern houses in Western cultures will contain one or more <a href="/wiki/Bedroom" title="Bedroom">bedrooms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bathroom" title="Bathroom">bathrooms</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Kitchen" title="Kitchen">kitchen</a> or cooking area, and a <a href="/wiki/Living_room" title="Living room">living room</a>. A house may have a separate <a href="/wiki/Dining_room" title="Dining room">dining room</a>, or the eating area may be integrated into another room. Some large houses in North America have a <a href="/wiki/Recreation_room" title="Recreation room">recreation room</a>. In traditional agriculture-oriented societies, <a href="/wiki/List_of_domesticated_animals" title="List of domesticated animals">domestic animals</a> such as chickens or larger livestock (like cattle) may share part of the house with humans. The social unit that lives in a house is known as a <a href="/wiki/Household" title="Household">household</a>. </p>
      <p>Most commonly, a <a href="/wiki/Household" title="Household">household</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a> unit of some kind, although households may also be other <a href="/wiki/Group_(sociology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Group (sociology)">social groups</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Roommate" title="Roommate">roommates</a> or, in a <a href="/wiki/Rooming_house" title="Rooming house">rooming house</a>, unconnected individuals. Some houses only have a dwelling space for one family or similar-sized group; larger houses called <a href="/wiki/Townhouse" title="Townhouse">townhouses</a> or <a href="/wiki/Row_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Row house">row houses</a> may contain numerous family dwellings in the same structure. A house may be accompanied by outbuildings, such as a <a href="/wiki/Garage_(residential)" title="Garage (residential)">garage</a> for vehicles or a <a href="/wiki/Shed" title="Shed">shed</a> for gardening equipment and tools. A house may have a backyard or frontyard, which serve as additional areas where inhabitants can relax or eat. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:94px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Beowulf_-_hus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d1/Beowulf_-_hus.jpg" decoding="async" width="92" height="67" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="92" data-file-height="67"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Beowulf_-_hus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>Hus, an 
         <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> word
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>The English word <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/house" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:house">house</a></i> derives directly from the Old English <i>hus</i> meaning "dwelling, shelter, home, house," which in turn derives from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" class="mw-redirect" title="Proto-Germanic">Proto-Germanic</a> <i>husan</i> (reconstructed by etymological analysis) which is of unknown origin.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The house itself gave rise to the letter 'B' through an early <a href="/wiki/Proto-Semitic_language" title="Proto-Semitic language">Proto-Semitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Hieroglyphic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hieroglyphic">hieroglyphic</a> symbol depicting a house. The symbol was called <a href="/wiki/Bet_(letter)" title="Bet (letter)">"bayt", "bet" or "beth"</a> in various related languages, and became <i><a href="/wiki/Beta" title="Beta">beta</a></i>, the Greek letter, before it was used by the Romans.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Beit" title="Beit">Beit</a></i> in Arabic means house, while in Maltese <i>bejt</i> refers to the roof of the house.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elements">Elements</span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Layout">Layout</span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Gingerbread_House_Essex_CT.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Gingerbread_House_Essex_CT.jpg/220px-Gingerbread_House_Essex_CT.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Gingerbread_House_Essex_CT.jpg/330px-Gingerbread_House_Essex_CT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Gingerbread_House_Essex_CT.jpg/440px-Gingerbread_House_Essex_CT.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="431"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Gingerbread_House_Essex_CT.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>Example of an early 
         <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Victorian</a> "Gingerbread House" in 
         <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, United States, built in 1855
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>Ideally, <a href="/wiki/Architect" title="Architect">architects</a> of houses design <a href="/wiki/Room" title="Room">rooms</a> to meet the needs of the people who will live in the house. <a href="/wiki/Feng_shui" title="Feng shui">Feng shui</a>, originally a <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> method of moving houses according to such factors as rain and micro-climates, has recently expanded its scope to address the design of interior spaces, with a view to promoting harmonious effects on the people living inside the house, although no actual effect has ever been demonstrated. Feng shui can also mean the "aura" in or around a dwelling, making it comparable to the <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">real-estate</a> sales concept of "indoor-outdoor flow". </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Square_footage" class="mw-redirect" title="Square footage">square footage</a> of a house in the United States reports the area of "living space", excluding the garage and other non-living spaces. The "square metres" figure of a house in Europe reports the area of the walls enclosing the home, and thus includes any attached garage and non-living spaces.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The number of floors or levels making up the house can affect the square footage of a home. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parts">Parts</span></h3> 
      <p>Many houses have several large rooms with specialized functions and several very small rooms for other various reasons. These may include a living/eating area, a sleeping area, and (if suitable facilities and services exist) separate or combined washing and <a href="/wiki/Toilet_(room)" title="Toilet (room)">lavatory</a> areas. Some larger properties may also feature rooms such as a spa room, indoor pool, indoor basketball court, and other 'non-essential' facilities. In traditional agriculture-oriented societies, <a href="/wiki/List_of_domesticated_animals" title="List of domesticated animals">domestic animals</a> such as chickens or larger livestock (like cattle) often share part of the house with human beings. Most conventional modern houses will at least contain a <a href="/wiki/Bedroom" title="Bedroom">bedroom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bathroom" title="Bathroom">bathroom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kitchen" title="Kitchen">kitchen</a> or cooking area, and a <a href="/wiki/Living_room" title="Living room">living room</a>. A typical "<a href="/wiki/American_Foursquare" title="American Foursquare">foursquare</a> house" (as pictured) occurred commonly in the early history of the US where they were mainly built, with a <a href="/wiki/Staircase" class="mw-redirect" title="Staircase">staircase</a> in the center of the house, surrounded by four rooms, and connected to other sections of the home (including in more recent eras a <a href="/wiki/Garage_(house)" class="mw-redirect" title="Garage (house)">garage</a>). </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_interior">History of the interior</span></h3>

      <p>Little is known about the earliest origin of the house and its interior, however it can be traced back to the simplest form of shelters. <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> architect <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvius</a>' theories have claimed the first form of <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a> as a frame of timber branches finished in mud, also known as the <a href="/wiki/Primitive_hut" class="mw-redirect" title="Primitive hut">primitive hut</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup> Philip Tabor later states the contribution of 17th century Dutch houses as the foundation of houses today. </p> 
      <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>As far as the idea of the home is concerned, the home of the home is the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>. This idea's crystallization might be dated to the first three-quarters of the 17th century, when the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> Netherlands amassed the unprecedented and unrivalled accumulation of capital, and emptied their purses into domestic space.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Manor_house" title="Manor house">Manor Houses</a> facilitated different activities and events. Furthermore, the houses accommodated numerous people, including family, relatives, employees, servants and their guests.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup> Their lifestyles were largely communal, as areas such as the <a href="/wiki/Great_Hall" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Hall">Great Hall</a> enforced the custom of dining and meetings and the <a href="/wiki/Solar_(room)" title="Solar (room)">Solar</a> intended for shared sleeping beds.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Interconnecting_rooms">Interconnecting rooms</span></h4> 
      <p>During the 15th and 16th centuries, the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance_architecture" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian Renaissance architecture">Italian Renaissance Palazzo</a> consisted of plentiful rooms of connectivity. Unlike the qualities and uses of the Manor Houses, most rooms of the <a href="/wiki/Palazzo" class="mw-redirect" title="Palazzo">palazzo</a> contained no purpose, yet were given several <a href="/wiki/Door" title="Door">doors</a>. These doors adjoined rooms in which <a href="/wiki/Robin_Evans" title="Robin Evans">Robin Evans</a> describes as a "matrix of discrete but thoroughly interconnected chambers."<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-11">[11]</a></sup> The layout allowed occupants to freely walk room to room from one door to another, thus breaking the boundaries of <a href="/wiki/Privacy" title="Privacy">privacy</a>. </p> 
      <dl>
       <dd>
        "Once inside it is necessary to pass from one room to the next, then to the next to traverse the building. Where passages and staircases are used, as inevitably they are, they nearly always connect just one space to another and never serve as general distributors of movement. Thus, despite the precise architectural containment offered by the addition of room upon room, the villa was, in terms of occupation, an open plan, relatively permeable to the numerous members of the household."
        <sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-11">[11]</a></sup>
       </dd>
      </dl> 
      <p>Although very public, the open <a href="/wiki/House_plan" title="House plan">plan</a> encouraged sociality and connectivity for all inhabitants.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Corridor">Corridor</span></h4> 
      <p>An early example of the segregation of rooms and consequent enhancement of privacy may be found in 1597 at the Beaufort House built in <a href="/wiki/Chelsea,_London" title="Chelsea, London">Chelsea</a>. It was designed by English <a href="/wiki/Architect" title="Architect">architect</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Thorpe" title="John Thorpe">John Thorpe</a> who wrote on his plans, "A Long Entry through all".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The separation of the passageway from the room developed the function of the <a href="/wiki/Corridor_(architecture)" class="mw-redirect" title="Corridor (architecture)">corridor</a>. This new extension was revolutionary at the time, allowing the integration of one door per room, in which all universally connected to the same corridor. <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English</a> architect <a href="/wiki/Sir_Roger_Pratt" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Roger Pratt">Sir Roger Pratt</a> states "the common way in the middle through the whole length of the house, [avoids] the offices from one molesting the other by continual passing through them."<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Social_hierarchies" class="mw-redirect" title="Social hierarchies">Social hierarchies</a> within the 17th century were highly regarded, as architecture was able to epitomize the servants and the upper class. More privacy is offered to the <a href="/wiki/Occupant" class="mw-redirect" title="Occupant">occupant</a> as Pratt further claims, "the ordinary servants may never publicly appear in passing to and fro for their occasions there."<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-13">[13]</a></sup> This social divide between rich and poor favored the physical integration of the corridor into housing by the 19th century. </p>
      <p>Sociologist <a href="/wiki/Witold_Rybczynski" title="Witold Rybczynski">Witold Rybczynski</a> wrote, "the subdivision of the house into day and night uses, and into formal and informal areas, had begun."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Rooms were changed from public to private as single entryways forced notions of entering a room with a specific purpose.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Employment-free_house">Employment-free house</span></h4> 
      <p>Compared to the large scaled houses in England and the Renaissance, the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Golden_Age" title="Dutch Golden Age">17th Century Dutch</a> house was smaller, and was only inhabited by up to four to five members.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup> This was due to their embracing "self-reliance",<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup> in contrast to the dependence on servants, and a design for a lifestyle centered on the <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a>. It was important for the Dutch to separate work from domesticity, as the <a href="/wiki/Home" title="Home">home</a> became an escape and a place of <a href="/wiki/Comfort" title="Comfort">comfort</a>. This way of living and the home has been noted as highly similar to the <a href="/wiki/Contemporary" class="mw-redirect" title="Contemporary">contemporary</a> family and their dwellings. House layouts also incorporated the idea of the corridor as well as the importance of function and privacy. </p>
      <p>By the end of the 17th Century, the house layout was soon transformed to become employment-free, enforcing these ideas for the future. This came in favour for the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a>, gaining large-scale factory production and workers.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup> The house layout of the Dutch and its functions are still relevant today. The names of parts of a house often echo the names of parts of other buildings, but could typically include: </p>

      <p> In the American context, some professions, such as doctors, in the 19th and early 20th century typically operated out of the front room or parlor or had a two-room office on their property, which was detached from the house. By the mid 20th century and the increase in high-tech equipment saw a marked shift whereby the contemporary doctor typically works from an office complex or hospital.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> </p>
      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology_and_privacy">Technology and privacy</span></h4> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Kamena_kuca_u_Pokreveniku.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Kamena_kuca_u_Pokreveniku.jpg/220px-Kamena_kuca_u_Pokreveniku.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Kamena_kuca_u_Pokreveniku.jpg/330px-Kamena_kuca_u_Pokreveniku.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Kamena_kuca_u_Pokreveniku.jpg/440px-Kamena_kuca_u_Pokreveniku.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Kamena_kuca_u_Pokreveniku.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>Traditional stone house in Serbia
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>The introduction of <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> and electronic systems within the house has questioned the impressions of privacy as well as the segregation of work from <a href="/wiki/Home" title="Home">home</a>. Technological advances of <a href="/wiki/Surveillance" title="Surveillance">surveillance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">communications</a> allow insight of personal habits and private lives.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup> As a result, the "private becomes ever more public, [and] the desire for a protective home life increases, fuelled by the very media that undermine it" writes <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Hill_(architect)" title="Jonathan Hill (architect)">Hill</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup> Work also, has been altered due to the increase of communications. The "deluge of information",<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup> has expressed the efforts of work, conveniently gaining access inside the house. Although commuting is reduced, "the desire to separate working and living remains apparent."<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Hill_(architect)" title="Jonathan Hill (architect)">Jonathan Hill</a>'s book <i>Immature Architecture</i>, he identifies this new invasion of privacy as <i><a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">Electromagnetic</a> Weather</i>. Natural or man-made <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> remains concurrent inside or outside the house, yet the electromagnetic weather is able to generate within both positions.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2011)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> On the other hand, some architects have designed homes in which eating, working and living are brought together. </p>

      <p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, modern house-construction techniques include <a href="/wiki/Framing_(construction)" title="Framing (construction)">light-frame construction</a> (in areas with access to supplies of wood) and <a href="/wiki/Adobe" title="Adobe">adobe</a> or sometimes <a href="/wiki/Rammed_earth" title="Rammed earth">rammed-earth construction</a> (in arid regions with scarce wood-resources). Some areas use <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">brick</a> almost exclusively, and quarried <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">stone</a> has long provided walling. To some extent, aluminum and steel have displaced some traditional <a href="/wiki/Building_material" title="Building material">building materials</a>. Increasingly popular alternative construction materials include <a href="/wiki/Insulating_concrete_form" title="Insulating concrete form">insulating concrete forms</a> (foam forms filled with <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a>), structural insulated panels (foam panels faced with <a href="/wiki/Oriented_strand_board" title="Oriented strand board">oriented strand board</a> or fiber cement), and light-gauge steel framing and heavy-gauge steel framing. </p>
      <p>More generally, people often build houses out of the nearest available material, and often tradition or culture govern construction-materials, so whole towns, areas, counties or even states/countries may be built out of one main type of material. For example, a large fraction of American houses use wood, while most British and many European houses use stone or brick or mud. </p>

      <p>In the 1900s (decade), some house designers started using <a href="/wiki/Prefabrication" title="Prefabrication">prefabrication</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sears,_Roebuck_%26_Co." class="mw-redirect" title="Sears, Roebuck &amp; Co.">Sears, Roebuck &amp; Co.</a> first marketed their <a href="/wiki/Sears_Catalog_Home" title="Sears Catalog Home">Sears Catalog Homes</a> to the general public in 1908. Prefab techniques became popular after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. First small inside rooms framing, then later, whole walls were prefabricated and carried to the <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction site</a>. The original impetus was to use the <a href="/wiki/Labor_force" class="mw-redirect" title="Labor force">labor force</a> inside a shelter during inclement weather. More recently builders have begun to collaborate with structural engineers who use computers and <a href="/wiki/Finite_element_analysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Finite element analysis">finite element analysis</a> to design prefabricated <a href="/wiki/Steel_frame" title="Steel frame">steel-framed</a> homes with known resistance to high wind-loads and <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">seismic</a> forces. These newer products provide labor savings, more consistent quality, and possibly accelerated construction processes. </p>

      <p>In the developed world, <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">energy-conservation</a> has grown in importance in house-design. Housing produces a major proportion of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_emissions" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon emissions">carbon emissions</a> (studies have show that it is <a href="/wiki/Energy_efficiency_in_British_housing" title="Energy efficiency in British housing">30% of the total in the United Kingdom</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Development of a number of <a href="/wiki/Category:Low-energy_building" title="Category:Low-energy building">low-energy building</a> types and techniques continues. They include the <a href="/wiki/Zero-energy_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Zero-energy house">zero-energy house</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Passive_solar_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Passive solar house">passive solar house</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_buildings" class="mw-redirect" title="Autonomous buildings">autonomous buildings</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Superinsulation" title="Superinsulation">superinsulated</a> and houses built to the <i><a href="/wiki/Passive_house" title="Passive house">Passivhaus</a></i> standard. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earthquake_protection">Earthquake protection</span></h3> 
      <p>One tool of <a href="/wiki/Earthquake_engineering" title="Earthquake engineering">earthquake engineering</a> is <a href="/wiki/Base_isolation" class="mw-redirect" title="Base isolation">base isolation</a> which is increasingly used for <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquake</a> protection. <a href="/wiki/Base_isolation" class="mw-redirect" title="Base isolation">Base isolation</a> is a collection of structural elements of a <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">building</a> that should substantially <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/decouple" class="extiw" title="wikt:decouple">decouple</a> it from the shaking ground thus protecting the building's integrity<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and enhancing its <a href="/wiki/Seismic_performance" class="mw-redirect" title="Seismic performance">seismic performance</a>. This technology, which is a kind of seismic <a href="/wiki/Vibration_control" title="Vibration control">vibration control</a>, can be applied both to a newly designed building and to seismic upgrading of existing structures.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Normally, excavations are made around the building and the building is separated from the foundations. Steel or <a href="/wiki/Reinforced_concrete" title="Reinforced concrete">reinforced concrete</a> beams replace the connections to the foundations, while under these, the isolating pads, or <i>base isolators</i>, replace the material removed. While the <i><a href="/wiki/Base_isolation" class="mw-redirect" title="Base isolation">base isolation</a></i> tends to restrict transmission of the ground motion to the building, it also keeps the building positioned properly over the foundation. Careful attention to detail is required where the building interfaces with the ground, especially at entrances, stairways and ramps, to ensure sufficient <a href="/wiki/Kinematics" title="Kinematics">relative motion</a> of those structural elements. </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">Bamboo</a> is an earthquake-resistant material, and is very versatile because it comes from fast-grow plants. Adding that bamboos are common in Asia, bamboo-made houses are popular in some Asian countries. </p>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span></h3> 
      <p>New houses in the UK are not covered by the <a href="/wiki/Sale_of_Goods_Act" title="Sale of Goods Act">Sale of Goods Act</a>. When purchasing a new house the buyer has different legal protection than when buying other products. New houses in the UK are covered by a <a href="/wiki/National_House_Building_Council" title="National House Building Council">National House Building Council</a> guarantee. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identifying_houses">Identifying houses</span></h2> 
      <p>With the growth of dense settlement, humans designed ways of identifying houses and <a href="/wiki/Land_lot" title="Land lot">parcels</a> of land. Individual houses sometimes acquire <a href="/wiki/Proper_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Proper name">proper names</a>, and those names may acquire in their turn considerable emotional connotations. For example, the house of <i><a href="/wiki/Howards_End" title="Howards End">Howards End</a></i> or the castle of <i><a href="/wiki/Brideshead_Revisited" title="Brideshead Revisited">Brideshead Revisited</a></i>. A more systematic and general approach to identifying houses may use various methods of <a href="/wiki/House_numbering" title="House numbering">house numbering</a>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Houses_and_symbolism">Houses and symbolism</span></h2> 
      <p>Houses may express the circumstances or opinions of their builders or their inhabitants. Thus, a vast and elaborate house may serve as a sign of conspicuous wealth whereas a low-profile house built of recycled materials may indicate support of energy conservation. </p>
      <p>Houses of particular historical significance (former residences of the famous, for example, or even just very old houses) may gain a protected status in <a href="/wiki/Town_planning" class="mw-redirect" title="Town planning">town planning</a> as examples of built <a href="/wiki/Cultural_heritage" title="Cultural heritage">heritage</a> or of streetscape. <a href="/wiki/Commemorative_plaques" class="mw-redirect" title="Commemorative plaques">Commemorative plaques</a> may mark such structures. </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Home_ownership" class="mw-redirect" title="Home ownership">Home ownership</a> provides a common measure of <a href="/wiki/Prosperity" title="Prosperity">prosperity</a> in <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>. Contrast the importance of house-destruction, tent dwelling and house rebuilding in the wake of many <a href="/wiki/Natural_disaster" title="Natural disaster">natural disasters</a>. </p>
