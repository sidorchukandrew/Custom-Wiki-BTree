      <p>A <b>reverse takeover</b> or <b>reverse merger takeover</b> (reverse <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">IPO</a>) is the acquisition of a <a href="/wiki/Public_company" title="Public company">public company</a> by a <a href="/wiki/Private_company" class="mw-redirect" title="Private company">private company</a> so that the private company can bypass the lengthy and complex process of going public.<sup id="cite_ref-ReverseMergers_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReverseMergers-1">[1]</a></sup> The transaction typically requires reorganization of capitalization of the acquiring company.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Sometimes, conversely, the private company is bought by the public listed company through an asset swap and share issue.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Process">Process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reverse_takeover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In a reverse takeover, shareholders of the private company purchase control of the public <a href="/wiki/Shell_(corporation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Shell (corporation)">shell company</a> and then merge it with the private company. The publicly traded corporation is called a "shell" since all that exists of the original company is its organizational structure. The private company shareholders receive a substantial majority of the shares of the public company and control of its <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">board of directors</a>. The transaction can be accomplished within weeks. </p>
      <p>The transaction involves the private and shell company exchanging information on each other, negotiating the merger terms, and signing a share exchange agreement. At the closing, the shell company issues a substantial majority of its shares and board control to the shareholders of the private company. The private company's shareholders pay for the shell company by contributing their shares in the private company to the shell company that they now control. This share exchange and change of control completes the reverse takeover, transforming the formerly privately held company into a publicly held company. </p>
      <p>In the United States, if the shell is an <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">SEC</a>-registered company, the private company does not go through an expensive and time-consuming review with state and federal regulators because this process was completed beforehand with the public company. However, a comprehensive disclosure document containing audited financial statements and significant legal disclosures is required by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">Securities and Exchange Commission</a> for reporting issuers. The disclosure is filed on Form 8-K and is filed immediately upon completion of the reverse merger transaction. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">Benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reverse_takeover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The advantages of public trading status include the possibility of commanding a higher price for a later offering of the company's securities. Going public through a reverse takeover allows a privately held company to become publicly held at a lesser cost, and with less <a href="/wiki/Stock_dilution" title="Stock dilution">stock dilution</a> than through an <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> (IPO). While the process of going public and raising capital is combined in an IPO, in a reverse takeover, these two functions are separate. A company can go public without raising additional capital. Separating these two functions greatly simplifies the process. </p>
      <p>In addition, a reverse takeover is less susceptible to <a href="/wiki/Financial_markets" class="mw-redirect" title="Financial markets">market</a> conditions. Conventional IPOs are risky for companies to undertake because the deal relies on market conditions, over which senior management has little control. If the market is off, the underwriter may pull the offering. The market also does not need to plunge wholesale. If a company in registration participates in an industry that's making unfavorable headlines, investors may shy away from the deal. In a reverse takeover, since the deal rests solely between those controlling the public and private companies, market conditions have little bearing on the situation. </p>
      <p>The process for a conventional IPO can last for a year or more. When a company transitions from an entrepreneurial venture to a public company fit for outside ownership, how time is spent by strategic managers can be beneficial or detrimental. Time spent in meetings and drafting sessions related to an IPO can have a disastrous effect on the growth upon which the offering is predicated, and may even nullify it. In addition, during the many months it takes to put an IPO together, market conditions can deteriorate, making the completion of an IPO unfavorable. By contrast, a reverse takeover can be completed in as little as thirty days. </p>
      <p>A 2013 study by Charles Lee of <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> found that: "Chinese reverse mergers performed much better than their reputation" and had performed better than other similar sized publicly traded companies in the same industrial sector.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Drawbacks">Drawbacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reverse_takeover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Drawbacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Reverse takeovers always come with some history and some shareholders. Sometimes this history can be bad and manifest itself in the form of currently sloppy records, pending lawsuits and other unforeseen liabilities. Additionally, these shells may sometimes come with angry or deceitful shareholders who are anxious to "dump" their stock at the first chance they get. </p>
      <p>One way the acquiring or surviving company can safeguard against the "dump" after the takeover is consummated is by requiring a lockup on the shares owned by the group from which they are purchasing the public shell. Other shareholders that have held stock as investors in the company being acquired pose no threat in a dump scenario because the number of shares they hold is not significant. </p>
      <p>On June 9, 2011, the United States <a href="/wiki/Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Securities and Exchange Commission">Securities and Exchange Commission</a> issued an investor bulletin cautioning investors about investing in reverse mergers, stating that they may be prone to fraud and other abuses.<sup id="cite_ref-ReverseMergers_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReverseMergers-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Reverse mergers may have other drawbacks. Private-company CEOs may be naive and inexperienced in the world of publicly traded companies unless they have past experience as an officer or director of a public company. In addition, reverse merger transactions only introduce liquidity to a previously private stock if there is bona fide public interest in the company. A comprehensive investor relations and investor marketing program may be an indirect cost of a reverse merger. <sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_financing">Future financing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reverse_takeover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Future financing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The greater number of financing options available to publicly held companies is a primary reason to undergo a reverse takeover. These financing options include: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>The issuance of additional stock in a secondary offering.</li> 
       <li>An exercise of warrants, where stockholders have the right to purchase additional shares in a company at predetermined prices. When many shareholders with warrants exercise their option to purchase additional shares, the company receives an infusion of capital.</li> 
       <li>Other investors are more likely to invest in a company via a private offering of stock when a mechanism to sell their stock is in place should the company be successful.</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>In addition, the now-publicly held company obtains the benefits of public trading of its securities: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Increased liquidity of company stock.</li> 
       <li>Possible higher company <a href="/wiki/Valuation_(finance)" title="Valuation (finance)">valuation.</a></li> 
       <li>Greater access to capital markets.</li> 
       <li>Ability to acquire other companies through stock transactions.</li> 
       <li>Ability to use stock incentive plans to attract and retain employees.</li>
      </ul> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reverse_takeover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In all of these cases—except for that of <a href="/wiki/US_Airways" title="US Airways">US Airways</a> and <a href="/wiki/America_West_Airlines" title="America West Airlines">America West Airlines</a>—shareholders of the acquiree controlled the resulting entity. With US Airways and America West Airlines, US Airways creditors (not shareholders) were left with control. </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>The corporate shell of REO Motor Car Company (whose sole asset was a tax loss carryover), in what amounted to a reverse "hostile" takeover, was forced by dissident shareholders to acquire a small publicly traded company, Nuclear Consultants. Eventually this company became the modern-day <a href="/wiki/Nucor" title="Nucor">Nucor</a>.</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/ValuJet_Airlines" title="ValuJet Airlines">ValuJet Airlines</a> was acquired by AirWays Corp. to form <a href="/wiki/AirTran_Holdings" title="AirTran Holdings">AirTran Holdings</a>, with the goal of shedding the tarnished reputation of the former.</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9rospatiale" title="Aérospatiale">Aérospatiale</a> was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Matra" title="Matra">Matra</a> to form <a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9rospatiale-Matra" title="Aérospatiale-Matra">Aérospatiale-Matra</a>, with the goal of taking the former, a state-owned company, public.</li> 
       <li>The game company <a href="/wiki/Atari" title="Atari">Atari</a> was acquired by <a href="/wiki/JT_Storage" title="JT Storage">JT Storage</a>, as marriage of convenience.<sup id="cite_ref-atarijts_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atarijts-7">[7]</a></sup></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/US_Airways" title="US Airways">US Airways</a> was acquired by <a href="/wiki/America_West_Airlines" title="America West Airlines">America West Airlines</a>, with the goal of removing the former from <a href="/wiki/Chapter_11_bankruptcy" class="mw-redirect" title="Chapter 11 bankruptcy">Chapter 11 bankruptcy</a>.</li> 
       <li>The <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> was acquired by Archipelago Holdings to form <a href="/wiki/NYSE_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="NYSE Group">NYSE Group</a>, with the goal of taking the former, a <a href="/wiki/Mutual_company" class="mw-redirect" title="Mutual company">mutual company</a>, public.</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Citadel_Media" class="mw-redirect" title="Citadel Media">ABC Radio</a> was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Citadel_Broadcasting_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Citadel Broadcasting Corporation">Citadel Broadcasting Corporation</a>, with the goal of spinning the former off from its parent, <a href="/wiki/Disney" class="mw-redirect" title="Disney">Disney</a>.</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/CBS_Radio" title="CBS Radio">CBS Radio</a> was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Entercom" title="Entercom">Entercom</a>, with the goal of spinning the former off from its parent, <a href="/wiki/CBS_Corporation" title="CBS Corporation">CBS Corporation</a></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Frederick%27s_of_Hollywood" title="Frederick's of Hollywood">Frederick's of Hollywood</a> parent FOH Holdings was acquired by apparel maker <a href="/wiki/Movie_Star" title="Movie Star">Movie Star</a> in order to take the larger lingerie maker public.<sup id="cite_ref-reuters_20061219_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters_20061219-8">[8]</a></sup></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Eddie_Stobart" title="Eddie Stobart">Eddie Stobart</a> in a reverse takeover with <a href="/wiki/Westbury_Property_Fund" class="mw-redirect" title="Westbury Property Fund">Westbury Property Fund</a> allowing transport by ship, road, rail, or boat to and within the UK, using only one company.</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Clearwire" title="Clearwire">Clearwire</a> acquired <a href="/wiki/Sprint_Corporation" title="Sprint Corporation">Sprint</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Xohm" title="Xohm">Xohm</a> division, taking the former company's name and with Sprint holding a controlling stake, leaving the resulting company publicly traded.</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/T-Mobile_US" title="T-Mobile US">T-Mobile US</a> which was called T-Mobile USA, Inc. at the time acquired <a href="/wiki/MetroPCS" class="mw-redirect" title="MetroPCS">MetroPCS</a> and after the merger was completed changed the company name to T-Mobile US and began trading on the New York Stock Exchange as TMUS.</li> 
       <li>When the <a href="/wiki/Holland_America_Line" title="Holland America Line">Holland America Line</a> (HAL) was sold to <a href="/wiki/Carnival_Corporation_%26_plc" title="Carnival Corporation &amp; plc">Carnival Corporation &amp; plc</a> in 1989, the former owners (the Van der Vorm family) put the proceeds in an investment company (HAL Investments), using the <a href="/wiki/Cruise_line" title="Cruise line">cruise line</a>'s former Dutch listing to go public.</li>
      </ul>
