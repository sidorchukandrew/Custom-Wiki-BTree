      <p><b>Henry VII</b> (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Harri Tudur</i>; 28 January 1457 – 21 April 1509) was the <a href="/wiki/King_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="King of England">King of England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord of Ireland">Lord of Ireland</a> from his seizure of the crown on 22 August 1485 to his death. He was the first monarch of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Tudor" title="House of Tudor">House of Tudor</a>. </p>
      <p>Henry attained the throne when his forces defeated King <a href="/wiki/Richard_III" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard III">Richard III</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bosworth_Field" title="Battle of Bosworth Field">Battle of Bosworth Field</a>, the culmination of the <a href="/wiki/Wars_of_the_Roses" title="Wars of the Roses">Wars of the Roses</a>. He was the last king of England to win his throne on the field of battle. He cemented his claim by marrying <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_of_York" title="Elizabeth of York">Elizabeth of York</a>, daughter of Richard's brother <a href="/wiki/Edward_IV" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward IV">Edward IV</a>. Henry was successful in restoring the power and stability of the English monarchy after the civil war. </p>
      <p>Henry is credited with a number of administrative, economic and diplomatic initiatives. His <a href="#Trade_agreements">supportive policy</a> toward <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>'s wool industry and his standoff with the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a> had long-lasting benefit to the whole English economy. He paid very close attention to detail, and instead of spending lavishly he concentrated on raising new revenues. New taxes stabilised the government's finances, although a commission after his death found widespread abuses in the tax collection process. After a reign of nearly 24 years, he was peacefully succeeded by his son, <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry VIII">Henry VIII</a>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancestry_and_early_life">Ancestry and early life</span></h2> 
      <p>Henry VII was born at <a href="/wiki/Pembroke_Castle" title="Pembroke Castle">Pembroke Castle</a> on 28 January 1457 to <a href="/wiki/Lady_Margaret_Beaufort" title="Lady Margaret Beaufort">Margaret Beaufort, Countess of Richmond</a>. His father, <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Tudor,_1st_Earl_of_Richmond" title="Edmund Tudor, 1st Earl of Richmond">Edmund Tudor, 1st Earl of Richmond</a>, died three months before his birth.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Henry's paternal grandfather, <a href="/wiki/Owen_Tudor" title="Owen Tudor">Owen Tudor</a>, originally from the <a href="/wiki/Tudors_of_Penmynydd" title="Tudors of Penmynydd">Tudors of Penmynydd</a>, Isle of <a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">Anglesey</a> in Wales, had been a page in the court of <a href="/wiki/Henry_V_of_England" title="Henry V of England">Henry V</a>. He rose to become one of the "Squires to the Body to the King" after military service at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Agincourt" title="Battle of Agincourt">Battle of Agincourt</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Owen is said to have secretly married the widow of Henry V, <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Valois" title="Catherine of Valois">Catherine of Valois</a>. One of their sons was Edmund Tudor, father of Henry VII. Edmund was created <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Richmond" title="Earl of Richmond">Earl of Richmond</a> in 1452, and "formally declared legitimate by Parliament".<sup id="cite_ref-Williams17_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams17-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Henry's main claim to the English throne derived from his mother through the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Beaufort" title="House of Beaufort">House of Beaufort</a>. Henry's mother, Lady Margaret Beaufort, was a great-granddaughter of <a href="/wiki/John_of_Gaunt,_1st_Duke_of_Lancaster" class="mw-redirect" title="John of Gaunt, 1st Duke of Lancaster">John of Gaunt</a>, Duke of Lancaster, fourth son of <a href="/wiki/Edward_III_of_England" title="Edward III of England">Edward III</a>, and his third wife <a href="/wiki/Katherine_Swynford" title="Katherine Swynford">Katherine Swynford</a>. Katherine was Gaunt's mistress for about 25 years. When they married in 1396 they already had four children, including Henry's great-grandfather <a href="/wiki/John_Beaufort,_1st_Earl_of_Somerset" title="John Beaufort, 1st Earl of Somerset">John Beaufort</a>. Thus, Henry's claim was somewhat tenuous; it was from a woman, and by illegitimate descent. In theory, the Portuguese and Castilian royal families had a better claim as descendants of <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Lancaster" title="Catherine of Lancaster">Catherine of Lancaster</a>, the daughter of John of Gaunt and his second wife <a href="/wiki/Constance_of_Castile,_Duchess_of_Lancaster" title="Constance of Castile, Duchess of Lancaster">Constance of Castile</a>. </p>
      <p>Gaunt's nephew <a href="/wiki/Richard_II_of_England" title="Richard II of England">Richard II</a> legitimised Gaunt's children by Katherine Swynford by <a href="/wiki/Letters_Patent" class="mw-redirect" title="Letters Patent">Letters Patent</a> in 1397. In 1407, <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_England" title="Henry IV of England">Henry IV</a>, Gaunt's son by his first wife, issued new Letters Patent confirming the legitimacy of his half-siblings, but also declaring them ineligible for the throne.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Henry IV's action was of doubtful legality, as the Beauforts were previously legitimised by an <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act of Parliament</a>, but it further weakened Henry's claim. </p>
      <p>Nonetheless, by 1483 Henry was the senior male <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lancaster" title="House of Lancaster">Lancastrian</a> claimant remaining after the deaths in battle, by murder or execution of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VI_of_England" title="Henry VI of England">Henry VI</a>, his son <a href="/wiki/Edward_of_Westminster,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Edward of Westminster, Prince of Wales">Edward of Westminster, Prince of Wales</a>, and the other Beaufort line of descent through Lady Margaret's uncle, the <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Beaufort,_2nd_Duke_of_Somerset" title="Edmund Beaufort, 2nd Duke of Somerset">2nd Duke of Somerset</a>. </p>
      <p>Henry also made some political capital out of his Welsh ancestry in attracting military support and safeguarding his army's passage through Wales on its way to the Battle of Bosworth.<sup id="cite_ref-Chrimes,_S.B._3_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chrimes,_S.B._3-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He came from an old, established Anglesey family that claimed descent from <a href="/wiki/Cadwaladr" title="Cadwaladr">Cadwaladr</a>, in legend, the last ancient British king,<sup id="cite_ref-Mackie,_J.D._47_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mackie,_J.D._47-7">[7]</a></sup> and on occasion Henry displayed the red dragon of Cadwaladr.<sup id="cite_ref-Chrimes,_S.B._3_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chrimes,_S.B._3-5">[5]</a></sup> He took it, as well as the standard of <a href="/wiki/St_George%27s_Cross" class="mw-redirect" title="St George's Cross">St George</a>, on his procession through London after the victory at Bosworth.<sup id="cite_ref-Mackie,_J.D._54_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mackie,_J.D._54-8">[8]</a></sup> A contemporary writer and Henry's biographer, <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Andr%C3%A9" title="Bernard André">Bernard André</a>, also made much of Henry's Welsh descent.<sup id="cite_ref-Mackie,_J.D._47_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mackie,_J.D._47-7">[7]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In reality, his hereditary connections to Welsh aristocracy were not strong. He was descended by the paternal line, through several generations, from <a href="/wiki/Ednyfed_Fychan" title="Ednyfed Fychan">Ednyfed Fychan</a>, the seneschal (steward) of Gwynedd and through this seneschal's wife from <a href="/wiki/Rhys_ap_Tewdwr" title="Rhys ap Tewdwr">Rhys ap Tewdwr</a>, the King of <a href="/wiki/Deheubarth" title="Deheubarth">Deheubarth</a> in South Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> His more immediate ancestor, Tudur ap Goronwy, had aristocratic land rights, but his sons, who were first cousins to <a href="/wiki/Owain_Glynd%C5%B5r" title="Owain Glyndŵr">Owain Glyndŵr</a>, sided with Owain in his revolt. One son was executed and the family land was forfeited. Another son, Henry's great-grandfather, became a butler to the Bishop of Bangor.<sup id="cite_ref-Mackie,_J.D._54_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mackie,_J.D._54-8">[8]</a></sup> Owen Tudor, the son of the butler, like the children of other rebels, was provided for by Henry V, a circumstance that precipitated his access to Queen Catherine of Valois.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Notwithstanding this lineage, to the bards of Wales, Henry was a candidate for <a href="/wiki/Y_Mab_Darogan" class="mw-redirect" title="Y Mab Darogan">Y Mab Darogan</a>, "The Son of Prophecy" who would free the Welsh from oppression. </p>

      <p>In 1456, Henry's father Edmund Tudor was captured while fighting for <a href="/wiki/Henry_VI_of_England" title="Henry VI of England">Henry VI</a> in South Wales against the <a href="/wiki/House_of_York" title="House of York">Yorkists</a>. He died in <a href="/wiki/Carmarthen_Castle" title="Carmarthen Castle">Carmarthen Castle</a>, three months before Henry was born. Henry's uncle <a href="/wiki/Jasper_Tudor" title="Jasper Tudor">Jasper Tudor</a>, the Earl of Pembroke and Edmund's younger brother, undertook to protect the young widow, who was 13 years old when she gave birth to Henry.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> When <a href="/wiki/Edward_IV_of_England" title="Edward IV of England">Edward IV</a> became King in 1461, Jasper Tudor went into exile abroad. Pembroke Castle, and later the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Pembroke" title="Earl of Pembroke">Earldom of Pembroke</a>, were granted to the Yorkist <a href="/wiki/William_Herbert,_1st_Earl_of_Pembroke_(1468_creation)" class="mw-redirect" title="William Herbert, 1st Earl of Pembroke (1468 creation)">William Herbert</a>, who also assumed the guardianship of Margaret Beaufort and the young Henry.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Henry lived in the Herbert household until 1469, when <a href="/wiki/Richard_Neville,_Earl_of_Warwick" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard Neville, Earl of Warwick">Richard Neville, Earl of Warwick</a> (the "Kingmaker"), went over to the Lancastrians. Herbert was captured fighting for the Yorkists and executed by Warwick.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams19_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams19-15">[15]</a></sup> When Warwick restored Henry VI in 1470, Jasper Tudor returned from exile and brought Henry to court.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams19_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams19-15">[15]</a></sup> When the Yorkist <a href="/wiki/Edward_IV_of_England" title="Edward IV of England">Edward IV</a> regained the throne in 1471, Henry fled with other Lancastrians to <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>, where he spent most of the next 14 years under the protection of <a href="/wiki/Francis_II,_Duke_of_Brittany" title="Francis II, Duke of Brittany">Francis II, Duke of Brittany</a>. In November 1476, Henry's protector fell ill and his principal advisers were more amenable to negotiating with the English king. Henry was handed over and escorted to the Breton port of Saint-Malo. While there, he feigned stomach cramps and in the confusion fled into a monastery. Following the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tewkesbury" title="Battle of Tewkesbury">Battle of Tewkesbury</a> in 1471, Edward IV prepared to order Henry's extraction and probable execution. The townspeople took exception to his behaviour and Francis recovered from his illness. Thus, a small band of scouts rescued Henry. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_to_the_throne">Rise to the throne</span></h2>

      <p>By 1483, Henry's mother was actively promoting him as an alternative to <a href="/wiki/Richard_III_of_England" title="Richard III of England">Richard III</a>, despite her being married to <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Stanley,_1st_Earl_of_Derby" title="Thomas Stanley, 1st Earl of Derby">Lord Stanley</a>, a Yorkist. At <a href="/wiki/Rennes_Cathedral" title="Rennes Cathedral">Rennes Cathedral</a> on Christmas Day 1483, Henry pledged to marry <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_of_York" title="Elizabeth of York">Elizabeth of York</a>, the eldest daughter of Edward IV, who was also Edward's heir since the presumed death of her brothers, the <a href="/wiki/Princes_in_the_Tower" title="Princes in the Tower">Princes in the Tower</a>, King <a href="/wiki/Edward_V_of_England" title="Edward V of England">Edward V</a> and his brother <a href="/wiki/Richard_of_Shrewsbury,_Duke_of_York" title="Richard of Shrewsbury, Duke of York">Richard of Shrewsbury, Duke of York</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> With money and supplies borrowed from his host, <a href="/wiki/Francis_II,_Duke_of_Brittany" title="Francis II, Duke of Brittany">Francis II, Duke of Brittany</a>, Henry tried to land in England, but his conspiracy unravelled resulting in the execution of his primary co-conspirator, the <a href="/wiki/Henry_Stafford,_2nd_Duke_of_Buckingham" title="Henry Stafford, 2nd Duke of Buckingham">Duke of Buckingham</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams25_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams25-17">[17]</a></sup> Now supported by Francis II's prime-minister, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Landais" title="Pierre Landais">Pierre Landais</a>, Richard III attempted to extradite Henry from Brittany, but Henry escaped to France.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> He was welcomed by the French, who readily supplied him with troops and equipment for a second invasion. </p>
      <p>Henry gained the support of the Woodvilles, in-laws of the late Edward IV, and sailed with a small French and Scottish force, landing at Mill Bay near <a href="/wiki/Dale,_Pembrokeshire" title="Dale, Pembrokeshire">Dale, Pembrokeshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> He marched toward England accompanied by his uncle Jasper and the <a href="/wiki/John_de_Vere,_13th_Earl_of_Oxford" title="John de Vere, 13th Earl of Oxford">Earl of Oxford</a>. Wales was traditionally a Lancastrian stronghold, and Henry owed the support he gathered to his Welsh birth and ancestry, being directly descended, through his father, from <a href="/wiki/Rhys_ap_Gruffydd" title="Rhys ap Gruffydd">Rhys ap Gruffydd</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> He amassed an army of about 5,000 soldiers.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Henry devised a plan to seize the throne by engaging Richard quickly because Richard had reinforcements in <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">Nottingham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leicester" title="Leicester">Leicester</a>. Though outnumbered, Henry's Lancastrian forces decisively defeated Richard's Yorkist army at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bosworth_Field" title="Battle of Bosworth Field">Battle of Bosworth Field</a> on 22 August 1485. Several of Richard's key allies, such as the <a href="/wiki/Henry_Percy,_4th_Earl_of_Northumberland" title="Henry Percy, 4th Earl of Northumberland">Earl of Northumberland</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Stanley_(Battle_of_Bosworth)" title="William Stanley (Battle of Bosworth)">William</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Stanley,_1st_Earl_of_Derby" title="Thomas Stanley, 1st Earl of Derby">Thomas</a> Stanley, crucially switched sides or left the battlefield. Richard III's death at Bosworth Field effectively ended the Wars of the Roses. </p>

      <p>As king, Henry was styled <i>by the Grace of God, King of England and France and Lord of Ireland</i>. On his succession, Henry became entitled to bear the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Arms_of_England" title="Royal Arms of England">Royal Arms of England</a>. After his marriage, Henry used as his emblem the red and white rose, which became known as the <a href="/wiki/Tudor_rose" title="Tudor rose">Tudor rose</a>. </p>
      <p>To secure his hold on the throne, Henry declared himself king by <a href="/wiki/Right_of_conquest" title="Right of conquest">right of conquest</a> retroactively from 21 August 1485, the day before Bosworth Field.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Thus, anyone who had fought for Richard against him would be guilty of <a href="/wiki/Treason_Act_1495" title="Treason Act 1495">treason</a> and Henry could legally confiscate the lands and property of Richard III, while restoring his own. Henry spared Richard's nephew and designated heir, the <a href="/wiki/John_de_la_Pole,_Earl_of_Lincoln" class="mw-redirect" title="John de la Pole, Earl of Lincoln">Earl of Lincoln</a>, and made <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Pole,_Countess_of_Salisbury" title="Margaret Pole, Countess of Salisbury">Margaret Plantagenet</a>, a Yorkist heiress, <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Pole,_Countess_of_Salisbury" title="Margaret Pole, Countess of Salisbury">Countess of Salisbury</a> <a href="/wiki/Sui_iuris" title="Sui iuris">sui juris</a>. He took care not to address the <a href="/wiki/Baronage" title="Baronage">baronage</a> or summon <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">Parliament</a> until after his coronation, which took place in <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a> on 30 October 1485.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> After his coronation Henry issued an edict that any gentleman who swore fealty to him would, notwithstanding any previous attainder, be secure in his property and person. </p>
      <p>Henry honoured his pledge of December 1483 to marry Elizabeth of York.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams25_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams25-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> They were third cousins, as both were great-great-grandchildren of John of Gaunt.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Henry and Elizabeth were married on 18 January 1486 at Westminster Abbey. The marriage unified the warring houses and gave his children a strong claim to the throne. The unification of the houses of York and Lancaster by this marriage is symbolised by the heraldic emblem of the <a href="/wiki/Tudor_rose" title="Tudor rose">Tudor rose</a>, a combination of the white rose of York and the red rose of Lancaster. It also ended future discussion as to whether the descendants of the fourth son of Edward III, <a href="/wiki/Edmund_of_Langley,_1st_Duke_of_York" title="Edmund of Langley, 1st Duke of York">Edmund, Duke of York</a>, through marriage to Philippa, heiress of the second son, <a href="/wiki/Lionel_of_Antwerp,_1st_Duke_of_Clarence" title="Lionel of Antwerp, 1st Duke of Clarence">Lionel, Duke of Clarence</a>, had a superior or inferior claim to those of the third son <a href="/wiki/John_of_Gaunt,_1st_Duke_of_Lancaster" class="mw-redirect" title="John of Gaunt, 1st Duke of Lancaster">John of Gaunt</a>, who had held the throne for three generations. In addition, Henry had Parliament repeal <i><a href="/wiki/Titulus_Regius" title="Titulus Regius">Titulus Regius</a></i>, the statute that declared Edward IV's marriage invalid and his children illegitimate, thus legitimising his wife. Amateur historians <a href="/wiki/Bertram_Fields" class="mw-redirect" title="Bertram Fields">Bertram Fields</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sir_Clements_Markham" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Clements Markham">Sir Clements Markham</a> have claimed that he may have been involved in the murder of the Princes in the Tower, as the repeal of <i>Titulus Regius</i> gave the Princes a stronger claim to the throne than his own. <a href="/wiki/Alison_Weir" title="Alison Weir">Alison Weir</a>, however, points out that the Rennes ceremony, two years earlier, was possible only if Henry and his supporters were certain that the Princes were already dead.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Henry secured his crown principally by dividing and undermining the power of the nobility, especially through the aggressive use of bonds and recognisances to secure loyalty. He also enacted laws against <a href="/wiki/Bastard_feudalism" title="Bastard feudalism">livery and maintenance</a>, the great lords' practice of having large numbers of "retainers" who wore their lord's badge or uniform and formed a potential private army. </p>
      <p>While he was still in Leicester, after the battle of Bosworth Field, Henry was already taking precautions to prevent any rebellions against his reign. Before leaving Leicester to go to London, Henry dispatched <a href="/wiki/Robert_Willoughby,_1st_Baron_Willoughby_de_Broke" title="Robert Willoughby, 1st Baron Willoughby de Broke">Robert Willoughby</a> to Sheriff Hutton in Yorkshire, to have the ten-year-old <a href="/wiki/Edward,_Earl_of_Warwick" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward, Earl of Warwick">Edward, Earl of Warwick</a> arrested and taken to the Tower of London.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Edward was the son of George, Duke of Clarence, and as such he presented a threat as a potential rival to the new King Henry VII for the throne of England. However, Henry was threatened by several active rebellions over the next few years. The first was the rebellion of the <a href="/wiki/Stafford_and_Lovell_Rebellion" class="mw-redirect" title="Stafford and Lovell Rebellion">Stafford brothers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Lovell,_1st_Viscount_Lovell" title="Francis Lovell, 1st Viscount Lovell">Viscount Lovell</a> of 1486, which collapsed without fighting.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1487, Yorkists led by Lincoln rebelled in support of <a href="/wiki/Lambert_Simnel" title="Lambert Simnel">Lambert Simnel</a>, a boy who was claimed to be the <a href="/wiki/Edward_Plantagenet,_17th_Earl_of_Warwick" title="Edward Plantagenet, 17th Earl of Warwick">Earl of Warwick</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> son of Edward IV's brother <a href="/wiki/George_Plantagenet,_1st_Duke_of_Clarence" title="George Plantagenet, 1st Duke of Clarence">Clarence</a> (who had last been seen as a prisoner in the <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_London" title="Tower of London">Tower</a>). The rebellion began in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, where the traditionally Yorkist nobility, headed by the powerful <a href="/wiki/Gerald_FitzGerald,_8th_Earl_of_Kildare" title="Gerald FitzGerald, 8th Earl of Kildare">Gerald FitzGerald, 8th Earl of Kildare</a>, proclaimed Simnel king and provided troops for his invasion of England. The rebellion was defeated and Lincoln killed at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Stoke" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Stoke">Battle of Stoke</a>. Henry showed remarkable clemency to the surviving rebels: he pardoned Kildare and the other Irish nobles, and he made the boy, Simnel, a servant in the royal kitchen.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1490, a young <a href="/wiki/Flemish_people" title="Flemish people">Fleming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perkin_Warbeck" title="Perkin Warbeck">Perkin Warbeck</a>, appeared and claimed to be <a href="/wiki/Richard_of_Shrewsbury,_1st_Duke_of_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard of Shrewsbury, 1st Duke of York">Richard</a>, the younger of the "Princes in the Tower". Warbeck won the support of Edward IV's sister <a href="/wiki/Margaret_of_York" title="Margaret of York">Margaret of Burgundy</a>. He led attempted invasions of Ireland in 1491 and England in 1495, and persuaded <a href="/wiki/James_IV_of_Scotland" title="James IV of Scotland">James IV of Scotland</a> to invade England in 1496. In 1497 Warbeck landed in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> with a few thousand troops, but was soon captured and executed.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1499, Henry had the Earl of Warwick executed. However, he spared Warwick's elder sister <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Pole,_8th_Countess_of_Salisbury" class="mw-redirect" title="Margaret Pole, 8th Countess of Salisbury">Margaret</a>. She survived until 1541, when she was executed by Henry VIII. </p>
      <p>Henry married Elizabeth of York with the hope of uniting the Yorkist and Lancastrian sides of the Plantagenet dynastic disputes, and he was largely successful. However, such a level of paranoia persisted that anyone (John de la Pole, Earl of Lincoln,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> for example) with blood ties to the Plantagenets was suspected of coveting the throne.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">

      <p>For most of Henry VII's reign <a href="/wiki/Edward_Story" title="Edward Story">Edward Story</a> was <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Chichester" title="Bishop of Chichester">Bishop of Chichester</a>. Story's register still exists and, according to the 19th-century historian W.R.W. Stephens, "affords some illustrations of the avaricious and parsimonious character of the king". It seems that the king was skillful at extracting money from his subjects on many pretexts, including that of war with France or war with Scotland. The money so extracted added to the king's personal fortune rather than being used for the stated purpose.<sup id="cite_ref-stephens176_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stephens176-35">[35]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Unlike his predecessors, Henry VII came to the throne without personal experience in estate management or financial administration.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> But during his reign he became a fiscally prudent monarch who restored the fortunes of an effectively bankrupt <a href="/wiki/Exchequer" title="Exchequer">exchequer</a>. Henry VII introduced stability to the financial administration of England by keeping the same financial advisors throughout his reign. For instance, except for the first few months of the reign, <a href="/wiki/John_Dynham,_1st_Baron_Dynham" title="John Dynham, 1st Baron Dynham">Lord Dynham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Howard,_2nd_Duke_of_Norfolk" title="Thomas Howard, 2nd Duke of Norfolk">Thomas Howard, 2nd Duke of Norfolk</a> were the only two office holders in the position of <a href="/wiki/Lord_High_Treasurer" title="Lord High Treasurer">Lord High Treasurer</a> of England throughout his reign.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Henry VII improved tax collection within the realm by introducing ruthlessly efficient mechanisms of taxation. He was supported in this effort by his chancellor, Archbishop <a href="/wiki/John_Morton_(archbishop)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Morton (archbishop)">John Morton</a>, whose "<a href="/wiki/Morton%27s_Fork" class="mw-redirect" title="Morton's Fork">Morton's Fork</a>" was a <a href="/wiki/Catch-22_(logic)" title="Catch-22 (logic)">catch-22</a> method of ensuring that nobles paid increased taxes: those nobles who spent little must have saved much, and thus they could afford the increased taxes; on the other hand, those nobles who spent much obviously had the means to pay the increased taxes.<sup id="cite_ref-B._Chrimes,_p._203_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B._Chrimes,_p._203-38">[38]</a></sup> Royal government was also reformed with the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="King's Council">King's Council</a> that kept the nobility in check. </p>
      <p>The capriciousness and lack of due process that indebted many would tarnish his legacy and were soon ended upon Henry VII's death, after a commission revealed widespread abuses.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> According to the contemporary historian <a href="/wiki/Polydore_Vergil" title="Polydore Vergil">Polydore Vergil</a>, simple "greed" underscored the means by which royal control was over-asserted in Henry's final years.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Henry VIII executed <a href="/wiki/Richard_Empson" title="Richard Empson">Richard Empson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Dudley" title="Edmund Dudley">Edmund Dudley</a>, his two most hated tax collectors, on trumped-up charges of treason.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>He established the <a href="/wiki/Pound_(mass)" title="Pound (mass)">pound</a> <a href="/wiki/Avoirdupois" class="mw-redirect" title="Avoirdupois">avoirdupois</a> as a standard of weight; it later became part of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_units" title="Imperial units">Imperial</a><sup id="cite_ref-sizes_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sizes-42">[42]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_customary_units" title="United States customary units">customary</a> systems of units.<sup id="cite_ref-sizes_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sizes-42">[42]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_policy">Foreign policy</span></h3> 
      <p>Henry VII's policy was both to maintain peace and to create economic prosperity. Up to a point, he succeeded. He was not a military man and had no interest in trying to regain French territories lost during the reigns of his predecessors; he was therefore ready to conclude a treaty with France at Etaples that brought money into the coffers of England, and ensured the French would not support pretenders to the English throne, such as Perkin Warbeck. However, this treaty came at a slight price, as Henry mounted a minor invasion of Brittany in November 1492. Henry decided to keep Brittany out of French hands, signed an alliance with Spain to that end, and sent 6,000 troops to France.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMackie195297_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMackie195297-43">[43]</a></sup> The confused, fractious nature of Breton politics undermined his efforts, which finally failed after three sizeable expeditions, at a cost of £24,000. However, as France was becoming more concerned with the Italian Wars, the French were happy to agree to the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Etaples" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Etaples">Treaty of Etaples</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Henry had pressured the French by <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Boulogne_(1492)" title="Siege of Boulogne (1492)">laying siege to Boulogne</a> in October 1492. </p> 
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:EmpsonHenryDudley.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/EmpsonHenryDudley.jpg/170px-EmpsonHenryDudley.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="224" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/EmpsonHenryDudley.jpg/255px-EmpsonHenryDudley.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/EmpsonHenryDudley.jpg/340px-EmpsonHenryDudley.jpg 2x" data-file-width="455" data-file-height="600"></a>

      <p>Henry had been under the financial and physical protection of the French throne or its vassals for most of his life, before he became king. To strengthen his position, however, he subsidised shipbuilding, so strengthening the <a href="/wiki/The_Tudors_and_the_Royal_Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="The Tudors and the Royal Navy">navy</a> (he commissioned Europe's first ever – and the world's oldest surviving – <a href="/wiki/Dry_dock" title="Dry dock">dry dock</a> at <a href="/wiki/HMNB_Portsmouth" title="HMNB Portsmouth">Portsmouth</a> in 1495) and improving trading opportunities. </p>
      <p>Henry VII was one of the first European monarchs to recognise the importance of the newly united Spanish kingdom; he concluded the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Medina_del_Campo_(1489)" title="Treaty of Medina del Campo (1489)">Treaty of Medina del Campo</a>, by which his son, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Tudor" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthur Tudor">Arthur Tudor</a>, was married to <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Aragon" title="Catherine of Aragon">Catherine of Aragon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWarnicke2000103_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWarnicke2000103-45">[45]</a></sup> He also concluded the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Perpetual_Peace" title="Treaty of Perpetual Peace">Treaty of Perpetual Peace</a> with Scotland (the first treaty between England and Scotland for almost two centuries), which betrothed his daughter Margaret to King James IV of Scotland. By this marriage, Henry VII hoped to break the <a href="/wiki/Auld_Alliance" title="Auld Alliance">Auld Alliance</a> between Scotland and France. Though this was not achieved during his reign, the marriage eventually led to the union of the English and Scottish crowns under Margaret's great-grandson, <a href="/wiki/James_VI_and_I" title="James VI and I">James VI and I</a>, following the death of Henry's granddaughter <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth I">Elizabeth I</a>. </p>
      <p>He also formed an alliance with Holy Roman Emperor <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Maximilian I, Holy Roman Emperor">Maximilian I</a> (1493–1519) and persuaded <a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_VIII" title="Pope Innocent VIII">Pope Innocent VIII</a> to issue a <a href="/wiki/Papal_bull" title="Papal bull">papal bull</a> of <a href="/wiki/Excommunication" title="Excommunication">excommunication</a> against all pretenders to Henry's throne. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trade_agreements">Trade agreements</span></h3> 
      <p>Henry VII was much enriched by trading <a href="/wiki/Alum" title="Alum">alum</a>, which was used in the wool and cloth trades as a <a href="/wiki/Chemical" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical">chemical</a> <a href="/wiki/Dye_fixative" class="mw-redirect" title="Dye fixative">fixative</a> for <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">dyeing</a> <a href="/wiki/Fabric" class="mw-redirect" title="Fabric">fabrics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Since alum was mined in only one area in Europe (Tolfa, Italy), it was a scarce commodity and therefore especially valuable to its land holder, the pope. With the English economy heavily invested in wool production, Henry VII became involved in the alum trade in 1486. With the assistance of the Italian merchant banker Lodovico della Fava and the Italian banker Girolamo Frescobaldi, Henry VII became deeply involved in the trade by licensing ships, obtaining alum from the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>, and selling it to the Low Countries and in England.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> This trade made an expensive commodity cheaper, which raised opposition from <a href="/wiki/Pope_Julius_II" title="Pope Julius II">Pope Julius II</a>, since the Tolfa mine was a part of papal territory and had given the Pope monopoly control over alum. </p>
      <p>Henry's most successful diplomatic achievement as regards the economy was the <i><a href="/wiki/Magnus_Intercursus" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnus Intercursus">Magnus Intercursus</a></i> ("great agreement") of 1496. In 1494, Henry embargoed trade (mainly in wool) with the <a href="/wiki/Burgundian_Netherlands" title="Burgundian Netherlands">Netherlands</a> in retaliation for Margaret of Burgundy's support for Perkin Warbeck. The <a href="/wiki/Company_of_Merchant_Adventurers_of_London" title="Company of Merchant Adventurers of London">Merchant Adventurers</a>, the company which enjoyed the monopoly of the Flemish wool trade, relocated from <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a> to <a href="/wiki/Calais" title="Calais">Calais</a>. At the same time, Flemish merchants were ejected from England. The stand-off eventually paid off for Henry. Both parties realised they were mutually disadvantaged by the reduction in commerce. Its restoration by the <i>Magnus Intercursus</i> was very much to England's benefit in removing taxation for English merchants and significantly increasing England's wealth. In turn, Antwerp became an extremely important trade <a href="/wiki/Entrep%C3%B4t" title="Entrepôt">entrepôt</a> (transshipment port), through which, for example, goods from the Baltic, spices from the east and Italian silks were exchanged for English cloth.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1506, Henry extorted the Treaty of Windsor from <a href="/wiki/Philip_I_of_Castile" title="Philip I of Castile">Philip the Handsome of Burgundy</a>. Philip had been shipwrecked on the English coast, and while Henry's guest, was bullied into an agreement so favourable to England at the expense of the Netherlands that it was dubbed the <i><a href="/wiki/Malus_Intercursus" title="Malus Intercursus">Malus Intercursus</a></i> ("evil agreement"). France, <a href="/wiki/Burgundian_Netherlands" title="Burgundian Netherlands">Burgundy</a>, the Holy Roman Empire, Spain and the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a> all rejected the treaty, which was never in force. Philip died shortly after the negotiations.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Law_enforcement_and_Justices_of_Peace">Law enforcement and Justices of Peace</span></h3> 
      <p>Henry's principal problem was to restore royal authority in a realm recovering from the Wars of the Roses. There were too many powerful noblemen and, as a consequence of the system of so-called <a href="/wiki/Bastard_feudalism" title="Bastard feudalism">bastard feudalism</a>, each had what amounted to private armies of indentured retainers (<a href="/wiki/Mercenary" title="Mercenary">mercenaries</a> masquerading as servants). </p> 
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:King_Henry_VII_from_NPG.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/King_Henry_VII_from_NPG.jpg/220px-King_Henry_VII_from_NPG.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="287" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/King_Henry_VII_from_NPG.jpg/330px-King_Henry_VII_from_NPG.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/King_Henry_VII_from_NPG.jpg/440px-King_Henry_VII_from_NPG.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2400" data-file-height="3132"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:King_Henry_VII_from_NPG.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>Late 16th-century copy of a portrait of Henry VII
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>He was content to allow the nobles their regional influence if they were loyal to him. For instance, the Stanley family had control of Lancashire and Cheshire, upholding the peace on the condition that they stayed within the law. In other cases, he brought his over-powerful subjects to heel by decree. He passed laws against "livery" (the upper classes' flaunting of their adherents by giving them badges and emblems) and "maintenance" (the keeping of too many male "servants"). These laws were used shrewdly in levying fines upon those that he perceived as threats. </p>
      <p>However, his principal weapon was the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Star_Chamber" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Star Chamber">Court of Star Chamber</a>. This revived an earlier practice of using a small (and trusted) group of the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Privy Council">Privy Council</a> as a personal or Prerogative Court, able to cut through the cumbersome legal system and act swiftly. Serious disputes involving the use of personal power, or threats to royal authority, were thus dealt with.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Henry VII used <a href="/wiki/Justices_of_the_Peace" class="mw-redirect" title="Justices of the Peace">Justices of the Peace</a> on a large, nationwide scale. They were appointed for every shire and served for a year at a time. Their chief task was to see that the laws of the country were obeyed in their area. Their powers and numbers steadily increased during the time of the Tudors, never more so than under Henry's reign.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Despite this, Henry was keen to constrain their power and influence, applying the same principles to the Justices of the Peace as he did to the nobility: a similar system of bonds and recognisances to that which applied to both the gentry and the nobles who tried to exert their elevated influence over these local officials. </p>
      <p>All Acts of Parliament were overseen by the Justices of the Peace. For example, Justices of the Peace could replace suspect jurors in accordance with the 1495 act preventing the corruption of juries. They were also in charge of various administrative duties, such as the checking of weights and measures. </p>
      <p>By 1509, Justices of the Peace were key enforcers of law and order for Henry VII. They were unpaid, which, in comparison with modern standards, meant a lesser tax bill to pay for a police force. Local gentry saw the office as one of local influence and prestige and were therefore willing to serve. Overall, this was a successful area of policy for Henry, both in terms of efficiency and as a method of reducing the corruption endemic within the nobility of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. </p>

      <p>In 1502, Henry VII's first son and heir apparent, <a href="/wiki/Arthur,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Arthur, Prince of Wales">Arthur, Prince of Wales</a>, died suddenly at <a href="/wiki/Ludlow_Castle" title="Ludlow Castle">Ludlow Castle</a>, very likely from a viral respiratory illness known at the time as the "<a href="/wiki/English_sweating_sickness" class="mw-redirect" title="English sweating sickness">English sweating sickness</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> This made Henry, Duke of York (<a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a>) heir apparent to the throne. The King, normally a reserved man who rarely showed much emotion in public unless angry, surprised his courtiers by his intense grief and sobbing at his son's death, while his concern for the Queen is evidence that the marriage was a happy one, as is his reaction to the Queen's death the following year, when he shut himself away for several days, refusing to speak to anyone.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Henry VII wanted to maintain the Spanish alliance. He therefore arranged a <a href="/wiki/Papal_dispensation" class="mw-redirect" title="Papal dispensation">papal dispensation</a> from <a href="/wiki/Pope_Julius_II" title="Pope Julius II">Pope Julius II</a> for Prince Henry to marry his brother's widow Catherine, a relationship that would have otherwise precluded marriage in the Roman Catholic Church. In 1503, Queen Elizabeth died in childbirth, so King Henry had the dispensation also permit him to marry Catherine himself. After obtaining the dispensation, Henry had second thoughts about the marriage of his son and Catherine. Catherine's mother <a href="/wiki/Isabella_I_of_Castile" title="Isabella I of Castile">Isabella I of Castile</a> had died and Catherine's sister <a href="/wiki/Joanna_of_Castile" title="Joanna of Castile">Joanna</a> had succeeded her; Catherine was therefore daughter of only one reigning monarch and so less desirable as a spouse for Henry VII's heir-apparent. The marriage did not take place during his lifetime. Otherwise, at the time of his father's arranging of the marriage to Catherine of Aragon, the future Henry VIII was too young to contract the marriage according to Canon Law, and would be ineligible until age fourteen.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Henry made half-hearted plans to remarry and beget more heirs, but these never came to anything. In 1505 he was sufficiently interested in a potential marriage to <a href="/wiki/Joan_of_Naples_(1478-1518)" class="mw-redirect" title="Joan of Naples (1478-1518)">Joan</a>, the recently widowed Queen of Naples, that he sent ambassadors to Naples to report on the 27-year-old's physical suitability.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The wedding never took place, and the physical description Henry sent with his ambassadors of what he desired in a new wife matched the description of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_of_York" title="Elizabeth of York">Elizabeth</a>. After 1503, records show the <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_London" title="Tower of London">Tower of London</a> was never again used as a royal residence by Henry Tudor, and all royal births under <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> took place in palaces. Henry VII was shattered by the loss of Elizabeth, and her death broke his heart.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> During his lifetime the nobility often jeered him for re-centralizing power in London, and later the 16th-century historian <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon" title="Francis Bacon">Francis Bacon</a> was ruthlessly critical of the methods by which he enforced tax law, but it is equally true that Henry Tudor was hellbent on keeping detailed records of his personal finances, down to the last halfpenny;<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> these and one account book detailing the expenses of his queen survive in the British National Archives. Until the death of his wife, the evidence is clear from these accounting books that Henry Tudor was a more doting father and husband than was widely known. Many of the entries show a man who loosened his purse strings generously for his wife and children, and not just on necessities: in spring 1491 he spent a great amount of gold on a lute for his daughter Mary; the following year he spent money on a lion for Elizabeth's menagerie. </p>
      <p>With Elizabeth's death, the possibilities for such family indulgences greatly diminished.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Immediately afterward, Henry became very sick and nearly died himself, allowing only <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Beaufort,_Countess_of_Richmond_and_Derby" class="mw-redirect" title="Margaret Beaufort, Countess of Richmond and Derby">Margaret Beaufort</a>, his mother, near him: "privily departed to a solitary place, and would that no man should resort unto him."<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Henry VII died of <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> at <a href="/wiki/Richmond_Palace" title="Richmond Palace">Richmond Palace</a> on 21 April 1509, and was buried at <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a>, next to his wife, Elizabeth, in the chapel he commissioned.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> He was succeeded by his second son, <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> (reigned 1509–47). His mother survived him, but died two months later on 29 June 1509. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance_and_character">Appearance and character</span></h2> 
      <p>Henry is the first English king of whose appearance good contemporary visual records in realistic portraits exist that are relatively free of idealization. At 27, he was tall and slender, with small blue eyes, which were said to have a noticeable animation of expression, and noticeably bad teeth in a long, sallow face beneath very fair hair. Amiable and high-spirited, Henry was friendly if dignified in manner, and it was clear to everyone that he was extremely intelligent. His biographer, Professor Chrimes, credits him – even before he had become king – with "a high degree of personal magnetism, ability to inspire confidence, and a growing reputation for shrewd decisiveness". On the debit side, he may have looked a little delicate as he suffered from poor health.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_and_memory">Legacy and memory</span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">

          <a href="/wiki/File:Henry_VII_and_Elizabeth_of_York_Common_Royal_Descent.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>

        </div>

      <p>Historians have always compared Henry VII with his continental contemporaries, especially Louis XI of France and Ferdinand II of Aragon. By 1600 historians emphasised Henry's wisdom in drawing lessons in statecraft from other monarchs. In 1622 <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon" title="Francis Bacon">Francis Bacon</a> published his <i><a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Reign_of_King_Henry_VII" title="History of the Reign of King Henry VII">History of the Reign of King Henry VII</a></i>. By 1900 the <a href="/wiki/New_Monarchs" title="New Monarchs">"New Monarchy"</a> interpretation stressed the common factors that in each country led to the revival of monarchical power. This approach raised puzzling questions about similarities and differences in the development of national states. In the late 20th century a model of European state formation was prominent in which Henry less resembles Louis and Ferdinand.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> </p>
