      <p>A <b><a href="/wiki/Matrilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Matrilineal">matrilineal</a> surname</b> or <b>matriname</b><sup id="cite_ref-sykes_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sykes-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[a]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vitales_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vitales-2">[2]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family name</a> inherited from one's mother, and maternal grandmother, etc. whose <a href="/wiki/Line_of_descent" class="mw-redirect" title="Line of descent">line of descent</a> is called a mother-line, or <b>matriline.</b> </p>
      <p>The term "matriname"was introduced by Prof. <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Sykes" title="Bryan Sykes">Bryan Sykes</a> in his book <a href="/wiki/The_Seven_Daughters_of_Eve" title="The Seven Daughters of Eve">The Seven Daughters of Eve</a>, stating that, quote, "we would then all have three names: a first name, a surname and a new one, a matriname perhaps."<sup id="cite_ref-sykes_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sykes-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Single_surname">Single surname</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matriname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Single surname">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Although the term "maternal surname" can be confused with "matriname", there is a difference in <a href="/wiki/Patrilineality" title="Patrilineality">patrilineal</a> cultures, where the <i>maternal</i> surname is the mother's patriname. In addition, in some cultures, women inherit a surname from their mother as well as from their father.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (December 2017)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup>In such patrilineal cultures, matrinames are able to co-exist with patrinames. </p>
      <p>For example, where matrinames exist, they are passed from mother to child along with <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> (mtDNA). Similarly, where patrinames exist, they are passed from father to son along with <a href="/wiki/Y_chromosome" title="Y chromosome">Y-chromosome</a> DNA (<a href="/wiki/Y-DNA" class="mw-redirect" title="Y-DNA">Y-DNA</a>). Within a patrilineal culture, if any group of women sharing mtDNA (from their common mother-line ancestor) was to choose a surname and then hand it down to successive generations, by definition, that surname would become a matrilineal surname or matriname within a patrilineal culture.<sup id="cite_ref-sykes_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sykes-1">[1]</a></sup> The test of whether a particular surname is a matriname is to determine if it is being handed down from mother to daughter in a <i>matriline</i>. </p>
      <p>The usual lack of matrinames to hand down in patrilineal cultures makes <i>traditional <a href="/wiki/Genealogy" title="Genealogy">genealogy</a></i> more difficult in the mother-line case than in the normal (father-line) case.<sup id="cite_ref-sykes_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sykes-1">[1]</a></sup> After all, <i>father-line</i> surnames originated partly to identify individuals clearly and <a href="/wiki/Surname#History" title="Surname">were adopted</a> partly for administrative reasons;<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[b]</a></sup> and these <i>patrinames</i> help in searching for facts and documentation from centuries ago. Patrinames are stable <i>identity-surnames</i>, surnames which identify an individual, whether now or in the past or future; and matrinames similarly are <i>identity-surnames</i> for women. </p>
      <p>In the 1979 "Convention on the Elimination of Discrimination Against Women," or <a href="/wiki/CEDAW" class="mw-redirect" title="CEDAW">CEDAW</a>, the UN officially adopted a provision, item (g) of CEDAW's Article 16, to the effect that women and men, and specifically wife and husband, shall have the same rights to choose a "family name", as well as a "profession" and an "occupation".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[3]</a></sup> These three rights are only a small part of the document's long list of rights related to <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a> meant to ensure women have equal opportunities to men. However, according to the article, the United States has not yet ratified this UN Convention or the multilateral treaty. </p>
      <p>In non-discriminating states, women may eventually gain the same right to their own matriname as men have traditionally had (within father-line cultures) to their own patriname. And similarly, within mother-line or matrilineal cultures, men may gain the right to their own patriname. In other words, the <i>handing down of both</i> matrinames and patrinames would co-exist within each culture to avoid discriminating against either women or men. (Note that some cultures have <i>no</i> <a href="/wiki/Surnames" class="mw-redirect" title="Surnames">surnames</a>&nbsp;– but if a culture has surnames then in this regard a non-discriminating culture would be a both-lines [mother-line and father-line] or <a href="/wiki/Ambilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Ambilineal">ambilineal</a> culture.) </p>
      <p>The actual use of a matriname would involve, first, invention or choice of a name by a group of women who share mtDNA (from their common matriline ancestor)<sup id="cite_ref-sykes_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sykes-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[4]</a></sup> and then using it in their daughters' <a href="/wiki/Birth_record" class="mw-redirect" title="Birth record">birth records</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Birth_certificate" title="Birth certificate">birth certificates</a>). </p>
      <p>This use of the mother's matriname would be parallel to and symmetric with the normal use of the father's patriname in each new son's birth record. Note well; this <i>is</i> the above-mentioned "handing down of both" the matriname and the patriname. </p>
      <p>It should be mentioned that the <i>patriname</i> is normally a single surname, like Smith or Jones, normally not a double surname like Smith-Jones or Smith Jones.<sup id="cite_ref-2patri_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2patri-7">[c]</a></sup> And, just as men normally do not change their patriname,<sup id="cite_ref-2patri_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2patri-7">[c]</a></sup> so also women would normally not change their matriname. Thus, both identity-surnames should be equally stable over the generations.<sup id="cite_ref-sykes_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sykes-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Note that one's birth surname is one's <i>legal surname</i>, unless one changes the latter&nbsp;– such as in some purely patrilineal cultures where women traditionally change to their husband's patriname at marriage, as described in <a href="/wiki/Married_and_maiden_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Married and maiden names">Married and maiden names</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Name_change" title="Name change">Name change</a>. </p>
      <p>Here is a specific example to illustrate and summarize these concepts: the father and sons in a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_family" title="Nuclear family">nuclear family</a> have the very-familiar patriname <i>Smith</i> while the mother and daughters have the matriname <i>Momline</i> as their own (and thus equally stable) identity-surname. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Double_surname">Double surname</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matriname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Double surname">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The 4 <b>double surname</b> possibilities which combine a matriname with a patriname, in either order, and with or without a hyphen, and which thus provide the aforementioned "gender symmetry", are as follows: matriname patriname, matriname-patriname, patriname matriname, and patriname-matriname. The patrilineal surname or patriname that is received from the mother in patrilineal cultures does not qualify as a matriname, as stated in the preceding section. </p>
      <p>3 of the 4 possibilities enumerated above are used together in the example below. Such conventions of double surnames were proposed<sup id="cite_ref-sykes_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sykes-1">[1]</a></sup> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seven_Daughters_of_Eve" title="The Seven Daughters of Eve">The Seven Daughters of Eve</a></i>, and an English family with the matriname "Phythian" actually used one of them, as demonstrated and discussed in an online "feature" article.<sup id="cite_ref-dblname_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dblname-8">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>As a hypothetical example of these double surnames, let the matrinames be "Mamaname" and "Momline" and the patrinames be "Smith" and "Jones". The mother, with birth double surname "<b>Momline</b>-Jones", and the father, with birth double surname "Mamaname <b>Smith</b>", both choose to retain their birth double surnames unchanged throughout their lives and agree in some wise to denominate all of their daughters and sons with the birth double surname "Smith Momline": The mother hands down her matriname and, symmetrically, the father hands down his patriname. All of their sons have the Y-DNA of and, accordingly, the patriname "Smith" of their patriline, while all of the daughters have both the mtDNA of and, accordingly, the matriname "Momline" of their matriline.<sup id="cite_ref-sykes_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sykes-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dblname_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dblname-8">[5]</a></sup> (Note that most societies give all children of a family the same surname, as in this example.) Thus, each person has only one identity-surname, which in this example is either "Momline" or "Smith". The identity-surname of each is stable throughout life<sup id="cite_ref-2patri_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2patri-7">[c]</a></sup> and always half of whatever double surname(s) he or she assumes throughout life, including at birth and marriage(s). </p>
      <p>The family in this hypothetical example could choose to adopt a convention, given its 3 co-existing surnames "Momline-Jones", "Mamaname Smith", and "Smith Momline", of all members using only one family "usage name" in daily social life. Possible samples of this family's usage name might be any one of its 3 co-existing double surnames; one of its identity-surnames "Momline" or "Smith"; or an invented surname, e. g., "Momith", which combines "Momline" and "Smith".<sup id="cite_ref-MrsMan_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MrsMan-9">[6]</a></sup> Single surname families could use such usage names also. There is some relevant discussion in this footnote.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[d]</a></sup> This family's 3 surnames, however, <i>should</i> be used in their respective members' personal legal documents, and could also be used otherwise such as in the respective members' personal profession/vocation. </p>
      <p>Rather than keeping their own birth or legal surnames, the parents in this example might prefer <i>at marriage</i> to change their surnames to "Smith Momline", the same as their children-to-be, so that the members of their nuclear family would all share this one surname. </p>
      <p>Of course, one's own identity-surname (here, the matriname "Momline" or the patriname "Smith") would always be available as one's own usage name, such as in one's profession/vocation. </p>
      <p>This double surname example should be compared with its single surname counterpart at the end of the preceding section. </p>
      <p>In toto, the gender-symmetric single surnames presented in the preceding section have the advantage of being simpler and briefer, but if used alone, would give <i>different</i> surnames for members of the different genders in a nuclear family. In contrast, all of the children in a nuclear family would have the <i>same double</i> surname. Also, these double surnames would <i>record</i> on legal documents both the matriname and patriname, with <i>both</i> identity-surnames later aiding <i>each</i> gender in genealogy and other searches of historical records.<sup id="cite_ref-sykes_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sykes-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dblname_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dblname-8">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MrsMan_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MrsMan-9">[6]</a></sup> </p>

The Reference<sup id="cite_ref-vitales_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vitales-2">[2]</a></sup> provides a 1992 example, parts of which are available for Wikipedia readers to readily see, via "Google books".</span> </li> 
        <li id="cite_note-4"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-4">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">For a more complete historical background, see <a href="/wiki/Surname#History" title="Surname">Surname §&nbsp;History</a>.</span> </li> 
        <li id="cite_note-2patri-7"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-2patri_7-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-2patri_7-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-2patri_7-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">By reading within the <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">Family name</a> article, one notices that the single-surname (or patriname) is indeed used as the identity-surname, such as Smith or Jones, and that the double-surname Smith-Jones <i>is</i> normally used as <i>two</i> patrinames. Similarly one notices that patrinames normally are not changed.</span> </li> 
        <li id="cite_note-10"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-10">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Not only this double-surname family, but also its <i>single surname</i> version at the end of the previous section might both somehow choose to use a <i>single</i> name as a <i>usage name</i>, whether "Momline", "Smith", "Momith", or another invented single surname. In principle, any new friends have no way of knowing whether either of the family's surnames are actually single or double. These 2 cases would look the same to all others&nbsp;– except someone handling their legal documents.</span> </li> 
       </ol>
