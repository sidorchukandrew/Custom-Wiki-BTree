   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Mezzanine capital</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub" class="noprint">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div> 
    <div id="contentSub"></div> 
    <div id="jump-to-nav"></div> 
    <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a> 
    <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a> 
    <div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>In <a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">finance</a>, <b>mezzanine capital</b> is any <a href="/wiki/Subordinated_debt" title="Subordinated debt">subordinated debt</a> or <a href="/wiki/Preferred_equity" class="mw-redirect" title="Preferred equity">preferred equity</a> <a href="/wiki/Financial_instrument" title="Financial instrument">instrument</a> that represents a claim on a company's assets which is senior only to that of the <a href="/wiki/Common_stock" title="Common stock">common shares</a>. Mezzanine financings can be structured either as <a href="/wiki/Debt" title="Debt">debt</a> (typically an <a href="/wiki/Unsecured_debt" title="Unsecured debt">unsecured</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subordinated_debt" title="Subordinated debt">subordinated</a> note) or <a href="/wiki/Preferred_stock" title="Preferred stock">preferred stock</a>. </p>
      <p>Mezzanine capital is often a more expensive financing source for a company than <a href="/wiki/Secured_debt" class="mw-redirect" title="Secured debt">secured debt</a> or <a href="/wiki/Senior_debt" title="Senior debt">senior debt</a>. The higher <a href="/wiki/Cost_of_capital" title="Cost of capital">cost of capital</a> associated with mezzanine financings is the result of its being an unsecured, subordinated (or junior) obligation in a company's <a href="/wiki/Capital_structure" title="Capital structure">capital structure</a> (i.e., in the event of <a href="/wiki/Default_(finance)" title="Default (finance)">default</a>, the mezzanine financing is only repaid after all senior obligations have been satisfied). Additionally, mezzanine financings, which are usually <a href="/wiki/Private_placements" class="mw-redirect" title="Private placements">private placements</a>, are often used by smaller companies and may involve greater overall levels of leverage than issues in the <a href="/wiki/High-yield_debt" title="High-yield debt">high-yield market</a>; they thus involve additional risk. In compensation for the increased risk, mezzanine debt holders require a higher return for their investment than secured or more senior lenders. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mezzanine_capital&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Mezzanine financings can be completed through a variety of different structures based on the specific objectives of the transaction and the existing capital structure in place at the company.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The basic forms used in most mezzanine financings are <a href="/wiki/Subordinated_debt" title="Subordinated debt">subordinated notes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Preferred_stock" title="Preferred stock">preferred stock</a>. Mezzanine lenders, typically specialist mezzanine <a href="/wiki/Investment_fund" title="Investment fund">investment funds</a>, look for a certain <a href="/wiki/Internal_rate_of_return" title="Internal rate of return">rate of return</a> which can come from (each individual security can be made up of any of the following or a combination thereof): </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Cash interest: A periodic payment of cash based on a percentage of the outstanding balance of the mezzanine financing. The interest rate can be either fixed throughout the term of the loan or can fluctuate (i.e., float) along with <a href="/wiki/LIBOR" class="mw-redirect" title="LIBOR">LIBOR</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Base_rate" title="Base rate">base rates</a>.</li> 
       <li>PIK interest: <a href="/wiki/PIK_loan" title="PIK loan">Payable in kind</a> interest is a periodic form of payment in which the interest payment is not paid in cash but rather by increasing the principal amount by the amount of the interest (e.g., a $100 million bond with an 8% PIK interest rate will have a balance of $108 million at the end of the period, but will not pay any cash interest).</li> 
       <li>Ownership: Along with the typical <a href="/wiki/Interest" title="Interest">interest</a> payment associated with <a href="/wiki/Debt" title="Debt">debt</a>, mezzanine capital will often include an <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">equity</a> stake in the form of attached <a href="/wiki/Warrant_(finance)" title="Warrant (finance)">warrants</a> or a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Conversion_feature&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Conversion feature (page does not exist)">conversion feature</a> similar to that of a <a href="/wiki/Convertible_bond" title="Convertible bond">convertible bond</a>. The ownership component in mezzanine securities is almost always accompanied by either cash interest or PIK interest, and, in many cases, by both.</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>Mezzanine lenders will also often charge an arrangement fee, payable upfront at the closing of the transaction. Arrangement fees contribute the least return, and their purposes are primarily to cover administrative costs or as an incentive to complete the transaction. </p>
      <p>The following are illustrative examples of mezzanine financings: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>$100 million of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Senior_subordinated_note&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Senior subordinated note (page does not exist)">senior subordinated notes</a> with warrants (10% cash interest, 3% PIK interest and warrants representing 4% of the fully diluted ownership of the company)<sup id="cite_ref-example_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-example-1">[1]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>$50 million of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Redeemable_preferred_stock&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Redeemable preferred stock (page does not exist)">redeemable preferred stock</a> with warrants (0% cash interest, 14% PIK interest and warrants representing 6% of the fully diluted ownership of the company)<sup id="cite_ref-example_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-example-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
      </ul> 
      <p>In structuring a mezzanine security, the company and lender work together to avoid burdening the borrower with the full interest cost of such a loan. Because mezzanine lenders will seek a return of 14% to 20%, this return must be achieved through means other than simple cash interest payments. As a result, by using equity ownership and PIK interest, the mezzanine lender effectively defers its compensation until the due date of the security or a change of control of the company. </p>
      <p>Mezzanine financings can be made at either the operating company level or at the level of a <a href="/wiki/Holding_company" title="Holding company">holding company</a> (also known as <a href="/wiki/Structural_subordination" title="Structural subordination">structural subordination</a>). In a holding company structure, as there are no operations and hence no cash flows, the structural subordination of the security and the reliance on cash <a href="/wiki/Dividends" class="mw-redirect" title="Dividends">dividends</a> from the operating company introduces additional risk and typically higher cost. This approach is taken most often <span class="clarify-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">as a result of the company's existing capital structure.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Clarify" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="This passage needs to be better explained. (October 2019)">clarify</span></a></i>]</sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mezzanine_capital&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leveraged_buyouts">Leveraged buyouts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mezzanine_capital&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Leveraged buyouts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In <a href="/wiki/Leveraged_buyout" title="Leveraged buyout">leveraged buyouts</a>, mezzanine capital is used in conjunction with other securities to fund the purchase price of the company being acquired. Typically, mezzanine capital will be used to fill a financing gap between less expensive forms of financing (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Senior_debt" title="Senior debt">senior loans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Second_lien_loan" title="Second lien loan">second lien loan</a>, <a href="/wiki/High-yield_debt" title="High-yield debt">high yield financings</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">equity</a>. Often, a <a href="/wiki/Financial_sponsor" title="Financial sponsor">financial sponsor</a> will exhaust other sources of capital before turning to mezzanine capital. </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Financial_sponsor" title="Financial sponsor">Financial sponsors</a> will seek to use mezzanine capital in a <a href="/wiki/Leveraged_buyout" title="Leveraged buyout">leveraged buyout</a> in order to reduce the amount of the capital invested by the <a href="/wiki/Private_equity_firm" title="Private equity firm">private equity firm</a>; because mezzanine lenders typically have a lower target cost of capital than the private equity investor, using mezzanine capital can potentially enhance the private equity firm's investment returns. Additionally, <a href="/wiki/Middle-market_company" title="Middle-market company">middle market companies</a> may be unable to access the <a href="/wiki/High-yield_debt" title="High-yield debt">high yield market</a> due to high minimum size requirements, creating a need for flexible, private mezzanine capital. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Real_estate_finance">Real estate finance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mezzanine_capital&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Real estate finance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">real estate</a> <a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">finance</a>, mezzanine loans are often used by <a href="/wiki/Real_estate_developer" class="mw-redirect" title="Real estate developer">developers</a> to secure supplementary financing for development projects (typically in cases where the primary mortgage or construction loan equity requirements are larger than 10%).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> These sorts of mezzanine loans are often secured by a second ranking real property mortgage (that is, ranking subordinate to the first mortgage lenders). Standard mortgage foreclosure proceedings can take more than a year, depending upon the relationship between the first mortgage lenders and the mezzanine debt lender, governed by an <a href="/w/index.php?title=Intercreditor_Deed&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Intercreditor Deed (page does not exist)">Intercreditor Deed</a>. </p>
