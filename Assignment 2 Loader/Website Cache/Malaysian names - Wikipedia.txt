      <p><b>Personal names in Malaysia</b> vary greatly according to ethno-cultural group. <a href="/wiki/Personal_name" title="Personal name">Personal names</a> are, to a certain degree, regulated by the national registration department, especially since the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_identity_card" title="Malaysian identity card">National Registration Identity Card (NRIC)</a>. </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_Chinese" title="Malaysian Chinese">Malaysian Chinese</a> are the only major ethnic group in Malaysia to use <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family names</a>. Most other groups, including the ethnic <a href="/wiki/Malays_(ethnic_group)" title="Malays (ethnic group)">Malays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orang_Asli" title="Orang Asli">Orang Asli</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bumiputera_(Malaysia)" title="Bumiputera (Malaysia)">Bumiputera</a> of Sabah and Sarawak, share a naming custom that includes the use of a personal name followed by a <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronym</a> name. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Malay_names">Malay names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Malay names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Traditional Malay names were taken from one of a number of languages, or even a combination of two or more elements from these languages: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li><a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a> such as Intan, Kiambang or Tuah</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Khmer_language" title="Khmer language">Khmer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Siamese</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cham_language" title="Cham language">Cham</a> such as Tam, Som or Lai</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Javanese_language" title="Javanese language">Javanese</a> such as Ratnasari, Joyo or Kesuma</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pali" title="Pali">Pali</a> such as Wira, Darma or Wati</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>Arabic names were introduced later along with Islam but did not become dominant among commoners until the colonial era. Although traditional Malay names were still widely used for centuries afterward, they are now primarily confined to rural areas. Malaysia's National Registration Department doesn't allow names which they deem to have negative or obscene meanings, such as Pendek which means short. The Department additionally bans names with the meaning of colors, animals and natural phenomena. This effectively renders many traditional names illegal including Puteh or Putih (white), Bulan (moon), Suria (sun), Rimau (tiger) and Awan (cloud). Because of these restrictions, the vast majority of Malays today tend to favour Arabic names. However, names from the following languages are common as well: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li><a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> such as Jihan, Mirza or Shah</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> or <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> such as Maria, Marina or Johana</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>, such as Tiara, Orked (from the English "orchid") or Ros (from "rose")</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>Names of <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabo</a>-<a href="/wiki/Hebrew" class="mw-redirect" title="Hebrew">Hebrew</a> origins are also common, for example <a href="/wiki/Adam_in_Islam" title="Adam in Islam">Adam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacob_in_Islam" title="Jacob in Islam">Yaakob</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isaac_in_Islam" title="Isaac in Islam">Ishak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bunyamin" class="mw-redirect" title="Bunyamin">Bunyamin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daniel_in_Islam" title="Daniel in Islam">Danial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sarah" title="Sarah">Sarah</a>. In addition, names of Arabo-Hebrew origins that are seldom used by Muslim Arabs are widespread among Malays, such as the female names of Meriam or <a href="/wiki/Miriam" title="Miriam">Miriam</a> (the Arabs commonly spell it as Mariam), Saloma and Rohana. </p>
      <p>In pre-modern times, words and names of Arabic derivation were adapted to suit the Classical Malay language. This is still reflected in the rural pronunciation of certain Middle Eastern names. Thus, Sharif would be Sarip and Aziz would become Ajis. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>A Malay's name consists of a personal name, which is used to address them in all circumstances, almost always followed by a patronym. Thus, most Malays do not use family names or surnames. In this respect, Malay names are similar to <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_naming_conventions" class="mw-redirect" title="Icelandic naming conventions">Icelandic naming conventions</a>. For men, the patronym consists of the title <i>bin</i> (from the Arabic <i lang="ar-Latn" title="DIN 31635 Arabic (Arabic language) transliteration">بن</i>, meaning 'son of') followed by his father's personal name. if Osman has a son called Musa, Musa will be known as Musa bin Osman. For women, the patronym consists of the title <i>binti</i> (from the Arabic <i lang="ar-Latn" title="DIN 31635 Arabic (Arabic language) transliteration">بنت</i>, meaning 'daughter of') followed by her father's name. Thus, if Musa has a daughter called Aisyah, Aisyah will be known as Aisyah binti Musa. Upon marriage, a woman does not change her name, as is done in many cultures. </p>
      <p>In the past it was uncommon for a Malay to have more than one personal name, but in modern times Malay names may consist of two and sometimes three personal names. </p>
      <p>In October 2018, the longest recorded name for a Malaysian is 'Princess Aura Nurr Ermily Amara Auliya Bidadari Nawal El-Zendra', comprizing 78 letters, 2 letters short of the allowed 80 letters. </p>
      <p>Some are taken from public figures around the world such as Mohammad Rifae Zidane, whose third personal name is taken from the <a href="/wiki/Zinedine_Zidane" title="Zinedine Zidane">famous footballer</a>. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Patronymic">Patronymic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Patronymic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The patronymic is employed by almost all Malays in accordance with local customs as well as ones adopted from the Arabs, Hebrews and others. Sometimes the title part of the patronymic, <i>Bin</i> or <i>Binti</i>, is reduced to <i>B.</i> for men, or to <i>Bt.</i>, <i>Bte.</i> or <i>Bint.</i> for women. Foreigners sometimes take this abbreviation erroneously for a <a href="/wiki/Middle_name" title="Middle name">middle initial</a>. In general practice, most Malays omit the title <i>Bin</i> or <i>Binti</i> from their names. Therefore, the two examples from the paragraph above would be known as Musa Osman and Aisyah Musa. When presented in this way, the second part of the name is often mistaken by foreigners for a <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family name</a>. </p>
      <p>When someone is referred to using only one name, the first name is always used, never the second (because it would be rude to call someone by his or her father's name). Thus, Musa Osman is Mr Musa (or Encik Musa in Malay), and Aisyah Musa is Mrs/Ms/Miss Aisyah (or Puan/Cik Aisyah in Malay). Occasionally, however, a man's personal name comes after the prophet Mohammed's name, or the word <a href="/wiki/Abdul" title="Abdul">Abdul</a>. In such a case, the man will usually be referred to by his second name, if the third name is the patronymic. For example, Mohammed Hisyam bin Ariffin would be referred by the name Mr Hisyam, or Abdul Rahman bin Rasyid would be referred to as Mr Abdul Rahman. </p>
      <p>It is argued<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (March 2016)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> that the Mr or Mrs form of address is not compatible with the Malay naming system, probably due to the lack of family or surnames.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (March 2016)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> It is therefore customary to address Malays using the Malay forms of address (Encik or Puan/Cik). </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surnames">Surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       See also: 
       <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_names#Hereditary_titles" title="Malaysian names">Malaysian names §&nbsp;Hereditary titles</a>
      </div>
      <p><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Category:Articles_contradicting_other_articles" title="Category:Articles contradicting other articles"><span title="Are they surnames or titles after all? (January 2016)">contradictory</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>A few Malay families do use surnames, such as Tengku, Megat, Nik, Wan, Raja and Che, which are passed down patrilineally, while others such as <i>Merican</i>, <i>Khan</i> and <i>Munsi</i> indicate an Indian Muslim ancestry (since these names are Indian Muslim in origin). Other common surnames includes <i>Sayid</i> or <i>Syed</i> (of Arab ancestry), <i>Teuku</i> (of <a href="/wiki/Aceh" title="Aceh">Aceh</a>), <i>Daeng</i> (of <a href="/wiki/Bugis" class="mw-redirect" title="Bugis">Bugis</a> origin), etc. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Honorifics">Honorifics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Honorifics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In olden times, the <a href="/wiki/Peranakan_Chinese" class="mw-redirect" title="Peranakan Chinese">first group of Chinese people in Malaysia</a> used to be held in high regard by Malays. Some Malays in the past may have taken the word "Baba", referring to Chinese males, and put it into their name, when this used to be the case.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This is not followed by the younger generation, and the current <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Malaysians" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese Malaysians">Chinese Malaysians</a> do not have the same status as they previously had. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_personal_names_or_double_names">Second personal names or double names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Second personal names or double names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Another feature in Malay names, which is very common, is the existence of second personal names or double names. This seems to have been developed in response to the use of very popular Muslim names, like <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_(name)" title="Muhammad (name)">Muhammad</a> and Ahmad for men, and Nur and Siti for women. Bearers of these names, and their variants, often add a more distinctive second name, like Muhammad Osman or Nur Mawar. The patronym is then added after these. </p>
      <p>The popular first elements in double Malay male names are: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li><a href="/wiki/Muhammad_(name)" title="Muhammad (name)">Muhammad</a>/ Mohammad/ Mohammed (often abbreviated to Muhd., Mohd., Md. or simply M.)</li> 
       <li>Mat — the Malay variant of Muhammad. Mat is also the casual spoken form of names ending with -mad or -mat such as Ahmad, Rahmat, Samad, etc.</li> 
       <li>Mamat - another variety of Muhammad</li> 
       <li>Ahmad</li> 
       <li>Awang (Commonly used in Brunei and Sabah)</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>The most common first elements in double Malay female names are: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Nur/Nurul/Noor/Nor</li> 
       <li>Siti/Ct</li> 
       <li>Dayang (Commonly used in Brunei and Sabah)</li>
      </ul> 
      <p><br> A special case of double names for men is the use of <a href="/wiki/Abdul" title="Abdul">Abdul</a>. Following Arabic naming practices, <i>Abdul</i> simply means 'servant of' and must be followed by one of the <a href="/wiki/Names_of_God_in_the_Qur%27an" class="mw-redirect" title="Names of God in the Qur'an">names of God in the Qur'an</a>; for example Abdul Haqq means 'servant of the Truth'. </p>
      <p>Thus, Osman may have another son called Abdul Haqq, who is known as Abdul Haqq bin Osman, or Abdul Haqq Osman. Then he, in turn, may have a daughter called Nur Mawar, who is known as Nur Mawar binte Abdul Haqq, or Nor Mawar Abdul Haqq. It is often common to drop the first element in these double names, even if it is Abdul, and so the examples could be known as Haqq Osman and Mawar Haqq. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Malay_titles">Malay titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Malay titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: 
       <a href="/wiki/Malay_styles_and_titles" title="Malay styles and titles">Malay styles and titles</a>
      </div> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hereditary_titles">Hereditary titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hereditary titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In different parts of Malaysia, traditionally inherited (patrilineally) <a href="/wiki/Malay_titles" class="mw-redirect" title="Malay titles">Malay titles</a> and sometimes matrilineally, are used and often incorporated into the naming system as the first part of double names. Most of those with these titles are descended from royalty or nobility. </p>
      <p>The examples of inherited titles are: </p>
      <p>by Patrilineal Royal descent (Malay) </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Tunku</li> 
       <li>Tengku</li> 
       <li>Raja</li> 
       <li>Wan</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>by Patrilineal Royal descent (Malay - Mon-Khmer) </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Yang</li> 
       <li>Long</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>by Patrilineal Royal descent (Acheh - Malay) </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Teuku</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>by Patrilineal Royal descent (Bugis - Malay) </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Daeng</li> 
       <li>Daing</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>by Matrilineal Royal descent (Malay) </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Engku</li> 
       <li>Ungku</li> 
       <li>Megat (male)</li> 
       <li>Puteri (female)</li> 
       <li>Wan</li> 
       <li>Nik</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>by Patrilineal and/or Matrilineal, Royal and/or Noble descent </p> 
      <ul>
       <li><a href="/wiki/Syed" class="mw-redirect" title="Syed">Syed</a>/Sharifah (for male and female, respectively) — indicating direct patrilineal descent from the family of the <a href="/wiki/Prophet_Muhammad" class="mw-redirect" title="Prophet Muhammad">Prophet Muhammad</a>.</li> 
       <li>Mior (for male only) — indicating direct matrilineal descent from the family of the <a href="/wiki/Prophet_Muhammad" class="mw-redirect" title="Prophet Muhammad">Prophet Muhammad</a>.</li> 
       <li>Awangku/Pangiran/Pengiran, Dayangku (commonly found in <a href="/wiki/Sabah" title="Sabah">Sabah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sarawak" title="Sarawak">Sarawak</a>, for male and female, denoting <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Bruneian</a>-Malay aristocratic descent).</li> 
       <li>Tuan (used more generally as a respectful term of address for men, like 'sir')</li> 
       <li>Awang/Dayang, Abang/Dayang or Dayangku (used in <a href="/wiki/Sabah" title="Sabah">Sabah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sarawak" title="Sarawak">Sarawak</a>, for male and female, respectively)</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>by Patrilineal Noble descent </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Wan</li> 
       <li>Nik</li> 
       <li>Che</li> 
       <li>Tun</li> 
       <li>Azmi</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>All hereditary titles are controlled and regulated as well as registered by the Malaysian National Registration Department and must appear in the National Registration Identity Cards (NRIC), passports as well as all official documents. A person may not in any circumstances be denied or stripped of his or her hereditary titles and persons with no evidence of inheritance are not allowed to carry these titles in accordance to local customs as well as the national registration naming regulations. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-hereditary_titles">Non-hereditary titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Non-hereditary titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The titles above should not be confused with those given by special award which are non-hereditary, like 'Datuk', 'Tan Sri' and 'Tun'. </p>
      <p>These titles are usually awarded by the <a href="/wiki/Sultan" title="Sultan">Sultans</a> of the recipients' respective states as well as the <a href="/wiki/Yang_Dipertuan_Agong" class="mw-redirect" title="Yang Dipertuan Agong">Yang Dipertuan Agong</a> and the state <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yang_Dipertua&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Yang Dipertua (page does not exist)">Yang Dipertua</a> as recognition for their contributions and services to the nation and the respective states. For example, the title 'Datuk' is given to Malaysians of all races as an honorary title. An example is <a href="/wiki/Lee_Chong_Wei" title="Lee Chong Wei">Datuk Lee Chong Wei</a>, a famous badminton player who was awarded the title as recognition to his achievement in becoming the third Malaysian to win a silver Olympic medal <a href="/wiki/2008_Summer_Olympics" title="2008 Summer Olympics">2008 Beijing Summer Olympics</a>. If the recipient is a man, his wife is automatically bestowed with the title 'Datin' but not in reverse. </p>
      <p>The title 'Tun' is reserved for nationally important persons, like the fourth <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Malaysia" title="Prime Minister of Malaysia">Prime Minister of Malaysia</a> <a href="/wiki/Mahathir_bin_Mohamad" class="mw-redirect" title="Mahathir bin Mohamad">Tun Mahathir bin Mohamad</a>. He was given the title after his resignation at 2003. </p>
      <p>For an example of a complex name, one former <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Malaysia" title="Prime Minister of Malaysia">Prime Minister of Malaysia</a> has the full name <a href="/wiki/Najib_Tun_Razak" class="mw-redirect" title="Najib Tun Razak">Dato' Seri Mohd Najib bin Tun Haji Abdul Razak</a>, where 'Dato' Seri' is a <a href="/wiki/Malay_title" class="mw-redirect" title="Malay title">Malay title</a> of honour, 'Mohd Najib' is his personal name (often further abbreviated to 'Najib'), 'bin' introduces his father's titles and names, <i>Tun</i> is a higher honour, 'Haji' denotes his father as a pilgrim to <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a>, and 'Abdul Razak' is his father's personal name (often abbreviated to <i>Razak</i>). The entire name has various shorter forms, like 'Mohd Najib Tun Abdul Razak', 'Najib Tun Razak' and 'Najib Razak'). </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Haji_or_Hajjah">Haji or Hajjah</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Haji or Hajjah">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>If someone has been on the <a href="/wiki/Hajj" title="Hajj">Hajj</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pilgrimage" title="Pilgrimage">pilgrimage</a> to <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a>, they may be called <a href="/wiki/Hajji" title="Hajji">Haji</a> for men or Hajjah for women. Thus, if Musa Bin Osman went on the Hajj, he could be called Haji Musa Bin Osman, and his daughter Aisyah might be called Aisyah Binti Haji Musa. If Aisyah herself have gone for the hajj, her name would be Hajjah Aisyah Binti Haji Musa. The titles can also be shortened in writing to 'Hj.' for Haji and 'Hjh' for Hajjah. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_names">Chinese names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Chinese names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Traditional <a href="/wiki/Chinese_name" title="Chinese name">Chinese names</a> are used among the <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_Chinese" title="Malaysian Chinese">Malaysian Chinese</a>. These names are usually represented as three words, for example <i>Foo Li Leen</i> or <i>Tan Ai Lin</i>. The first is the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese family name">Chinese family name</a>, which is passed down from a father to all his children. The two other parts of the name form an indivisible <a href="/wiki/Chinese_given_name" title="Chinese given name">Chinese given name</a>, which may contain a <a href="/wiki/Generation_name" title="Generation name">generation name</a>. In other cultures, the family name is sometimes shifted to the end of the name (for example, <i>Li Leen Foo</i>). </p>
      <p>Some Chinese use a Western personal name (for example, <i>Denise Foo</i>), and some use this in preference to a Chinese given name. Most of these are used by Chinese <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_Malaysia" title="Christianity in Malaysia">Malaysian Christians</a>, or Chinese who primarily speak English. On official documents, this name is either written in the order <i>Western given name - surname - Chinese given name</i> (e.g., Denise Foo Li Leen) or <i>surname - Chinese given name - Western given name</i> (e.g., Foo Li Leen Denise),<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <i>or Western given name - Chinese given name - surname</i> (e.g., Denise Li Leen Foo). In general practice, only one of the given names (the Western or Chinese name) is used. Chinese Malaysian Muslims may use Arabic given names while some use Arabic-derived Chinese names, e.g., Firdaus Fong Siew Chong. </p>
      <p>As no formal system of <a href="/wiki/Romanisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Romanisation">romanisation</a> is imposed on Chinese names in Malaysia at the time of birth registration, names are often romanised according to the judgment of the registration clerk or according to the preference of the proposer. Hence, romanisation errors are not uncommon resulting in unusual names. Since the 1980s, <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">Pinyin</a> names are becoming more common, although one would not say popular. The Pinyin form is based on <a href="/wiki/Standard_Chinese" title="Standard Chinese">Mandarin or Putonghua</a>, whereas most existing romanised surnames are based on <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Chinese" title="Varieties of Chinese">dialects</a>. For example, a Tan (<a href="/wiki/Fujian" title="Fujian">Fujian</a> dialect) is Chen in the Pinyin form. In <a href="/wiki/Fuzhou" title="Fuzhou">Fuzhou</a>, the existing romanised form is Ding. As parents prefer their children to have the same romanised surname as their father, names such as Tan Jia Ling where Tan is in <a href="/wiki/Hokkien_dialect" class="mw-redirect" title="Hokkien dialect">Hokkien</a> and Jia Ling in Mandarin are becoming common. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indian_names">Indian names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Indian names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Officially, <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_Indian" class="mw-redirect" title="Malaysian Indian">Malaysian Indians</a> use a patronymic naming system combining their traditional <a href="/wiki/Indian_name" title="Indian name">Indian names</a> with some Malay words, while others use <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telugu_language" title="Telugu language">Telugu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malayalam" title="Malayalam">Malayalam</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> names. A man's name would consist of his personal name followed by the Malay phrase <i>anak lelaki</i>, meaning 'son of', and then his father's name. A woman's name would consist of her personal name followed by the Malay phrase <i>anak perempuan</i>, meaning 'daughter of', and then her father's name. The Malay patronymic phrase is often abbreviated to <i>a/l</i> ('son of') or <i>a/p</i> ('daughter of') and then their father's name. In many circumstances, the intervening Malay is omitted, and the father's name follows immediately after a person's given name. </p>
      <p>Following traditional practice from South India, the father's name is sometimes abbreviated to an <a href="/wiki/Initialism" class="mw-redirect" title="Initialism">initial</a> and placed before the personal name. Thus, a man called Anbuselvan whose father is called Ramanan may be called Anbuselvan anak lelaki Ramanan (formal), Anbuselvan a/l Ramanan (as on his government identification card), Anbuselvan Ramanan or R. Anbuselvan. Whereas, his daughter Mathuram would be called Mathuram anak perempuan Anbuselvan (formal), Mathuram a/p Anbuselvan (as on her government identification card), Mathuram Anbuselvan or A. Mathuram. Although not recorded officially, an Indian woman may use her husband's personal name instead of her father's name after marriage. </p>
      <p>Indian Malaysian Muslims, like ethnic Malays, use Arabic names or names of their own languages, while Arabic-derived Christian names may also be used by Indian Malaysian Christians. </p>
      <p>Sikh Malaysian usage follows either the western fashion of personal names followed by the family name or more commonly the South Indian pattern with the personal name followed with the phrase " anak lelaki" (son of) for males and "anak perempuan (daughter of) for females. Examples of Sikh western-style usage where their full personal names followed by their family (or clan) name are: Harjit Singh Gill (for a male) or Harjit Kaur Gill (for a female). Examples of usage in the South Indian fashion would be: Harjit Singh a/l Jit Singh (for a male) and Harjit Kaur a/p Jit Singh. </p>
      <p>Abbreviations 'a/l' and 'a/p' are mostly eliminated in Malaysian passports (along with the use of 'bin' and 'binti') since the infamous Binladen became notorious. That is probably to avoid confusion on whether the (Muslim) holder is related to Binladen.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indonesian_names">Indonesian names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Indonesian names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: 
       <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_names" title="Indonesian names">Indonesian names</a>
      </div> 
      <p>Although Indonesian (especially Javanese) immigrants in Malaysia may carry Indonesian names such as Sukanto, their Malaysian-born children tend to have Arabic names.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_of_members_of_other_groups">Names of members of other groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Names of members of other groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">Peninsular</a> <a href="/wiki/Orang_Asli" title="Orang Asli">Orang Asli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sarawak" title="Sarawak">Sarawakian</a> <a href="/wiki/Bumiputra" class="mw-redirect" title="Bumiputra">Bumiputra</a> use the Malay word <i>anak</i> ('child of') to form their patronymics regardless of an individual's sex, for example, <i>Sagong anak Tasi</i>. However, most of the new generation indigenous people in Sabah and Sarawak who live in town areas and who practice Christianity as a religion, tend to have a Christian first name, for example Melissa Melanie Raweng (Raweng being the father's name). </p>
      <p>Some <a href="/wiki/Sabah" title="Sabah">Sabah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sarawak" title="Sarawak">Sarawak</a> Bumiputra have patronymics in the same fashion as Malays, using <i>bin</i> or <i>binti</i>, while others have <a href="/wiki/Patrilineal_surname" class="mw-redirect" title="Patrilineal surname">patrilineal surnames</a> which are handed down unchanged from generation to generation. </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Minangkabau_people" title="Minangkabau people">Minangkabau descendants</a> use clan and tribal names passed down matrilineally. </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Kristang_people" title="Kristang people">Kristang people</a> usually have <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Portuguese names">Portuguese</a>, or, at least, more European-sounding names, including inherited <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family names</a>. In fact, Arabs and Portuguese have common denominator in influence in names: Fatima, Omar, and Soraya. These names are common in Portugal given by Arab influence. </p>
