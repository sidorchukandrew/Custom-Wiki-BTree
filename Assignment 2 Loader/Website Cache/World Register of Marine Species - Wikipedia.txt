      <p>The <b>World Register of Marine Species</b> (<b>WoRMS</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Taxonomic_database" title="Taxonomic database">taxonomic database</a> that aims to provide an authoritative and comprehensive list of names of marine organisms.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Register_of_Marine_Species&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The content of the registry is edited and maintained by scientific specialists on each group of organism. These <a href="/wiki/Taxonomist" class="mw-redirect" title="Taxonomist">taxonomists</a> control the quality of the information, which is gathered from the primary scientific literature as well as from some external regional and taxon-specific databases. WoRMS maintains valid names of all marine organisms, but also provides information on <a href="/wiki/Synonym_(taxonomy)" title="Synonym (taxonomy)">synonyms</a> and invalid names. It is an ongoing task to maintain the registry, since new species are constantly being discovered and described by scientists; in addition, the nomenclature and taxonomy of existing species is often corrected or changed as new research is constantly being published. </p>
      <p>Subsets of WoRMS content are made available, and can have separate badging and their own home/launch pages, as "subregisters", such as the <i>World List of Marine Acanthocephala</i>, <i>World List of Actiniaria</i>, <i>World Amphipoda Database</i>, and so on. As of December 2018 there were 60 such taxonomic subregisters, including a number presently under construction.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A second category of subregisters comprises regional species databases such as the <i>African Register of Marine Species</i>, <i>Belgian Register of Marine Species</i>, etc., while a third comprises thematic subsets such as the <i>World Register of Deep-Sea species (WoRDSS)</i>, <i>World Register of Introduced Marine Species (WRiMS)</i>, etc. In all of these cases, the base data are entered and held once only as part of the WoRMS data system for ease of maintenance and data consistency, and are redisplayed as needed in the context of the relevant subregister or subregisters to which they may also belong. </p>
      <p>Certain subregisters expand content beyond the original "marine" concept of WoRMS by including freshwater or terrestrial taxa for completeness in their designated area of interest; such records can be excluded from a standard search of WoRMS by selecting appropriate options in the online search interface. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Register_of_Marine_Species&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>WoRMS was founded in 2008 and grew out of the <a href="/wiki/MarBEF_Data_System" title="MarBEF Data System">European Register of Marine Species</a> and the UNESCO-IOC Register of Marine Organisms (URMO), which was compiled by Jacob van der Land (and several colleagues) at the National Museum of Natural History, Leiden <sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is primarily funded by the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> and hosted by the <a href="/wiki/Flanders_Marine_Institute" title="Flanders Marine Institute">Flanders Marine Institute</a> (VLIZ) in <a href="/wiki/Ostend" title="Ostend">Ostend</a>, Belgium. WoRMS has established formal agreements with several other biodiversity projects, including the <a href="/wiki/Global_Biodiversity_Information_Facility" title="Global Biodiversity Information Facility">Global Biodiversity Information Facility</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Encyclopedia_of_Life" title="Encyclopedia of Life">Encyclopedia of Life</a>. In 2008, WoRMS stated that it hoped to have an up-to-date record of all marine species completed by 2010, the year in which the <a href="/wiki/Census_of_Marine_Life" title="Census of Marine Life">Census of Marine Life</a> was completed.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>As of February 2018, WoRMS contained listings for 480,931 marine species names (including synonyms) of which 240,633 are valid marine species (95&nbsp;% checked). Their goal is to have a listing for each of the approximately more than 240,000 marine species.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>VLIZ also hosts the <a href="/wiki/Interim_Register_of_Marine_and_Nonmarine_Genera" title="Interim Register of Marine and Nonmarine Genera">Interim Register of Marine and Nonmarine Genera</a> (IRMNG), using a common infrastructure.<sup id="cite_ref-LifeWatch_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LifeWatch-7">[7]</a></sup> </p>
