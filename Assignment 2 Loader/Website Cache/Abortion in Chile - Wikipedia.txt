   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Abortion in Chile</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub" class="noprint">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div> 
    <div id="contentSub"></div> 
    <div id="jump-to-nav"></div> 
    <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a> 
    <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a> 
    <div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p><b>Abortion in Chile</b> is legal in the following cases: when the mother's life is at risk, when the fetus will not survive the pregnancy, and during the first 12 weeks of pregnancy (14 weeks, if the woman is under 14 years old) in the case of rape.<sup id="cite_ref-law_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-law-1">[1]</a></sup> Between 1989 and 2017 Chile had one of the most restrictive abortion policies in the world, criminalizing its practice without exception.<sup id="cite_ref-forosalud_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forosalud-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The bill permitting abortion was approved by the <a href="/wiki/National_Congress_of_Chile" title="National Congress of Chile">National Congress</a> in August 2017, and became law a month later<sup id="cite_ref-law_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-law-1">[1]</a></sup> after surviving a constitutional challenge brought by the <a href="/wiki/Chile_Vamos" title="Chile Vamos">conservative opposition</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Medical coverage in the public and private sector became available on January 29, 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-coverage_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-coverage-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-resolucion_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-resolucion-7">[7]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_background">Legal background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abortion_in_Chile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Legal background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Therapeutic_abortion" class="mw-redirect" title="Therapeutic abortion">Therapeutic abortion</a>, which was permitted by the Health Code in 1931,<sup id="cite_ref-forosalud_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forosalud-2">[2]</a></sup> was abolished by <a href="/wiki/Military_dictatorship_of_Chile_(1973%E2%80%9390)" class="mw-redirect" title="Military dictatorship of Chile (1973–90)">the military dictatorship</a> on September 15, 1989,<sup id="cite_ref-CodigoSanitario_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CodigoSanitario-8">[8]</a></sup> arguing that due to advances in medicine it was "no longer justifiable."<sup id="cite_ref-PuntoFinal_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PuntoFinal-9">[9]</a></sup> Before the ban, any woman whose life was in danger could ask to get an abortion, if she had the approval of two doctors.<sup id="cite_ref-forosalud_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forosalud-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Current laws against abortion are codified in the <a href="/wiki/Penal_Code" class="mw-redirect" title="Penal Code">Penal Code</a> articles 342 to 345 under the title "Crimes and Offences against Family Order, Public Morality and Sexual Integrity."<sup id="cite_ref-CodigoPenal_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CodigoPenal-10">[10]</a></sup> The Penal Code punishes illegal abortions with up to five years in jail for the person performing it, and with up to ten years if violence is used against the pregnant woman. The pregnant woman may get up to five years if she consents to an illegal abortion or performs it on herself. A medical doctor practicing an illegal abortion may get up to 15 years. The country's <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Chile" title="Constitution of Chile">constitution</a> in article 19-1, states that "the law protects the life of those about to be born." A two-thirds majority of each chamber of the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Chile" class="mw-redirect" title="Congress of Chile">Chilean Congress</a> is required to amend it.<sup id="cite_ref-Constitution_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Constitution-11">[11]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Since 1990, 15 abortion-related bills have been submitted by legislators to Congress for discussion, 12 in the <a href="/wiki/Chamber_of_Deputies_of_Chile" title="Chamber of Deputies of Chile">Chamber of Deputies</a> and three in the <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_Chile" title="Senate of Chile">Senate</a>. About half called to either increase existing penalties or to create legal barriers to make it more difficult for abortion to be legalized. Two other bills suggested erecting monuments to the "innocent victims of abortion." Four bills have requested that abortion be allowed when the mother's life is at risk and one in the case of rape. Nine are currently in review and one has been rejected. Five others have been archived, which means they have not been discussed for two years. Two identical bills requesting the reestablishment of therapeutic abortion as it was before 1989 are currently in review in the Chamber's Medical Commission, the first submitted on January 23, 2003, and the latest on March 19, 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-COD_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-COD-12">[12]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In November 2013, during the administration of conservative President <a href="/wiki/Sebasti%C3%A1n_Pi%C3%B1era" title="Sebastián Piñera">Sebastián Piñera</a>, a law was published declaring March 25 as the "day of those about to be born and of adoption."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>On 31 January 2015, President <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Bachelet" title="Michelle Bachelet">Michelle Bachelet</a> sent a draft bill to Congress to decriminalize abortion in the following cases: when the mother's life is at risk, when the fetus will not survive the pregnancy, and in the case of rape during the first 12 weeks of pregnancy (18 weeks, if the woman is under 14 years old).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> On 2 August 2017, the Congress approved the bill, lowering the number of weeks to 14 from 18, in the case a girl under 14 is raped.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-15">[15]</a></sup> A request by the opposition to declare the law unconstitutional was rejected by the country's Constitutional Court in a 6-4 decision on 21 August 2017. The ruling allowed health facilities to refuse to provide abortions by becoming "<a href="/wiki/Conscientious_objector" title="Conscientious objector">conscientious objectors</a>," although the bill, as approved by Congress, granted this right only to individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">[4]</a></sup> The law was promulgated by President Bachelet on 14 September 2017,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and was published in the country's official gazette on 23 September 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-law_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-law-1">[1]</a></sup> Medical coverage in the public and private sector became available on January 29, 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-coverage_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-coverage-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-resolucion_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-resolucion-7">[7]</a></sup> A protocol for "conscientious objectors" was published by the Health Ministry on January 27, 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Such protocol was modified by the incoming administration of <a href="/wiki/Sebasti%C3%A1n_Pi%C3%B1era" title="Sebastián Piñera">Sebastián Piñera</a> on 23 March 2018, lifting the prohibition on private health institutions receiving state funds from invoking conscientious objection.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span id="Women.27s_health"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Women's_health">Women's health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abortion_in_Chile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Women's health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>An amendment made by the Chilean government to section 119 of the Health Code in 1989 stated that there could be no actions taken that would induce an abortion. This amendment was made due to the belief that with medical advances in maternal care, abortion was no longer seen as a necessary means of saving a woman's life.<sup id="cite_ref-unpd_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unpd-20">[20]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Concern over high rates of abortion and high <a href="/wiki/Maternal_mortality" class="mw-redirect" title="Maternal mortality">maternal mortality</a> rates led the Chilean government to launch a publicly funded <a href="/wiki/Family_planning" title="Family planning">family planning</a> program in 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-unpd_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unpd-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-shep_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shep-21">[21]</a></sup> Deaths due to illegal abortions dropped from 118 to 24 per 100,000 live births between 1964 and 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-unpd_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unpd-20">[20]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>There was also a statistically significant decrease in maternal deaths due to abortion from 1990 to 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Experts attribute the decline in hospitalizations due to abortion during this period to the increased use of <a href="/wiki/Asepsis" title="Asepsis">sterilization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotics</a> by illegal abortion providers, the increased availability of the <a href="/wiki/Abortifacient" title="Abortifacient">abortifacient</a> drug <a href="/wiki/Misoprostol" title="Misoprostol">misoprostol</a>, and the increased use of <a href="/wiki/Contraception" class="mw-redirect" title="Contraception">contraception</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-shep_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shep-21">[21]</a></sup> In terms of accessibility, in 2002 it was noted that most of the family planning services were offered to married women.<sup id="cite_ref-unpd_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unpd-20">[20]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>A 2015 study by the Chilean epidemiologist Elard Koch has shown that the decreasing trend in maternal deaths due to abortion has continued through 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> These results challenge the common notion that less permissive abortion laws lead to greater mortality associated with abortion. Koch states that the increases in women's education and in community support programs for women with unplanned pregnancies have contributed to the reduction of induced abortions and maternal deaths in Chile. </p>
      <p>In the period 2000 to 2004, abortion was the third leading cause of maternal mortality in the country, accounting for 12% of all maternal deaths.<sup id="cite_ref-MaternalMortality_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MaternalMortality-24">[24]</a></sup> While there are no accurate statistics, it is estimated that between 2000 and 2002 there were between 132,000 and 160,000 abortions in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-humanas_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-humanas-25">[25]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>A 1997 study found that the majority of eighty women prosecuted in <a href="/wiki/Santiago,_Chile" class="mw-redirect" title="Santiago, Chile">Santiago</a> for having an abortion were young, single mothers, and that many were domestic workers who had moved to the city from rural areas. It also found most of the women were reported to authorities by the hospital at which they sought treatment for their complications, and had no legal representation, or were defended by inexperienced law students.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_opinion">Public opinion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abortion_in_Chile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Public opinion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>A July 2006 <a href="/wiki/MORI" class="mw-redirect" title="MORI">MORI</a> survey found that 26% of Chileans believed that abortion is "justified," up from 18% in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-liberal_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-liberal-27">[27]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>A July 2008 all-female nationwide face-to-face poll by <a href="/wiki/NGO" class="mw-redirect" title="NGO">NGO</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corporaci%C3%B3n_Humanas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Corporación Humanas (page does not exist)">Corporación Humanas</a> found that 79.2% of Chilean women were in favor of decriminalizing abortion when the life of the pregnant woman is at risk; 67.9% said it was urgent to legislate on the matter. According to the study, 74.0% of women believed abortion should be permitted in cases of rape, 70.1% in instances of fetal abnormality and 24% in all cases a woman decided it was appropriate.<sup id="cite_ref-humanas_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-humanas-25">[25]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>A March 2009 nationwide telephone poll published by <i><a href="/wiki/La_Tercera" title="La Tercera">La Tercera</a></i> newspaper found 67% were against abortion, 19% in favor and 11% in favor only in extreme cases. Regarding abortion when the life of the pregnant woman is at risk, 48% were in favor, 3% only in extreme cases and 47% were against. In cases where the baby would be born with a defect or disease that would most likely cause the baby's death, 51% were against permitting an abortion, 45% were in favor and 2% only in extreme cases. 83% were against performing an abortion on an underage girl who had unprotected sex, while 14% were in favor. 57% were in favor of abortion in the case of rape, with 39% against it.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>An October 2009 opinion poll published by <a href="/wiki/Universidad_Diego_Portales" class="mw-redirect" title="Universidad Diego Portales">Universidad Diego Portales</a> and covering 85% of urban areas of Chile found that a majority were against abortion when the pregnant woman or couple did not have the economic means to raise a baby (80%), when the pregnant woman or couple did not want to have a baby (68%), and when the fetus had a "serious defect" (51%). On the other hand, a majority were in favor of abortion when the pregnant woman's health is at risk (63%) and in cases of rape (64%).<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>A January 2017 opinion poll conducted by CADEM found that 57% wanted abortion to be allowed in only a few cases, while 19% wanted abortion illegal in all cases and 22% wanted it legal in all cases.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> A majority were in favor of abortion when the woman's health is at risk (76%), when the fetus does not have a high probability of survival (72%), and when a woman is pregnant as a result of rape (71%), while only a minority supported abortion in cases of a fetus having a physical disability (36%) and in cases of a mother not being able to afford a child (20%). </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_reaction">International reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abortion_in_Chile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: International reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In November 2004, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> (UN) committee monitoring compliance with the <a href="/wiki/International_Covenant_on_Economic,_Social_and_Cultural_Rights" title="International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights">International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights</a> (CESCR) ruled that Chile should allow abortion in cases of rape and incest. In 2007, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Human_Rights_Council" title="United Nations Human Rights Council">United Nations Human Rights Council</a> expressed concern over the country's "improperly restrictive" legislation on abortion, especially in cases where the life of the mother is at risk.<sup id="cite_ref-LaTercera_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LaTercera-31">[31]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Office_of_the_United_Nations_High_Commissioner_for_Human_Rights" title="Office of the United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights">UN's High Commissioner for Human Rights</a> also expressed concern over the country's "excessively restrictive abortion laws" in May 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-LaTercera_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LaTercera-31">[31]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Move_towards_liberalisation_of_the_law">Move towards liberalisation of the law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abortion_in_Chile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Move towards liberalisation of the law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>On July 19, 2017,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_Chile" title="Senate of Chile">Senate of Chile</a> approved legislation permitting abortion under limited circumstances (if the pregnancy endangers the life of the woman, if the fetus is not viable, if the pregnancy resulted from rape) with 22 votes in favor and 13 against.<sup id="cite_ref-cnnchile_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnnchile-33">[33]</a></sup> On August 3, the <a href="/wiki/Chamber_of_Deputies_of_Chile" title="Chamber of Deputies of Chile">Chamber of Deputies of Chile</a> approved the legislation with 70 votes in favor, 45 votes against and 1 abstention.<sup id="cite_ref-cnnchile_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnnchile-33">[33]</a></sup> On August 21, 2017,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Chile's Constitutional Court accepted the constitutionality of the measure with a 6-4 vote.<sup id="cite_ref-abcchile_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abcchile-35">[35]</a></sup> President Michelle Bachelet, who had already signed the draft bill version of the law in 2015 before it was passed in August 2017,<sup id="cite_ref-euro8001f_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euro8001f-36">[36]</a></sup> praised the court's decision to make the bill a law.<sup id="cite_ref-euro8001f_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euro8001f-36">[36]</a></sup> According to polls, 70% of Chileans approve of such a law.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> </p>
