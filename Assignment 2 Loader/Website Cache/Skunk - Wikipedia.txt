      <p><b>Skunks</b> are North and South American <a href="/wiki/Mammals" class="mw-redirect" title="Mammals">mammals</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Mephitidae" title="Mephitidae">Mephitidae</a>. Mephitidae means 'stink' which is how skunks got their name. While related to <a href="/wiki/Polecat" title="Polecat">polecats</a> and other members of the <a href="/wiki/Weasel_family" class="mw-redirect" title="Weasel family">weasel family</a>, skunks have as their closest Old World relatives the <a href="/wiki/Stink_badger" title="Stink badger">stink badgers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The animals are known for their ability to spray a liquid with a strong, unpleasant <a href="#Anal_scent_glands">smell</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Different species of skunk vary in appearance from black-and-white to brown, cream or ginger colored, but all have <a href="/wiki/Aposematism" title="Aposematism">warning coloration</a>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The word "skunk" is an Americanism from the 1630s, the Massachusetts reflex of proto-Algonquian squunck, from a southern New England <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian language</a> (probably <a href="/wiki/Abenaki_language" title="Abenaki language">Abenaki</a>) seganku, from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Algonquian" class="mw-redirect" title="Proto-Algonquian">Proto-Algonquian</a> */šeka:kwa/, from */šek-/ "to urinate" + */-a:kw/ "fox." "Skunk" has historic use as an insult, attested from 1841.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1634, a skunk was described in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Jesuit_Relations" title="The Jesuit Relations">The Jesuit Relations</a></i>: </p> 
      <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>The other is a low animal, about the size of a little dog or cat. I mention it here, not on account of its excellence, but to make of it a symbol of sin. I have seen three or four of them. It has black fur, quite beautiful and shining; and has upon its back two perfectly white stripes, which join near the neck and tail, making an oval which adds greatly to their grace. The tail is bushy and well furnished with hair, like the tail of a Fox; it carries it curled back like that of a Squirrel. It is more white than black; and, at the first glance, you would say, especially when it walks, that it ought to be called Jupiter's little dog. But it is so stinking, and casts so foul an odor, that it is unworthy of being called the dog of Pluto. No sewer ever smelled so bad. I would not have believed it if I had not smelled it myself. Your heart almost fails you when you approach the animal; two have been killed in our court, and several days afterward there was such a dreadful odor throughout our house that we could not endure it. I believe the sin smelled by <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Siena" title="Catherine of Siena">Saint Catherine de Sienne</a> must have had the same vile odor.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> </p>
      </blockquote> 
      <p>In Southern United States dialect, the term <i>polecat</i> is sometimes used as a colloquial nickname for a skunk.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_description">Physical description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Skunk species vary in size from about 15.6 to 37&nbsp;in (40 to 94&nbsp;cm) long and in weight from about 1.1&nbsp;lb (0.50&nbsp;kg) (spotted skunks) to 18&nbsp;lb (8.2&nbsp;kg) (<a href="/wiki/Hog-nosed_skunk" title="Hog-nosed skunk">hog-nosed skunks</a>). They have moderately elongated bodies with relatively short, well-muscled legs and long front <a href="/wiki/Claw" title="Claw">claws</a> for digging. </p>
      <p>Although the most common fur color is black and white, some skunks are brown or grey and a few are cream-colored. All skunks are striped, even from birth. They may have a single thick stripe across back and tail, two thinner stripes, or a series of white spots and broken stripes (in the case of the spotted skunk). Some also have stripes on their legs. </p>
      <p>Skunks have five toes on the front paws and four toes on the back paws.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p><span id="diet"></span> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Skunks are <a href="/wiki/Omnivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Omnivorous">omnivorous</a>, eating both plant and animal material and changing their diets as the seasons change. They eat <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beetle#Life_cycle" title="Beetle">grubs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salamander" title="Salamander">salamanders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mole_(animal)" title="Mole (animal)">moles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>. They also commonly eat <a href="/wiki/Berries" class="mw-redirect" title="Berries">berries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">roots</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leaves" class="mw-redirect" title="Leaves">leaves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grass" class="mw-redirect" title="Grass">grasses</a>, fungi and <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nuts</a>. </p>
      <p>In settled areas, skunks also seek garbage left by humans. Less often, skunks may be found acting as <a href="/wiki/Scavenger" title="Scavenger">scavengers</a>, eating bird and <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodent</a> carcasses left by cats or other animals. Pet owners, particularly those of cats, may experience a skunk finding its way into a garage or basement where pet food is kept. Skunks commonly dig holes in lawns in search of grubs and worms. </p>
      <p>Skunks are one of the primary predators of the <a href="/wiki/Honeybee" class="mw-redirect" title="Honeybee">honeybee</a>, relying on their thick fur to protect them from stings. The skunk scratches at the front of the <a href="/wiki/Beehive" title="Beehive">beehive</a> and eats the guard bees that come out to investigate. Mother skunks are known to teach this behavior to their young. In addition, in California, skunks dig up yellow-jacket (small hornet) nests in summer, after the compacted soil under oak trees dries out and cracks open, which allows the yellow-jackets to build their nests underground.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Skunks are <a href="/wiki/Crepuscular" class="mw-redirect" title="Crepuscular">crepuscular</a> and solitary animals when not breeding, though in the colder parts of their <a href="/wiki/Range_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Range (biology)">range</a>, they may gather in communal dens for warmth. During the day they shelter in burrows, which they can dig with their powerful front claws. Males and females occupy overlapping home ranges through the greater part of the year, typically 2 to 4&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup> (0.77 to 1.54&nbsp;sq&nbsp;mi) for females and up to 20&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup> (7.7&nbsp;sq&nbsp;mi) for males. </p>
      <p>Skunks are not true hibernators in the winter, but do den up for extended periods of time. However, they remain generally inactive and feed rarely, going through a dormant stage. Over winter, multiple females (as many as 12) huddle together; males often den alone. Often, the same winter den is repeatedly used. </p>
      <p>Although they have excellent senses of smell and hearing, they have poor vision, being unable to see objects more than about 3&nbsp;m (10&nbsp;ft) away, making them vulnerable to <a href="/wiki/Roadkill" title="Roadkill">death by road traffic</a>. They are short-lived; their lifespan in the wild can reach seven years, with most living only up to a year.<sup id="cite_ref-UMich_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UMich-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PSU_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PSU-9">[9]</a></sup> In captivity, they may live for up to 10 years.<sup id="cite_ref-UMich_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UMich-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PSU_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PSU-9">[9]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Skunks mate in early spring and are <a href="/wiki/Polygynous" class="mw-redirect" title="Polygynous">polygynous</a>, meaning that successful males mate with more than one female. Before giving birth (usually in May), the female excavates a den to house her litter of four to seven kits. They are placental, with a gestation period of about 66 days.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>When born, skunk kits are blind, deaf, and covered in a soft layer of fur. About three weeks after birth, their eyes open. The kits are weaned about two months after birth, but generally stay with their mother until they are ready to mate, at about one year of age. </p>
      <p>The mother is protective of her kits, spraying at any sign of danger. The male plays no part in raising the young.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anal_scent_glands">Anal scent glands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Anal scent glands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Skunk_about_to_spray.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Skunk_about_to_spray.jpg/220px-Skunk_about_to_spray.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="239" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Skunk_about_to_spray.jpg/330px-Skunk_about_to_spray.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Skunk_about_to_spray.jpg/440px-Skunk_about_to_spray.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1766" data-file-height="1920"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Skunk_about_to_spray.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>Striped skunk (
         <i>Mephitis mephitis</i>) in defensive posture with erect and puffed tail, indicating that it may be about to spray.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>Skunks are notorious for their anal scent glands, which they can use as a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_defense" title="Chemical defense">defensive weapon</a>. They are similar to, though much more developed than, the glands found in species of the family <a href="/wiki/Mustelidae" title="Mustelidae">Mustelidae</a>. Skunks have two glands, one on each side of the <a href="/wiki/Anus" title="Anus">anus</a>. These glands produce the skunk's spray, which is a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>-containing chemicals such as <a href="/wiki/Thiol" title="Thiol">thiols</a> (traditionally called mercaptans), which have an offensive odor. A skunk's spray is powerful enough to ward off <a href="/wiki/Bear" title="Bear">bears</a> and other potential attackers.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Muscles" class="mw-redirect" title="Muscles">Muscles</a> located next to the scent glands allow them to <a href="/wiki/Spraying_(animal_behavior)" class="mw-redirect" title="Spraying (animal behavior)">spray</a> with a high degree of accuracy, as far as 3&nbsp;m (10&nbsp;ft). The spray can also cause irritation and even temporary blindness, and is sufficiently powerful to be detected by a human nose up to 5.6 km (3.5 miles) downwind.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Their chemical defense is effective, as illustrated by this extract from <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Voyage_of_the_Beagle" title="The Voyage of the Beagle">Voyage of the Beagle</a></i>: </p> 
      <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036">
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>We saw also a couple of <i>Zorrillos</i>, or skunks—odious animals, which are far from uncommon. In general appearance, the <i>Zorrillo</i> resembles a polecat, but it is rather larger and much thicker in proportion. Conscious of its power, it roams by day about the open plain, and fears neither dog nor man. If a dog is urged to the attack, its courage is instantly checked by a few drops of the fetid oil, which brings on violent sickness and running at the nose. Whatever is once polluted by it, is for ever useless. <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9lix_de_Azara" title="Félix de Azara">Azara</a> says the smell can be perceived at a league distant; more than once, when entering the harbour of Monte Video, the wind being off shore, we have perceived the odour on board the <i>Beagle</i>. Certain it is, that every animal most willingly makes room for the <i>Zorrillo</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> </p>
      </blockquote> 
      <p>Skunks are reluctant to use this weapon, as they carry just enough of the chemical for five or six uses&nbsp;– about 15&nbsp;cc&nbsp;– and require some ten days to produce another supply.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Their bold black and white <a href="/wiki/Animal_coloration" title="Animal coloration">coloration</a> makes their appearance memorable. It is to a skunk's advantage to warn possible <a href="/wiki/Predator" class="mw-redirect" title="Predator">predators</a> off without expending scent: black and white <a href="/wiki/Aposematic" class="mw-redirect" title="Aposematic">aposematic</a> warning coloration aside, threatened skunks will go through an elaborate routine of hisses, foot-stamping, and tail-high <a href="/wiki/Deimatic_behaviour" title="Deimatic behaviour">deimatic</a> or threat postures before resorting to spraying. Skunks usually do not spray other skunks, except among males in the mating season. If they fight over den space in autumn, they do so with teeth and claws.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>Most <a href="/wiki/Predator" class="mw-redirect" title="Predator">predators</a> of the Americas, such as <a href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf">wolves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">foxes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Badger_(animal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Badger (animal)">badgers</a>, seldom attack skunks, presumably out of fear of being sprayed. The exceptions are reckless predators whose attacks fail once they are sprayed, dogs, and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Horned_Owl" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Horned Owl">great horned owl</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> which is the skunk's only regular predator.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-16">[16]</a></sup> In one case, the remains of 57 <a href="/wiki/Striped_skunk" title="Striped skunk">striped skunks</a> were found in a single owl nest.<sup id="cite_ref-Hunter_Carnivores_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter_Carnivores-17">[17]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Skunks are common in suburban areas. Frequent encounters with dogs and other domestic animals, and the release of the odor when a skunk is <a href="/wiki/Roadkill" title="Roadkill">run over</a>, have led to many myths about the removal of skunk odor. Due to the chemical composition of the spray, most of these household remedies are ineffective.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/The_Humane_Society_of_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="The Humane Society of the United States">The Humane Society of the United States</a> recommends treating dogs using a mixture of dilute hydrogen peroxide (3%), baking soda, and dishwashing liquid.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Skunk spray is composed mainly of three low-molecular-weight <a href="/wiki/Thiol" title="Thiol">thiol</a> compounds, (<i>E</i>)-2-butene-1-thiol, 3-methyl-1-butanethiol, and 2-quinolinemethanethiol, as well as <a href="/wiki/Acetate" title="Acetate">acetate</a> <a href="/wiki/Thioester" title="Thioester">thioesters</a> of these.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> These compounds are detectable by the human nose at concentrations of only 10 parts per billion.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> </p> 
      <div class="center">
       <div class="floatnone">
        <a href="/wiki/File:SkunkMuskChem.svg" class="image"><img alt="SkunkMuskChem.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/SkunkMuskChem.svg/500px-SkunkMuskChem.svg.png" decoding="async" width="500" height="106" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/SkunkMuskChem.svg/750px-SkunkMuskChem.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/SkunkMuskChem.svg/1000px-SkunkMuskChem.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1077" data-file-height="229"></a>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bites">Bites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Bites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>It is rare for a healthy skunk to bite a human. While a tame skunk with its scent glands removed may defend itself by biting, there are few recorded incidents. The most prevalent cause of skunks biting humans is the <a href="/wiki/Rabies" title="Rabies">rabies</a> virus. The <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention" title="Centers for Disease Control and Prevention">Centers for Disease Control (CDC)</a> recorded 1,494 cases of rabies in skunks in the United States for the year 2006—about 21.5% of reported cases in all species.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Skunks trail <a href="/wiki/Raccoon" title="Raccoon">raccoons</a> as <a href="/wiki/Vector_(epidemiology)" title="Vector (epidemiology)">vectors</a> of rabies, although this varies regionally in the United States; <a href="/wiki/Raccoon" title="Raccoon">raccoons</a> dominate along the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Northeast" title="Atlantic Northeast">Atlantic coast</a> and eastern <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="Gulf Coast of the United States">Gulf of Mexico</a>, skunks throughout the <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwest</a> and down to the western <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="Gulf Coast of the United States">Gulf</a>, and in California. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_pets">As pets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: As pets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

      <p><i>Mephitis mephitis</i>, the striped skunk, is the most social skunk and the one most commonly <a href="/wiki/Pet_skunk" class="mw-redirect" title="Pet skunk"> kept as a pet</a>. In the US, skunks can legally be kept as pets in seventeen states.<sup id="cite_ref-PBS_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PBS-29">[29]</a></sup> When a skunk is kept as a pet, its scent glands are often surgically removed.<sup id="cite_ref-PBS_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PBS-29">[29]</a></sup> In the UK, skunks can be kept as pets,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> but the <a href="/wiki/Animal_Welfare_Act_2006" title="Animal Welfare Act 2006">Animal Welfare Act 2006</a> made it illegal to remove their scent glands.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> </p>
