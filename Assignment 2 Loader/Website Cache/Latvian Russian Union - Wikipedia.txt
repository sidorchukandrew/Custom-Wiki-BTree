      <p>The <b>Latvian Russian Union</b> (<a href="/wiki/Latvian_language" title="Latvian language">Latvian</a>: <i lang="lv">Latvijas Krievu savienība</i>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Русский союз Латвии</span>) (LKS) is an ethnic minority, <a href="/wiki/Left-wing" class="mw-redirect" title="Left-wing">left-wing</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_Latvia" title="List of political parties in Latvia">political party</a> in <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a>, supported mainly by ethnic <a href="/wiki/Russians" title="Russians">Russians</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Latvia#Historical_shifts" title="Demographics of Latvia">Russian-speaking minorities</a>. The co-chairpersons of the Latvian Russian Union are <a href="/wiki/Miroslav_Mitrofanov" title="Miroslav Mitrofanov">Miroslav Mitrofanov</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuriy_Petropavlovsky&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Yuriy Petropavlovsky (page does not exist)">Yuriy Petropavlovsky</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tatjana_%C5%BDdanoka" title="Tatjana Ždanoka">Tatjana Ždanoka</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-leaders_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leaders-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The party emphasizes issues important to the <a href="/wiki/Russians_in_Latvia" title="Russians in Latvia">Russian minority in Latvia</a>. It requests the granting of <a href="/wiki/Latvian_nationality_law" title="Latvian nationality law">Latvian citizenship</a> to all of Latvia's remaining <a href="/wiki/Non-citizens_(Latvia)" title="Non-citizens (Latvia)">non-citizens</a> and supports Russian and <a href="/wiki/Latgalian_language" title="Latgalian language">Latgalian</a> as <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">co-official languages</a> in municipalities where at least 20% of the population are native speakers of such a language. It supports stronger ties with <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and was the only major political organization to oppose Latvia's membership in <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>. The Latvian Russian Union is very <a href="/wiki/Social_conservatism" title="Social conservatism">socially conservative</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> but left-wing on economic issues, though emphasis is mostly on social issues. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latvian_Russian_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The party originated as the <a href="/wiki/Electoral_alliance" title="Electoral alliance">electoral alliance</a> <b>For Human Rights in a United Latvia</b> (ForHRUL) (<a href="/wiki/Latvian_language" title="Latvian language">Latvian</a>: <i lang="lv">Par cilvēka tiesībām vienotā Latvijā</i>, <b>PCTVL</b>; <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">За права человека в единой Латвии</span>, <b>ЗаПЧЕЛ</b>) that was established in May 1998 by three political parties: the <a href="/wiki/National_Harmony_Party" title="National Harmony Party">National Harmony Party</a>, <a href="/wiki/Equal_Rights_(Latvia)" title="Equal Rights (Latvia)">Equal Rights</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_of_Latvia" title="Socialist Party of Latvia">Socialist Party of Latvia</a>, all of which were mainly supported by <a href="/wiki/Russophone" class="mw-redirect" title="Russophone">Russophone</a> voters. The alliance won 16 out of 100 seats in the <a href="/wiki/1998_Latvian_parliamentary_election" title="1998 Latvian parliamentary election">1998 parliamentary election</a> and 25 seats in the <a href="/wiki/2002_Latvian_parliamentary_election" title="2002 Latvian parliamentary election">2002 parliamentary election</a>, as well as 13 out of 60 seats on <a href="/wiki/Riga_City_Council" title="Riga City Council">Riga City Council</a> in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=2001_Latvian_municipal_elections&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="2001 Latvian municipal elections (page does not exist)">2001 municipal elections</a>. After the municipal elections, ForHRUL became part of Riga's city government and National Harmony Party member <a href="/wiki/Sergey_Dolgopolov" title="Sergey Dolgopolov">Sergey Dolgopolov</a> became the <a href="/wiki/Deputy_mayor" title="Deputy mayor">deputy mayor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Riga_City_Council" title="Riga City Council">Riga City Council</a>. </p>
      <p>During this period, ForHRUL's most prominent leaders were <a href="/wiki/J%C4%81nis_Jurk%C4%81ns" title="Jānis Jurkāns">Jānis Jurkāns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alfr%C4%93ds_Rubiks" title="Alfrēds Rubiks">Alfrēds Rubiks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tatjana_%C5%BDdanoka" title="Tatjana Ždanoka">Tatjana Ždanoka</a>. Jurkāns was a leader of the <a href="/wiki/Popular_Front_of_Latvia" title="Popular Front of Latvia">Popular Front of Latvia</a> and founder of the National Harmony Party; Rubiks and Ždanoka were prominent as leaders of the <a href="/wiki/International_Front_of_the_Working_People_of_Latvia" title="International Front of the Working People of Latvia">Interfront</a> movement, the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Latvia" title="Communist Party of Latvia">Latvian branch</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Communist Party of the Soviet Union</a> and the federalist movement in Latvia in the early 1990s. They are fairly popular in the Russian community but very unpopular among ethnic <a href="/wiki/Latvians" title="Latvians">Latvians</a>. ForHRUL therefore remained in opposition, because other parties would have faced a backlash from ethnic Latvian voters if they entered into a coalition with Rubiks or Ždanoka. </p>
      <p>ForHRUL partially broke up in 2003. The National Harmony Party was the first to leave the alliance and the Socialist Party followed half a year later. The remnant of ForHRUL consisted of <a href="/wiki/Equal_Rights_(Latvia)" title="Equal Rights (Latvia)">Equal Rights</a> and <b><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_Choice_in_Peoples%27_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Free Choice in Peoples' Europe (page does not exist)">Free Choice in Peoples' Europe</a></b> (<a href="/wiki/Latvian_language" title="Latvian language">Latvian</a>: <i lang="lv">Brīvā izvēle tautu Eiropā</i>, BITE). The latter was composed of dissident Socialist Party and National Harmony Party members, like <a href="/wiki/Yakov_Pliner" title="Yakov Pliner">Yakov Pliner</a>, who opposed the decision to quit the alliance. This reduced grouping had only 6 members of the <a href="/wiki/Saeima" title="Saeima">Saeima</a> (out of 25 that the alliance had before the breakup). ForHRUL was the main force supporting <a href="/wiki/Russian_School_Defense_Staff" title="Russian School Defense Staff">Russian School Defense Staff</a> activities from 2003-2005. </p>
      <p>At the <a href="/wiki/2004_European_Parliament_election_in_Latvia" title="2004 European Parliament election in Latvia">first Latvian election to the European Parliament</a> in 2004, ForHRUL gained one seat, held by Tatjana Ždanoka, who sat with <a href="/wiki/The_Greens%E2%80%93European_Free_Alliance" class="mw-redirect" title="The Greens–European Free Alliance">the Greens–European Free Alliance</a> group in the European Parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It also proposed the idea of a Europe-wide party of ethnic Russians. ForHRUL supported a federal Europe, with a "common economic and political space from <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a> to <a href="/wiki/Vladivostok" title="Vladivostok">Vladivostok</a>". </p>
      <p>In 2007, ForHRUL was transformed into a single party that retained the name and identity of the old electoral alliance. In recent years the party's support has declined as ethnic Russian voters have switched allegiance to the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_%22Harmony%22" title="Social Democratic Party &quot;Harmony&quot;">Harmony</a> party, successor to the National Harmony Party. At the <a href="/wiki/2010_Latvian_parliamentary_election" title="2010 Latvian parliamentary election">2010 parliamentary election</a>, the party lost its representation in the Latvian Parliament. </p>
      <p>In 2011, the party launched an unsuccessful <a href="/wiki/Amendments_to_the_Citizenship_Law_(popular_initiative,_Latvia)" title="Amendments to the Citizenship Law (popular initiative, Latvia)">popular initiative</a> on amending the <a href="/wiki/Latvian_nationality_law" title="Latvian nationality law">law governing Latvian nationality</a>. The Central Electoral Commission considered the proposed amendment to be incompatible with the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Latvia" title="Constitution of Latvia">Constitution of Latvia</a> and the process of collecting signatures for a <a href="/wiki/Referendum" title="Referendum">referendum</a> on the proposals was suspended. This decision was eventually upheld by the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Court_of_Latvia" title="Constitutional Court of Latvia">Constitutional Court of Latvia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Latvia" title="Supreme Court of Latvia">Supreme Court of Latvia</a>. It also supported the <a href="/wiki/2012_Latvian_constitutional_referendum" title="2012 Latvian constitutional referendum">2012 initiative</a> to make Russian a co-official language in Latvia. </p>
      <p>In January 2014, ForHRUL changed its name to the <b>Latvian Russian Union</b>. At the <a href="/wiki/2014_European_Parliament_election_in_Latvia" title="2014 European Parliament election in Latvia">2014 European Parliament election</a>, it retained its single seat in the European Parliament. The party supported the <a href="/wiki/Annexation_of_Crimea_by_the_Russian_Federation" title="Annexation of Crimea by the Russian Federation">annexation of Crimea</a> by Russia in 2014 and has taken a pro-Russian stance in the subsequent <a href="/wiki/War_in_Donbass" title="War in Donbass">War in Donbass</a>. In August 2014 the party signed a cooperation agreement with the Crimean branch of <a href="/wiki/Russian_Unity" title="Russian Unity">Russian Unity</a> to "strengthen the unity of <a href="/wiki/Russian_World" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian World">Russian world</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In July 2018, Ždanoka resigned her mandate in the European Parliament to focus on the <a href="/wiki/2018_Latvian_parliamentary_election" title="2018 Latvian parliamentary election">2018 Latvian parliamentary election</a> and was succeed by <a href="/wiki/Miroslav_Mitrofanov" title="Miroslav Mitrofanov">Miroslav Mitrofanov</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LSM_2018-08-13_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LSM_2018-08-13-8">[8]</a></sup> With <a href="/wiki/Andrejs_Mamikins" title="Andrejs Mamikins">Andrejs Mamikins</a> as their prime minister candidate<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Latvian Russian Union gained 3.2% votes, failing to win any seats in <a href="/wiki/Saeima" title="Saeima">Saeima</a>, but qualifying for state funding of almost 20 000 euros a year<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> that the party will not be receiving since it does not possess an account in a credit institution registered in Latvia as required by the Law on Financing of Political Organizations.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Ždanoka blamed the poor election results on the party's "very modest" budget, but said self-liquidating is not on their agenda, assuring that the Latvian Russian Union "will continue working as usual". She also called Mamikins' Facebook following announcement on quitting politics "typical fake news, created to probe people’s reaction".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> </p>
