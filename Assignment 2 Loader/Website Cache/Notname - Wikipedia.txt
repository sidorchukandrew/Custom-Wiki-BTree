      <p>In <a href="/wiki/Art_history" title="Art history">art history</a>, a <i><b>notname</b></i> (<small>German: </small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈnoːtˌnaːmə]</a></span> <span class="nowrap" style="font-size:85%">(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap;margin-right:.25em;"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Notname.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" decoding="async" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20"></a></span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d1/De-Notname.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Notname.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</span>, "necessity-name" or "contingency-name") is an invented name given to an artist whose identity has been lost. The practice arose from the need to give such artists and their typically untitled, or generically titled works, an acceptable if unsatisfactory grouping, avoiding confusion when cataloging. The phrases <b>provisional name</b>, <b>name of convenience</b> and <b>emergency names</b> are sometimes used to describe anonymous masters; <b>nonce name</b> was at one time used. </p>
      <p>The practice of using generic names for unidentified artists is most common in the study of art of the antiquity, especially of the Roman era or with artists of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Renaissance" title="Northern Renaissance">Northern Renaissance</a> until about 1430. Typically a pseudonym is applied after commonality is established for a grouping of works, of which a similarity of theme, style, <a href="/wiki/Iconography" title="Iconography">iconography</a>, biblical source or physical location can probably be attributed to one individual or workshop, but because of lack of surviving documentary record, the name of that individual is lost. Groupings of works under a given notname can often be contentious; in specific cases art historians have argued that the reality may be a group or school of artists working under a common influence or commercial demand. Linking a generically titled old master with a historical person is usually a tempting and exciting prospect, and would establish an art historian's reputation. </p>
      <p>The given notname usually depends on the artist's location, the most distinctive feature of their work, or the theme or iconographic element they are best associated with. Some notnames created based on a single artwork, called <a href="/wiki/Namepiece" title="Namepiece">namepiece</a>. Well known examples include the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_the_Embroidered_Foliage" title="Master of the Embroidered Foliage">Master of the Embroidered Foliage</a> (active c. 1480 to c. 1510) so named after his distinctive way of painting grass and trees,<sup id="cite_ref-n22_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-n22-1">[1]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_the_Life_of_the_Virgin" title="Master of the Life of the Virgin">Master of the Life of the Virgin</a> (active c. 1463 to c. 1490) and the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_the_Legend_of_the_Magdalen" title="Master of the Legend of the Magdalen">Master of the Legend of the Magdalen</a> (active c. 1483 – c. 1527) both named after scenes from the <a href="/wiki/Life_of_the_Virgin" title="Life of the Virgin">Life of the Virgin</a> attributed to them, the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_the_Prado_Adoration_of_the_Magi" title="Master of the Prado Adoration of the Magi">Master of the Prado Adoration of the Magi</a> (active c. 1475 – 1500) named after his most famous panel, and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vienna_Master_of_Mary_of_Burgundy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Vienna Master of Mary of Burgundy (page does not exist)">Vienna Master of Mary of Burgundy</a> (c 1470 – c 1480), named after a manuscript owned by one of his patrons.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Painter" title="Berlin Painter">Berlin Painter</a> (active c. 490s-c. 460s BCE) was named by Sir <a href="/wiki/John_Beazley" title="John Beazley">John Beazley</a> for a large lidded <a href="/wiki/Amphora" title="Amphora">amphora</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Antikensammlung_Berlin" title="Antikensammlung Berlin">Antikensammlung Berlin</a>, the Berlin Painter's <i>namepiece</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the case of 14th and early 15th-century <a href="/wiki/Early_Netherlandish_painting" title="Early Netherlandish painting">Netherlandish</a>, French and German painters and illuminators, the problem is particularly acute and stems from a number of factors. Primarily, the practice of signing and dating works is rarely seen in the region until the 1420s,<sup id="cite_ref-n123_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-n123-5">[5]</a></sup> and the inventories of collectors were uninterested in the artist's names.<sup id="cite_ref-n44_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-n44-6">[6]</a></sup> Many of the unidentified late 14th and early 15th-century northern artists were of the first rank, but because they have not been attached to any historical person, have suffered from academic neglect. It is probably a truism to say that, as <a href="/wiki/Susie_Nash" title="Susie Nash">Susie Nash</a> put it, "much of what cannot be firmly attributed remains less studied".<sup id="cite_ref-n22_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-n22-1">[1]</a></sup> Some art historians believe that this has led to a lack of caution in connecting works with historical persons, and that such connections often hang on thin threads of circumstantial evidence. The identities of a number of well-known artists have been founded on the basis of a single signed, documented or otherwise attributed work, with similar works sharing close style or within a geographical range also attached to that name. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Hugo_van_der_Goes" title="Hugo van der Goes">Hugo van der Goes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Campin" title="Robert Campin">Robert Campin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Lochner" title="Stefan Lochner">Stefan Lochner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Simon_Marmion" title="Simon Marmion">Simon Marmion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> </p>
