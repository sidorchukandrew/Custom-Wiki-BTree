      <p>The <b>northern elephant seal</b> (<i>Mirounga angustirostris</i>) is one of two <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Elephant_seal" title="Elephant seal">elephant seal</a> (the other is the <a href="/wiki/Southern_elephant_seal" title="Southern elephant seal">southern elephant seal</a>). It is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Phocidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Phocidae">Phocidae</a> (true <a href="/wiki/Seal_(mammal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Seal (mammal)">seals</a>). Elephant seals derive their name from their great size and from the male's large <a href="/wiki/Proboscis" title="Proboscis">proboscis</a>, which is used in making extraordinarily loud roaring noises, especially during the mating competition. <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">Sexual dimorphism</a> in size is great. Correspondingly, the mating system is highly <a href="/wiki/Polygynous" class="mw-redirect" title="Polygynous">polygynous</a>; a successful male is able to impregnate up to 50 females in one season. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_elephant_seal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

      <p>The huge male northern elephant seal typically weighs 1,500–2,300&nbsp;kg (3,300–5,100&nbsp;lb) and measures 4–5&nbsp;m (13–16&nbsp;ft), although some males can weigh up to 3,700&nbsp;kg (8,200&nbsp;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Females are much smaller and can range from 400 to 900&nbsp;kg (880 to 1,980&nbsp;lb) in weight, or roughly a third of the male's bulk, and measure from 2.5 to 3.6&nbsp;m (8.2 to 11.8&nbsp;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The bull southern elephant seals are, on average, larger than those in the northern species, but the females in both are around the same size, indicating the even higher level of sexual dimorphism in the southern species.<sup id="cite_ref-Walker_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walker-5">[5]</a></sup> Northern elephant seals typically live for around 9 years.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Both adult and juvenile elephant seals are bar-skinned<sup class="noprint selfreference">[<i><a href="/wiki/Talk:Northern_elephant_seal" title="Talk:Northern elephant seal">discuss</a></i>]</sup> and black before molting. After molting, they generally have a silver to dark gray coat that fades to brownish-yellow and tan. Adult males have hairless necks and chests speckled with pink, white, and light brown. Pups are mostly black at birth and molt to a silver gray after weaning.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>The eyes are large, round, and black. The width of the eyes and a high concentration of low-light pigments suggest sight plays an important role in the capture of prey. Like all seals, elephant seals have atrophied hind limbs whose underdeveloped ends form the tail and tail fin. Each of the "feet" can deploy five long, webbed fingers. This agile, dual palm is used to propel water. The pectoral fins are used little while swimming.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> While their hind limbs are unfit for locomotion on land, elephant seals use their fins as support to propel their bodies. They are able to propel themselves quickly (as fast as 8&nbsp;km/h) in this way for short-distance travel, to return to water, catch up with a female or chase an intruder.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>Like other seals, elephant seals' bloodstreams are adapted to the cold in which a mixture of small veins surrounds arteries capturing heat from them. This structure is present in extremities such as the hindlimbs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>A unique characteristic of the northern elephant seal is that it has developed the ability to store oxygenated red blood cells within its spleen. In a 2004 study researchers used <a href="/wiki/MRI" class="mw-redirect" title="MRI">MRI</a> to observe physiological changes of the spleens of 5 seal pups during simulated dives. By 3 minutes, the spleens on average contracted to a fifth of their original size, indicating a dive-related sympathetic contraction of the spleen. Also, a delay was observed between contraction of the spleen and increased hematocrit within the circulating blood, and attributed to the hepatic sinus. This fluid-filled structure is initially expanded due to the rush of RBC from the spleen and slowly releases the red blood cells into the circulatory system via a muscular vena caval sphincter found on the cranial aspect of the diaphragm. This ability to slowly introduce RBC into the blood stream is likely to prevent any harmful effects caused by a rapid increase in hematocrit.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> </p>

      </div>
      <p>The northern elephant seal lives in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>. They spend most of their time at sea, and usually only come to land to give birth, breed, and molt. These activities occur at rookeries that are located on offshore islands or remote mainland beaches. The majority of these rookeries are in California and northern Baja California, ranging from <a href="/wiki/Point_Reyes" title="Point Reyes">Point Reyes National Seashore</a>, California to Isla Natividad, Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Significant breeding colonies exist at <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands_of_California" class="mw-redirect" title="Channel Islands of California">Channel Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/A%C3%B1o_Nuevo_State_Reserve" class="mw-redirect" title="Año Nuevo State Reserve">Año Nuevo State Reserve</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piedras_Blancas_Light" class="mw-redirect" title="Piedras Blancas Light">Piedras Blancas Light</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morro_Bay_State_Park" title="Morro Bay State Park">Morro Bay State Park</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Farallon_Islands" title="Farallon Islands">Farallon Islands</a> in the US,<sup id="cite_ref-stock_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stock-9">[9]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Isla_Guadalupe" class="mw-redirect" title="Isla Guadalupe">Isla Guadalupe</a>, Isla Benito del Este and <a href="/wiki/Isla_Cedros" class="mw-redirect" title="Isla Cedros">Isla Cedros</a> in Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-stock_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stock-9">[9]</a></sup> In recent decades the breeding range has extended northwards. In 1976 the first pup was found on <a href="/wiki/Point_Reyes" title="Point Reyes">Point Reyes</a> and a breeding colony established there in 1981.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Since the mid-1990s some breeding has been observed at <a href="/wiki/Castle_Rock_National_Wildlife_Refuge" title="Castle Rock National Wildlife Refuge">Castle Rock</a> in Northern California and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Arago_State_Park" title="Cape Arago State Park">Shell Island</a> off Oregon,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and in January 2009 the first elephant seal births were recorded in British Columbia at <a href="/wiki/Race_Rocks_Marine_Protected_Area" title="Race Rocks Marine Protected Area">Race Rocks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The California breeding population is now demographically isolated from the population in Baja California.<sup id="cite_ref-stock_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stock-9">[9]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Northern elephant seals exhibit extreme sexual dimorphism in their feeding behaviours. When the males leave their rookeries, they migrate northwards to their feeding grounds along the continental shelf from Washington to the western Aleutians in Alaska.<sup id="cite_ref-Ecology_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecology-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Feeding_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Feeding-14">[14]</a></sup> The males mostly feed on benthic organisms on the ocean floor.<sup id="cite_ref-Ecology_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecology-13">[13]</a></sup> When the females leave their rookeries, they head north or west into open ocean, and forage across a large area in the northeastern Pacific.<sup id="cite_ref-Ecology_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecology-13">[13]</a></sup> They have been recorded as far west as <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ecology_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecology-13">[13]</a></sup> Female elephant seals feed mainly on pelagic organisms in the water column.<sup id="cite_ref-Ecology_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecology-13">[13]</a></sup> </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">Vagrant</a> elephant seals possibly appear on tropical regions such as at <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Islands" title="Mariana Islands">Mariana Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Historical occurrences of elephant seal presence, residential or occasional, in western North Pacific are fairly unknown. There have been two records of vagrants visiting to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> coasts; a male on <a href="/wiki/Niijima" class="mw-redirect" title="Niijima">Niijima</a> in 1989<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> (reference introducing a visit by a <a href="/wiki/North_Pacific_right_whale" title="North Pacific right whale">North Pacific right whale</a> in 2011), and a young seal on beaches in Hasama, <a href="/wiki/Tateyama,_Chiba" title="Tateyama, Chiba">Tateyama</a> in 2001<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> (where another right whale was seen in 2000). A 2.5 meter female was found on Sanze beach, <a href="/wiki/Tsuruoka,_Yamagata" title="Tsuruoka, Yamagata">Tsuruoka, Yamagata</a> in October 2017, making it the first record from <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Japan" title="Sea of Japan">Sea of Japan</a>. This individual was severely weakened but showing signs of recovery after receiving medications at Kamo Aquarium, and the aquarium is discussing whether or not to release her.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Some individuals have been observed on the coast of northeast Asia. Certain individuals established haul-out sites at the <a href="/wiki/Commander_Islands" title="Commander Islands">Commander Islands</a> in the early 2000s; however, due to aggressive interactions with local <a href="/wiki/Steller_sea_lion" title="Steller sea lion">Steller sea lions</a>, long-term colonization is not expected.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>Female elephant seals forage in the open ocean, while male elephant seals forage along the continental shelf.<sup id="cite_ref-Ecology_13-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecology-13">[13]</a></sup> Males usually dive straight down to the ocean floor and stay at the bottom foraging for benthic prey.<sup id="cite_ref-Ecology_13-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecology-13">[13]</a></sup> The females hunt for pelagic prey in the open ocean, and dive deeper (up to 1700 m, though on average about 500 m) and stay down longer than the males.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ecology_13-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecology-13">[13]</a></sup> Northern elephant seals eat a variety of prey, including <a href="/wiki/Mesopelagic_zone" title="Mesopelagic zone">mesopelagic</a> fish such as <a href="/wiki/Lanternfish" title="Lanternfish">myctophids</a>, deep-water squid, <a href="/wiki/North_Pacific_hake" title="North Pacific hake">Pacific hake</a>, pelagic crustaceans, sharks, rays, and <a href="/wiki/Chimaera" title="Chimaera">ratfish</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Feeding_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Feeding-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Stomach_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stomach-23">[23]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Octopoteuthis_deletron" title="Octopoteuthis deletron">Octopoteuthis deletron</a></i> squid are a common prey item, one study found this species in the stomachs of 58% of individuals sampled off the coast of California.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> A female northern elephant seal was documented in 2013 by a deep sea camera at a depth of 894&nbsp;m (2,933&nbsp;ft), where it consumed a <a href="/wiki/Pacific_hagfish" title="Pacific hagfish">Pacific hagfish</a>, slurping it up from the ocean floor. The event was reported by a Ukrainian boy named Kirill Dudko, who further reported the find to scientists in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Elephant seals do not need to drink, as they get their water from food and metabolism of fats. </p>
      <p>While hunting in the dark depths, elephant seals seem to locate their prey at least partly by vision; the <a href="/wiki/Bioluminescence" title="Bioluminescence">bioluminescence</a> of some prey animals can facilitate their capture. Elephant seals do not have a developed a system of <a href="/wiki/Animal_echolocation" title="Animal echolocation">echolocation</a> in the manner of cetaceans, but their <a href="/wiki/Vibrissae" class="mw-redirect" title="Vibrissae">vibrissae</a>, which are sensitive to vibrations, are assumed to play a role in search of food. Males and females differ in diving behavior. Males tend to hug the continental shelf while making deep dives and forage along the bottom,<sup id="cite_ref-Ecology_13-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecology-13">[13]</a></sup> while females have more jagged routes and forage in the open ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-Ecology_13-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecology-13">[13]</a></sup> Elephant seals are prey for <a href="/wiki/Orca" class="mw-redirect" title="Orca">orcas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_white_sharks" class="mw-redirect" title="Great white sharks">great white sharks</a>. Both are most likely to hunt pups, and seldom hunt large bull elephant seals, but have taken seals of all ages. The shark, when hunting adults, is most likely to ambush a seal with a damaging bite and wait until it is weakened by blood loss to finish the kill.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>Northern elephant seals return to their terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">breeding ground</a> in December and January, with the bulls arriving first. The bulls haul out on isolated or otherwise protected beaches, typically on islands or very remote mainland locations. It is important that these beach areas offer protection from the winter storms and high surf wave action.<sup id="cite_ref-Pup_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pup-27">[27]</a></sup> The bulls engage in fights of supremacy to determine which few bulls will achieve a harem.<sup id="cite_ref-Sexual_behavior_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sexual_behavior-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-competition_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-competition-29">[29]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>After the males have arrived to the beach, the females arrive to give birth. Females fast for five weeks and nurse their single pup for four weeks; in the last few days of lactation, females come into estrus and mate.<sup id="cite_ref-Natal_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Natal-30">[30]</a></sup> In this <a href="/wiki/Polygynous" class="mw-redirect" title="Polygynous">polygynous</a> society, a high-ranking bull can have a <a href="/wiki/Harem_(zoology)" title="Harem (zoology)">harem</a> of 30–100 cows, depending on his size and strength. Males unable to establish harems will wait on the periphery, and will try to mount nearby females.<sup id="cite_ref-Sexual_behavior_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sexual_behavior-28">[28]</a></sup> Dominant bulls will disrupt copulations of lower-ranking bulls.<sup id="cite_ref-Sexual_behavior_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sexual_behavior-28">[28]</a></sup> They can mount females without interference, but commonly break off to chase off rivals.<sup id="cite_ref-Sexual_behavior_28-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sexual_behavior-28">[28]</a></sup> While fights are not usually to the death, they are brutal and often with significant bloodshed and injury; however, in many cases of mismatched opponents, the younger, less capable males are simply chased away, often to upland dunes. In a lifetime, a successful bull could easily sire over 500 pups. Most copulations in a breeding colony are done by only a small number of males and the rest may never be able to mate with a female.<sup id="cite_ref-competition_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-competition-29">[29]</a></sup> Pups are sometimes crushed during battles between bulls.<sup id="cite_ref-Pup_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pup-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-competition_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-competition-29">[29]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>After arrival on shore, males fast for three months, and females fast for five weeks during mating and when nursing their pups. The gestation period is about 11 months. Sometimes, a female can become very aggressive after giving birth and will defend her pup from other females.<sup id="cite_ref-birth_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birth-31">[31]</a></sup> Such aggression is more common in crowded beaches.<sup id="cite_ref-birth_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birth-31">[31]</a></sup> While most females nurse their own pups and reject nursings from alien pups, some do accept alien pups with their own.<sup id="cite_ref-Pup_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pup-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Natal_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Natal-30">[30]</a></sup> An orphaned pup may try to find another female to suckle and some are adopted, at least on Año Nuevo Island.<sup id="cite_ref-Pup_27-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pup-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Natal_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Natal-30">[30]</a></sup> Pups nurse about four weeks and are weaned abruptly before being abandoned by their mother, who heads out to sea within a few days. Left alone, weaned pups will gather into groups and stay on shore for 12 more weeks. The pups learn how to swim in the surf and eventually swim farther to forage. Thus, their first long journey at sea begins. </p>
      <p>Elephant seals communicate though various means. Males will threaten each other with the snort, a sound caused by expelling air though their probosces, and the clap-trap, a loud, clapping sound comparable to the sound of a diesel engine.<sup id="cite_ref-species_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-species-32">[32]</a></sup> Pups will vocalize when stressed or when prodding their mothers to allow them to suckle. Females make an unpulsed attraction call when responding to their young, and a harsh, pulsed call when threatened by other females, males or alien pups.<sup id="cite_ref-species_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-species-32">[32]</a></sup> Elephant seals produce low-frequency sounds, both substrate-borne and air-borne. These sounds help maintain social hierarchy in crowded or noisy environments and reduce energy consumption when fasting.<sup id="cite_ref-species_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-species-32">[32]</a></sup> </p>

       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>Beginning in the 18th century, northern elephant seals were hunted extensively, almost to extinction by the end of the 19th century,<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup> being prized for oil made from their <a href="/wiki/Blubber" title="Blubber">blubber</a>, and the population may have fallen as low as only 20-40 individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1874, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Melville_Scammon" title="Charles Melville Scammon">Charles Melville Scammon</a> recorded in <i>Marine Mammals of the Northwestern Coast of America</i>, that an 18-ft-long bull caught on <a href="/wiki/Santa_Barbara_Island" title="Santa Barbara Island">Santa Barbara Island</a> yielded 210 gallons of oil.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> They were thought to be extinct in 1884 until a remnant population of eight individuals was discovered on <a href="/wiki/Guadalupe_Island" title="Guadalupe Island">Guadalupe Island</a> in 1892 by a <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">Smithsonian</a> expedition, who promptly killed seven of the eight for their collections.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The elephant seals managed to survive, and were finally protected by the Mexican government in 1922. Since the early 20th century, they have been protected by law in both Mexico and in the United States. Subsequently, the U.S. protection was strengthened after passage of the <a href="/wiki/Marine_Mammal_Protection_Act_of_1972" class="mw-redirect" title="Marine Mammal Protection Act of 1972">Marine Mammal Protection Act of 1972</a>, and numbers have now recovered to over 100,000.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>Nevertheless, a <a href="/wiki/Genetic_bottleneck" class="mw-redirect" title="Genetic bottleneck">genetic bottleneck</a> experienced by Northern elephant seals during nineteen century, could make them more susceptible to disease, environmental changes and pollution.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> This bottleneck caused a sharp loss of <a href="/wiki/Genetic_diversity" title="Genetic diversity">genetic diversity</a> and increased <a href="/wiki/Homozygosity" class="mw-redirect" title="Homozygosity">homozygosity</a> in the surviving population, and also a decreased number of <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup" title="Haplogroup">haplogroups</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In California, the population is continuing to grow at around 6% per year, and new colonies are being established; they are now probably limited mostly by the availability of <a href="/wiki/Hauling-out" title="Hauling-out">haul-out</a> space. Their breeding was probably restricted to islands, before large carnivores were exterminated or prevented from reaching the side of the ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Numbers can be adversely affected by <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a> events and the resultant weather conditions, and the 1997–98 El Niño may have caused the loss of about 80% of that year's pups. Presently, the northern elephant seal is protected under the federal Marine Mammal Protection Act and has a fully protected status under California law.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>Populations of rookery sites in California have increased during the past century.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/A%C3%B1o_Nuevo_State_Park" title="Año Nuevo State Park">Año Nuevo State Park</a>, for example, no individuals were observed whatsoever until 1955; the first pup born there was observed in the early 1960s. Currently, thousands of pups are born every year at Año Nuevo, on both the island and mainland. The growth of the site near San Simeon has proved even more spectacular; no animals were there prior to 1990. Currently, the San Simeon site hosts more breeding animals than Año Nuevo State Park during winter season.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
