      <p><b>Arthur Greenwood</b>, <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:100;"><a href="/wiki/Companion_of_Honour" class="mw-redirect" title="Companion of Honour">CH</a></span> (8 February 1880 – 9 June 1954) was a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> politician. A prominent member of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> from the 1920s until the late 1940s, Greenwood rose to prominence within the party as secretary of its research department from 1920 and served as <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Secretary_to_the_Ministry_of_Health" title="Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Health">Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Health</a> in the short-lived <a href="/wiki/First_MacDonald_ministry" title="First MacDonald ministry">Labour government</a> of 1924. In 1940, he was instrumental in resolving that Britain would continue fighting <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Greenwood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Greenwood was born in <a href="/wiki/Hunslet" title="Hunslet">Hunslet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, the son of a painter and decorator. He was educated at the Yorkshire College (which later became the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Leeds" title="University of Leeds">University of Leeds</a>), where he took a <a href="/wiki/B.Sc." class="mw-redirect" title="B.Sc.">B.Sc.</a> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_career">Political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Greenwood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Greenwood was first elected to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_(United_Kingdom)" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Commons (United Kingdom)">House of Commons</a> at the <a href="/wiki/1922_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="1922 United Kingdom general election">1922 general election</a> for the constituency of <a href="/wiki/Nelson_and_Colne_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Nelson and Colne (UK Parliament constituency)">Nelson and Colne</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>. He held the seat until being defeated at the <a href="/wiki/1931_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="1931 United Kingdom general election">1931 election</a>, but returned to Parliament the following year, winning <a href="/wiki/1932_Wakefield_by-election" title="1932 Wakefield by-election">a by-election</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> constituency of <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Wakefield (UK Parliament constituency)">Wakefield</a>. Greenwood continued to represent Wakefield until his death in 1954. Greenwood was an active <a href="/wiki/Freemason" class="mw-redirect" title="Freemason">freemason</a>, associated with the <a href="/wiki/New_Welcome_Lodge" title="New Welcome Lodge">New Welcome Lodge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1929, Greenwood was appointed <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Health" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State for Health">Minister of Health</a> and sworn into the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Privy Council of the United Kingdom">Privy Council</a>, a position he held until the collapse of the Labour government in August 1931. During his time at the Ministry of Health, Greenwood raised widows' pensions and through the <a href="/wiki/Housing_Act_1930" title="Housing Act 1930">Housing Act 1930</a> enacted large-scale <a href="/wiki/Slum_clearance" title="Slum clearance">slum clearance</a>. </p>
      <p>Greenwood became <a href="/wiki/Deputy_Leader_of_the_Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Deputy Leader of the Labour Party (UK)">Deputy Leader of the Labour Party</a> under <a href="/wiki/Clement_Attlee" title="Clement Attlee">Clement Attlee</a>. Undoubtedly his most famous moment came on 2 September 1939 when, acting for an absent Attlee, he was called to respond to <a href="/wiki/Neville_Chamberlain" title="Neville Chamberlain">Neville Chamberlain</a>'s ambivalent speech on whether Britain would aid Poland. Preparing to respond, he was interrupted by an angry Conservative backbencher and former <a href="/wiki/First_Lord_of_the_Admiralty" title="First Lord of the Admiralty">First Lord of the Admiralty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leo_Amery" title="Leo Amery">Leo Amery</a>, who exclaimed "Speak for England, Arthur!"<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>A flustered Greenwood proceeded to denounce Chamberlain's remarks, to the applause of his colleagues. When the wartime coalition government was formed, <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> appointed him to the <a href="/wiki/War_Cabinet" class="mw-redirect" title="War Cabinet">War Cabinet</a> as <a href="/wiki/Minister_without_Portfolio" class="mw-redirect" title="Minister without Portfolio">Minister without Portfolio</a> in 1940. He was generally seen as ineffectual, but in May 1940 he emerged as Churchill's strongest and most vocal supporter in <a href="/wiki/May_1940_War_Cabinet_Crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="May 1940 War Cabinet Crisis">the lengthy War Cabinet debates</a> on whether to accept or reject a peace offer from Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Without the vote in favour of fighting on by Greenwood and Clement Attlee, Churchill would not have had the slim majority he needed to do so.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>After that his position declined and he resigned in 1943. The same year, he was elected as <a href="/wiki/Treasurer_of_the_Labour_Party" title="Treasurer of the Labour Party">Treasurer of the Labour Party</a>, beating <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Morrison" title="Herbert Morrison">Herbert Morrison</a> in a close contest.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Until the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Greenwood also performed the function of <a href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_Opposition_(United_Kingdom)" title="Leader of the Opposition (United Kingdom)">Leader of the Opposition</a>, though he did not receive the salary. </p>
      <p>During the <a href="/wiki/Attlee_ministry" title="Attlee ministry">Attlee government</a>, he served successively as <a href="/wiki/Lord_Privy_Seal" title="Lord Privy Seal">Lord Privy Seal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paymaster-General" class="mw-redirect" title="Paymaster-General">Paymaster-General</a>. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Greenwood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Greenwood was cremated at <a href="/wiki/Golders_Green_Crematorium" title="Golders Green Crematorium">Golders Green Crematorium</a> on 14 June 1954. His ashes and memorial lie in Bay 17 of the East Boundary Wall.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Greenwood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Greenwood's son <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Greenwood,_Baron_Greenwood_of_Rossendale" class="mw-redirect" title="Anthony Greenwood, Baron Greenwood of Rossendale">Anthony Greenwood</a> (later Lord Greenwood) (1911–1982) was an MP from 1946 until 1970, first for <a href="/wiki/Heywood_and_Radcliffe_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Heywood and Radcliffe (UK Parliament constituency)">Heywood and Radcliffe</a> and later for <a href="/wiki/Rossendale_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Rossendale (UK Parliament constituency)">Rossendale</a>, and a member of <a href="/wiki/Harold_Wilson" title="Harold Wilson">Harold Wilson</a>'s governments. </p>
