   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Beginning of human personhood</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub" class="noprint">

      <p>The <b>beginning of human personhood</b> is the moment when a <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> is first recognized as a <a href="/wiki/Person" title="Person">person</a>. There are differences of opinion as to the precise time when human <a href="/wiki/Personhood" title="Personhood">personhood</a> begins and the nature of that status. The issue arises in a number of fields including science, religion, philosophy, and law, and is most acute in debates relating to <a href="/wiki/Abortion_debate" title="Abortion debate">abortion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stem_cell_controversy" title="Stem cell controversy">stem cell research</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_rights" title="Reproductive rights">reproductive rights</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fetal_rights" title="Fetal rights">fetal rights</a>. </p>
      <p>Traditionally, the concept of <a href="/wiki/Personhood" title="Personhood">personhood</a> has entailed the concept of <a href="/wiki/Soul_(spirit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Soul (spirit)">soul</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysical</a> concept referring to a non-corporeal or extra-corporeal dimension of <a href="/wiki/Human_being" class="mw-redirect" title="Human being">human being</a>. However, in <a href="/wiki/Modernity" title="Modernity">modernity</a>, the concepts of <a href="/wiki/Subjectivity" title="Subjectivity">subjectivity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Intersubjectivity" title="Intersubjectivity">intersubjectivity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Personhood" title="Personhood">personhood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mind" title="Mind">mind</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Self" title="Self">self</a> have come to encompass a number of aspects of human being previously considered to be characteristics of the <a href="/wiki/Soul" title="Soul">soul</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> With regard to the beginning of human personhood, one historical question has been: when does the <a href="/wiki/Ensoulment" title="Ensoulment">soul enter the body</a>? In modern terms, the question could be put instead: at what point does the developing individual develop personhood or selfhood?<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Related issues attached to the question of the beginning of human personhood include both the legal status, bodily integrity, and subjectivity of mothers<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and the philosophical concept of "natality" (i.e. "the distinctively human capacity to initiate a new beginning", which a new human life embodies).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fertilization">Fertilization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beginning_of_human_personhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Fertilization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Fertilization is the fusing of the gametes, that is a <a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a> cell and an <a href="/wiki/Ovum" class="mw-redirect" title="Ovum">ovum</a> (egg cell), to form a zygote. At this point, the zygote is <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genetically</a> distinct from either of its parents. </p>
      <p>Fertilization was not understood in ancient times. <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> and <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus Caesar</a> were <a href="/wiki/Miraculous_births#Heroes_and_historical_figures" title="Miraculous births">reputed to have been conceived without fertilization</a> (<a href="/wiki/Miraculous_births" title="Miraculous births">virgin birth</a>). <a href="/wiki/Hippocrates" title="Hippocrates">Hippocrates</a> believed that the embryo was the product of male semen and a female factor. But <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> held that only male semen gave rise to an embryo, while the female only provided a place for the embryo to develop,<sup id="cite_ref-fertile_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fertile-6">[6]</a></sup> (a concept he acquired from the <a href="/wiki/Preformationism" title="Preformationism">preformationist</a> <a href="/wiki/Pythagoras" title="Pythagoras">Pythagoras</a>). <a href="/wiki/William_Harvey" title="William Harvey">William Harvey</a> refuted Aristotle's idea that <a href="/wiki/Menstruation" title="Menstruation">menstrual blood</a> could be <a href="/wiki/Aristotle%27s_biology#Inheritance" title="Aristotle's biology">involved in the formation</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetus</a>, asserting that eggs from the female were somehow caused to become a fetus as a result of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_intercourse" title="Sexual intercourse">sexual intercourse</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WHarvey_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHarvey-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Spermatozoon" title="Spermatozoon">Sperm</a> cells were discovered in 1677 by <a href="/wiki/Antonie_van_Leeuwenhoek" title="Antonie van Leeuwenhoek">Antonie van Leeuwenhoek</a>, who believed that Aristotle had been proven correct.<sup id="cite_ref-discover_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-discover-8">[8]</a></sup> Some observers believed they could see an entirely pre-formed little human body in the head of a sperm.<sup id="cite_ref-religions_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-religions-9">[9]</a></sup> The human <a href="/wiki/Egg_cell" title="Egg cell">ova</a> was first observed in 1827 by <a href="/wiki/Karl_Ernst_von_Baer" title="Karl Ernst von Baer">Karl Ernst von Baer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-discover_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-discover-8">[8]</a></sup> Only in 1876 did <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Hertwig" title="Oscar Hertwig">Oscar Hertwig</a> prove that fertilization is due to fusion of an egg and sperm cell.<sup id="cite_ref-fertile_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fertile-6">[6]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Some members of the medical community accept fertilization as the point at which life begins. Dr. Bradley M. Patten from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">University of Michigan</a> wrote in <i>Human Embryology</i> that the union of the sperm and the ovum "initiates the life of a new individual" beginning "a new individual life history." In the standard college <a href="/wiki/Text_book" class="mw-redirect" title="Text book">text book</a> <i>Psychology and Life</i>, Dr. Floyd L. Ruch wrote "At the time of conception, two living germ cells—the sperm from the father and the egg, or ovum, from the mother—unite to produce a new individual." Dr. Herbert Ratner wrote that "It is now of unquestionable certainty that a human being comes into existence precisely at the moment when the sperm combines with the egg." This certain knowledge, Ratner says, comes from the study of <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a>. At fertilization, all of the genetic characteristics, such as the color of the eyes, "are laid down determinatively." James C. G. Conniff noted the prevalence of the above views in a study published by <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Magazine" title="The New York Times Magazine">The New York Times Magazine</a></i> in which he wrote, "At that moment conception takes place and, scientists generally agree, a new life begins—silent, secret, unknown."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The view that life begins at fertilization reached acceptance from mainstream sources at one point. In 1967, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Department_of_Education" title="New York City Department of Education">New York City school</a> officials launched a large <a href="/wiki/Sex_education" title="Sex education">sex education</a> program. The fifth grade textbook stated "Human life begins when the sperm cells of the father and the egg cells of the mother unite. This union is referred to as fertilization. For fertilization to take place and a baby to begin growing, the sperm cell must come in direct contact with the egg cell." Similarly, a textbook used in <a href="/wiki/Evanston,_Illinois" title="Evanston, Illinois">Evanston, Illinois</a> stated: "Life begins when a sperm cell and an ovum (egg cell) unite."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Catholic philosopher <a href="/wiki/Peter_Kreeft" title="Peter Kreeft">Peter Kreeft</a> goes so far as to say:<sup id="cite_ref-:0_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-12">[12]</a></sup> </p> 
      <blockquote>
       <p>This is widely accepted still today and has been verified by the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.princeton.edu/~prolife/articles/embryoquotes2.html">scientific community</a>.</p>
      </blockquote> 
      <p>"To begin with, scientifically something very radical occurs between the processes of gametogenesis and fertilization: the change from a simple part of one human being (i.e., a sperm) and a simple part of another human being (i.e., an oocyte,usually referred to as an "ovum" or "egg"), which simply possess "human life", to a new, genetically unique, newly existing, individual, whole living human being (a single-cell embryonic human zygote). That is, upon fertilization, parts of human beings have actually been transformed into something very different from what they were before; they have been changed into a single, whole human being. During the process of fertilization, the sperm and the oocyte cease to exist as such, and a new human being is produced. </p>
      <p>To understand this, it should be remembered that each kind of living organism has a specific number and quality of chromosomes that are characteristic for each member of a species. (The number can vary only slightly if the organism is to survive.) For example, the characteristic number of chromosomes for a member of the human species is 46 (plus or minus, e.g., in human beings with Down's or Turner's syndromes). Every somatic (or, body) cell in a human being has this characteristic number of chromosomes. Even the early germ cells contain 46 chromosomes; it is only their mature forms - the sex gametes, or sperms and oocytes - which will later contain only 23 chromosomes each..1 Sperms and oocytes are derived from primitive germ cells in the developing fetus by means of the process known as "gametogenesis." Because each germ cell normally has 46 chromosomes, the process of "fertilization" can not take place until the total number of chromosomes in each germ cell are cut in half. This is necessary so that after their fusion at fertilization the characteristic number of chromosomes in a single individual member of the human species (46) can be maintained, otherwise we would end up with a monster of some sort."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Others have disputed this view. Law professor and ethicist Richard Stith has written that the proper word for the growth of a fetus is not construction, as of a house or car, but development, as of a (pre-digital-era) photograph or a tree sapling: </p> 
      <blockquote>
       <p>Human beings do develop. To think they are constructed is flatly erroneous.... We know with certainty that quickening is an illusion, that the child is developing from the beginning, not being made from the outside, for its form lies within it, in its active potency, in its activated DNA.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> </p>
      </blockquote> 
      <p>That a human individual's existence begins at fertilization is the accepted position of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>, whose <a href="/wiki/Pontifical_Academy_for_Life" title="Pontifical Academy for Life">Pontifical Academy for Life</a> declared: "The moment that marks the beginning of the existence of a new 'human being' is constituted by the penetration of sperm into the <a href="/wiki/Oocyte" title="Oocyte">oocyte</a>. Fertilization promotes a series of linked events and transforms the egg cell into a '<a href="/wiki/Zygote" title="Zygote">zygote</a>'."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Congregation_for_the_Doctrine_of_the_Faith" title="Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith">Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith</a> also has stated and reaffirmed: "From the time that the ovum is fertilized, a new life is begun which is neither that of the father nor of the mother; it is rather the life of a new human being with his own growth."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Eastern Orthodox churches and most of the more conservative Protestant denominations also teach this view of life. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophical_and_religious_perspectives">Philosophical and religious perspectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beginning_of_human_personhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Philosophical and religious perspectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Answers to the question of when human life begins and when personhood begins have varied among social contexts, and have changed with shifts in ethical and religious beliefs, sometimes as a result of advances in scientific knowledge; in general they have developed in parallel with attitudes to <a href="/wiki/Abortion_debate" title="Abortion debate">abortion</a><sup id="cite_ref-devbio_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-devbio-17">[17]</a></sup> and to the use of <a href="/wiki/Infanticide" title="Infanticide">infanticide</a> as a means of reproductive control. </p>
      <p>Since the zygote is genetically identical to the embryo, the fully formed fetus, and the baby, questioning the beginning of personhood could lead to an instance of the <a href="/wiki/Sorites_paradox" title="Sorites paradox">Sorites paradox</a>, also known as <i>the paradox of the heap</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sorites_JVI_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sorites_JVI-18">[18]</a></sup> </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Neil_Postman" title="Neil Postman">Neil Postman</a> has written that in pre-modern societies, the lives of children were not regarded as unique or valuable in the same way they are in modern societies, in part as a result of high <a href="/wiki/Infant_mortality" title="Infant mortality">infant mortality</a>. However, when childhood began to develop its own distinctive features (including graded schools to teach reading, children's stories, games, etc.) this view changed. According to Postman, "the custom of celebrating a child's birthday did not exist in America throughout most of the eighteenth century, and, in fact, the precise marking of a child's age in any way is a relatively recent cultural habit, no more than two hundred years old."<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Ancient writers held diverse views on the subject of the beginning of personhood, understood as the soul's entry or development in the human body. In <i>Panpsychism in the West</i>, David Skrbina noted the various kinds of soul envisioned by the early Greeks.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Generally, the question of the ensoulment of the fetus revolved around the question of when the rational soul entered the body, whether it was an integral part of the bodily form and substance, or whether it was pre-existent and subject to <a href="/wiki/Reincarnation" title="Reincarnation">reincarnation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pre-existence" title="Pre-existence">pre-existence</a>. </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> developed a theory of progressive ensoulment. In <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Generation_of_Animals" class="mw-redirect" title="On the Generation of Animals">On the Generation of Animals</a></i>, he declared that the soul develops first a vegetative soul, then animal, and finally human, adding that abortions were permissible early in pregnancy, before certain biological processes began. He believed that the female substance was passive, the male active, and that it required time for the male substance to "animate" the whole.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Hippocrates" title="Hippocrates">Hippocrates</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pythagoreans" class="mw-redirect" title="Pythagoreans">Pythagoreans</a> stated that fertilization marked the beginning of a human life, and that the human soul was created at the time of fertilization. </p>
      <p>According to <i><a href="/wiki/Hinduism_Today" title="Hinduism Today">Hinduism Today</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Vedas" title="Vedas">Vedic literature</a> states that the soul enters the body at conception.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Concepts of <a href="/wiki/Pre-existence" title="Pre-existence">pre-existence</a> are found in various forms in Plato, Judaism, and Islam. </p>
      <p>The Jewish <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">Talmud</a> holds that all life is precious but that a fetus is not a person, in the sense of termination of pregnancy being considered murder. If a woman's life is endangered by a pregnancy, an abortion is permitted. However, if the "greater part" of the fetus has emerged from the womb, then its life may not be taken even to save the woman's, <i>"because you cannot choose between one human life and another".</i><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Some medieval <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> theologians, such as <a href="/wiki/Marsilio_Ficino" title="Marsilio Ficino">Marsilio Ficino</a>, held that <a href="/wiki/Ensoulment" title="Ensoulment">ensoulment</a> occurs when an infant takes its first breath of air. They cite, among other passages, Genesis 2:7, which reads: "And the Lord God formed man of the dust of the ground, and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life; and man became a living soul."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The Early Church held various views on the subject, primarily either the ensoulment at conception or delayed hominization. <a href="/wiki/Tertullian" title="Tertullian">Tertullian</a> held a view, <a href="/wiki/Traducianism" title="Traducianism">traducianism</a>, which was later condemned as heresy. This view held that the soul was derived from the parents and generated in parallel with the generation of the physical body. This viewpoint was deemed unsatisfactory by <a href="/wiki/St._Augustine" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Augustine">St. Augustine</a>, as it did not account for original sin. Basing himself on the Septuagint version of Exodus 21:22, he affirmed the Aristotelian view of delayed hominization. </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/St._Thomas_Aquinas" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Thomas Aquinas">St. Thomas Aquinas</a> and <a href="/wiki/St._Augustine_of_Hippo" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Augustine of Hippo">St. Augustine of Hippo</a> held the view that fetuses were "animated" (using Aristotle's term for ensoulment) near the 40th day after conception.<sup id="cite_ref-devbio_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-devbio-17">[17]</a></sup> However, both held that abortion was always gravely wrong.<sup id="cite_ref-Fitzgerald,_1_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fitzgerald,_1-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In general, the soul was viewed as some kind of animating principle; and the human variety was referred to as the "rational soul". </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fetal_personhood_in_law">Fetal personhood in law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beginning_of_human_personhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fetal personhood in law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecclesiastical_courts">Ecclesiastical courts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beginning_of_human_personhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecclesiastical courts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Following the decline of the Roman Empire, <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_courts" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecclesiastical courts">ecclesiastical courts</a> held wide jurisdiction throughout Europe. According to Donald DeMarco, PhD,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> the Church treated the killing of an unformed or "unanimated" fetus as a matter of "anticipated homicide", with a corresponding lesser penance required. In the <a href="/wiki/Catechism_of_the_Catholic_Church" title="Catechism of the Catholic Church">Catechism of the Catholic Church</a>, the following statement regarding the beginning of human life and personhood is provided: </p> 
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Human life must be respected and protected absolutely from the moment of conception. From the first moment of his existence, a human being must be recognized as having the rights of a person - among which is the inviolable right of every innocent being to life.
        <sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
       </dd>
      </dl> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_law">Common law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beginning_of_human_personhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Common law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Although <a href="/wiki/Abortion_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Abortion in the United Kingdom">abortion in the United Kingdom</a> was traditionally dealt with in the <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_court" title="Ecclesiastical court">ecclesiastical courts</a>, English common law addressed the issue from 1115 on, beginning with first mention in <i><a href="/wiki/Leges_Henrici_Primi" title="Leges Henrici Primi">Leges Henrici Primi</a></i>. In this treatise, abortion, even of a "formed" fetus, was a "quasi-homicide", carrying a penalty of 10 years' penance. This was a much lesser penalty than would accrue to full homicide. With the exception of Bracton, later writers insisted that killing a fetus was "great misprision, and no murder", as formulated by <a href="/wiki/Sir_Edward_Coke" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Edward Coke">Sir Edward Coke</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Institutes_of_the_Lawes_of_England" title="Institutes of the Lawes of England">Institutes of the Lawes of England</a></i>. Coke noted that the murder victim must have been "a reasonable creature <i>in rerum natura</i>", in accordance with the standards of <a href="/wiki/Murder_in_English_law" title="Murder in English law">murder in English law</a>. This formulation was repeated by <a href="/wiki/Sir_William_Blackstone" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir William Blackstone">Sir William Blackstone</a> in England and in <i><a href="/wiki/Bouvier%27s_Law_Dictionary" title="Bouvier's Law Dictionary">Bouvier's Law Dictionary</a></i> in the United States. </p>
      <p>The reasonableness of the creature is of some considerable weight in the legal conception of personhood. Children are not considered full persons under the law until they reach the age of majority. </p>
      <p>Nonetheless, children have been treated as persons with respect to bodily offences, beginning with <a href="/wiki/Offences_against_the_Person_Act_1828" class="mw-redirect" title="Offences against the Person Act 1828">Offences against the Person Act 1828</a>, although this protection did not prevent children from being sold by their parents, as in the <a href="/wiki/Eliza_Armstrong_case" title="Eliza Armstrong case">Eliza Armstrong case</a>, long after the slave trade had been abolished in England. </p>
      <p>In addition, "a child <i><a href="/wiki/En_ventre_sa_mere" title="En ventre sa mere">en ventre sa mere</a></i>" (in utero) was regarded by common law as "in being," or "as born" when ensuring that wills and trusts do not run afoul of the <a href="/wiki/Rule_against_perpetuities" title="Rule against perpetuities">rule against perpetuities</a>; nine (or sometimes ten) months of gestation were allotted for this purpose.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> </p>
      <p><br> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Implantation">Implantation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beginning_of_human_personhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Implantation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In his book <i><a href="/wiki/Aborting_America" class="mw-redirect" title="Aborting America">Aborting America</a>,</i> <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Nathanson" title="Bernard Nathanson">Bernard Nathanson</a> argued that implantation should be considered the point at which life begins.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> </p> 
      <blockquote> 
       <p>Biochemically, this is when alpha announces its presence as part of the human community by means of its hormonal messages, which we now have the technology to receive. We also know biochemically that it is an independent organism distinct from the mother. [Note: in writing the book, "alpha" was Nathanson's term for any human before birth.] </p> 
      </blockquote>
      <p>In their book, <i>When Does Human Life Begin?</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> John L. Merritt, MD and his son J. Lawrence Meritt II, MD, present the idea that if "the breath of life" (Genesis 2:7) is oxygen, then a <a href="/wiki/Blastocyst" title="Blastocyst">blastocyst</a> starts taking in the breath of life from the mother's blood the moment it successfully implants in her womb, which is about a week after fertilization. If the end-point to human life is the moment the body stops using oxygen, then it may follow that the corresponding starting-point is the moment the body starts using oxygen. </p>
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Segmentation">Segmentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beginning_of_human_personhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Segmentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Non-conjoined monozygotic twins form up to day 14 of embryonic development, but when twinning occurs after 14 days, the twins will likely be conjoined.<sup id="cite_ref-twinning_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-twinning-33">[33]</a></sup> Some argue that an early embryo cannot be a person because "If every person is an individual, one cannot be divided from oneself."<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>However, Fr. Norman Ford stated that "the evidence would seem to indicate <i>not</i> that there is <i>no</i> individual at conception, but that there is at least one and possibly more."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Fr. Ford, in his 1988 book When Did I Begin?, was actually a famous proponent of the argument that embryos were NOT human individuals until reaching the stage where twinning is no longer possible. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> He went on to support the idea that, similar to processes found in other species, one twin could be the parent of the other <a href="/wiki/Asexual_reproduction" title="Asexual reproduction">asexually</a>. Theodore Hall agreed with the plausibility of this explanation saying, "We wonder if the biological process in twinning isn't simply another example of how nature reproduces from other individuals without destroying that person's or persons' individuality."<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span id="Brain_function_.28brain_birth.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Brain_function_(brain_birth)">Brain function (brain birth)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beginning_of_human_personhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Brain function (brain birth)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In the years since the designation of brain death as a new criterion for death, attention has been directed towards the central role of the nervous system in a number of areas of ethical decision-making. The notion that there exists a neurological end-point to human life has led to efforts at defining a corresponding neurological starting-point. This latter quest has led to the concept of brain birth (or brain life), signifying the converse of <a href="/wiki/Brain_death" title="Brain death">brain death</a>. The quest for a neurological marker of the beginning of human personhood owes its impetus to the perceived symmetry between processes at the beginning and end of life, thus if brain function is a criterion used to determine the medical death of a person, it should also be the criterion for its beginning. </p>
      <p>Just as there are two types of brain death - whole brain death (which refers to the irreversible cessation of function of both the <a href="/wiki/Brain_stem" class="mw-redirect" title="Brain stem">brain stem</a> and higher parts of the brain) and higher brain death (destruction of the cerebral hemispheres alone, with possible retention of brain stem function), there are two types of brain birth (based on their reversal) - brain stem birth at the first appearance of brain waves in lower brain (brain stem) at 6–8 weeks of gestation, and higher brain birth, at the first appearance of brain waves in higher brain (<a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex" title="Cerebral cortex">cerebral cortex</a>) at 22–24 weeks of gestation.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones1998_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones1998-36">[36]</a></sup> </p>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth">Birth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beginning_of_human_personhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Birth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>While, at one end of the ideological spectrum, some believe human personhood begins at fertilization, at the other end of the spectrum others believe that as long as the fetus is still inside the body of the woman (whether it is viable or not), it does not have any rights of its own.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_markers">Other markers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beginning_of_human_personhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other markers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>There are also other ideas of when personhood is achieved: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>at <a href="/wiki/Ensoulment" title="Ensoulment">ensoulment</a></li> 
       <li>at "formation" – an early concept of bodily development (see <a href="/wiki/Preformationism" title="Preformationism">Preformationism</a>).</li> 
       <li>at the emergence of consciousness</li> 
       <li>at the emergence of rationality (see <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Kant</a>)</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>Human personhood may also be seen as a work-in-progress, with the beginning being a <a href="/wiki/Continuum_(theory)" class="mw-redirect" title="Continuum (theory)">continuum</a> rather than a strict point in time.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Individuation">Individuation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beginning_of_human_personhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Individuation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Philosophers such as <a href="/wiki/Aquinas" class="mw-redirect" title="Aquinas">Aquinas</a> use the concept of <a href="/wiki/Individuation" title="Individuation">individuation</a>. In regard to the <a href="/wiki/Abortion_debate" title="Abortion debate">abortion debate</a>, they argue that abortion is not permissible from the point at which individual human identity is realised. <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Kenny" title="Anthony Kenny">Anthony Kenny</a> argues that this can be derived from everyday beliefs and language and one can legitimately say "if my mother had had an abortion six months into her pregnancy, she would have killed me" then one can reasonably infer that at six months the "me" in question would have been an existing person with a valid claim to life. Since division of the zygote into twins through the process of <a href="/wiki/Twins" class="mw-redirect" title="Twins">monozygotic twinning</a> can occur until the fourteenth day of pregnancy, Kenny argues that individual identity is obtained at this point and thus abortion is not permissible after two weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethical_perspectives">Ethical perspectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beginning_of_human_personhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ethical perspectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The distinction in <a href="/wiki/Value_(ethics)" title="Value (ethics)">ethical value</a> between existing persons and <a href="/wiki/Potential_future_person" class="mw-redirect" title="Potential future person">potential future persons</a> has been questioned.<sup id="cite_ref-hare1975p212-213_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hare1975p212-213-47">[47]</a></sup> Subsequently, it has been argued that contraception and even the decision not to procreate at all could be regarded as immoral on a similar basis as <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-warren1977_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-warren1977-48">[48]</a></sup> Subsequently, any marker of the beginning of human personhood doesn't necessarily mark where it is ethically right or wrong to assist or intervene. In a <a href="/wiki/Consequentialism" title="Consequentialism">consequentialistic</a> point of view, an assisting or intervening action may be regarded as basically equivalent whether it is performed before, during or after the creation of a human being, because the end result would basically be the same, that is, the existence or non-existence of that human being. In a view holding value in bringing potential persons into existence, it has been argued to be justified to perform abortion of an <a href="/wiki/Unintended_pregnancy" title="Unintended pregnancy">unintended pregnancy</a> in favor for conceiving a new child later in better conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-Savulescu2002_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Savulescu2002-49">[49]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_perspectives">Legal perspectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beginning_of_human_personhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Legal perspectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beginning_of_human_personhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main articles: 
       <a href="/wiki/Eighth_Amendment_of_the_Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Eighth Amendment of the Constitution of Ireland">Eighth Amendment of the Constitution of Ireland</a> and 
       <a href="/wiki/Abortion_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Abortion in the Republic of Ireland">Abortion in the Republic of Ireland</a>
      </div> 
      <p>The 1983 <a href="/wiki/Eighth_Amendment_of_the_Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Eighth Amendment of the Constitution of Ireland">Eight Amendment</a> granted the full right to life, and personhood, to any "unborn". As such, abortion was banned in nearly all cases, except to save the life of the mother. This was <a href="/wiki/Thirty-sixth_Amendment_of_the_Constitution_Bill_2018" class="mw-redirect" title="Thirty-sixth Amendment of the Constitution Bill 2018">repealed</a> on 25 May 2018 by a 66% voting margin, with abortion becoming legal on 1 January 2019.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beginning_of_human_personhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In its 1885 decision <i><a href="/wiki/McArthur_v._Scott" title="McArthur v. Scott">McArthur v. Scott</a></i>, the US Supreme Court affirmed the common law principle that a child in its mother's womb can be regarded as "in being" for the purpose of resolving a dispute about wills and trusts.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1973, <a href="/wiki/Harry_Blackmun" title="Harry Blackmun">Harry Blackmun</a> wrote the court opinion for <i><a href="/wiki/Roe_v._Wade" title="Roe v. Wade">Roe v. Wade</a></i>, saying "We need not resolve the difficult question of when life begins. When those trained in the respective disciplines of medicine, philosophy, and theology are unable to arrive at any consensus, the judiciary, at this point in the development of man's knowledge, is not in a position to speculate." </p>
      <p>In 2002, the <a href="/wiki/Born-Alive_Infants_Protection_Act" title="Born-Alive Infants Protection Act">Born-Alive Infants Protection Act</a> was enacted, which ensures that the legal concepts of <i>person, baby, infant,</i> and <i>child</i> include those which have been born alive in the course of a miscarriage or abortion, regardless of development, gestational age, or whether the placenta and umbilical cord are still attached. This law makes no comment on personhood in utero but ensures that no person after birth is characterized as not a person.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 2003, the <a href="/wiki/Partial-Birth_Abortion_Ban_Act" title="Partial-Birth Abortion Ban Act">Partial-Birth Abortion Ban Act</a> was enacted, which prohibits an abortion if <i>"either the entire baby's head is outside the body of the mother, or any part of the baby's trunk past the navel is outside the body of the mother."</i><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 2004, President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> signed the <a href="/wiki/Unborn_Victims_of_Violence_Act" title="Unborn Victims of Violence Act">Unborn Victims of Violence Act</a> into law.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The law effectively extends personhood status<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> to a "<i>child in utero at any stage of development, who is carried in the womb</i>"<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> if they are targeted, injured or killed during the commission of any of over 60 listed violent crimes. The law also prohibits the prosecutions of "<i>any person for conduct relating</i>" to a legally consented to abortion. </p>
      <p>Today, 38 U.S. States legally recognize a human fetus or "unborn child" as a crime victim, at least for the purpose of <a href="/wiki/Homicide" title="Homicide">homicide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Feticide" class="mw-redirect" title="Feticide">feticide</a> laws.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> According to progressive media watchdog <a href="/wiki/Media_Matters_for_America" title="Media Matters for America">Media Matters for America</a>, "Further, a prenatal personhood measure might subject a woman who suffers a pregnancy-related complication or a miscarriage to criminal investigations and possibly jail time for homicide, manslaughter or reckless endangerment. And because so many laws use the terms "persons" or "people," a prenatal personhood measure could affect large numbers of a state's laws, changing the application of thousands of laws and resulting in unforeseeable, unintended, and absurd consequences." <sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the United States, the 1992 <a href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Supreme Court">United States Supreme Court</a> case of <i><a href="/wiki/Planned_Parenthood_v._Casey" title="Planned Parenthood v. Casey">Planned Parenthood v. Casey</a></i> held that a law cannot place legal restrictions imposing an <a href="/wiki/Undue_burden_standard" title="Undue burden standard">undue burden</a> for "the purpose or effect of placing a substantial obstacle in the path of a woman seeking an abortion of a nonviable fetus."<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> This standard was also upheld in the Supreme Court case of <i><a href="/wiki/Whole_Woman%27s_Health_v._Hellerstedt" title="Whole Woman's Health v. Hellerstedt">Whole Woman's Health v. Hellerstedt</a></i> (2016) in which several <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> restrictions were struck down.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> </p>
