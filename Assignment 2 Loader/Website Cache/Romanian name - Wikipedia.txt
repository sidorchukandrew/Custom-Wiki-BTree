      <p>A <b>name in <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Romania" title="Culture of Romania">Romanian tradition</a></b> consists of a <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> (<i>prenume</i>) and a <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">family name</a> (surname) (<i>nume</i> or <i>nume de familie</i>). In official documents, surnames usually appear before given names. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Given_names">Given names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Romanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Given names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Romanians have one, two or more given names, e.g. Ana Cristina Maria (three given names), all being chosen by the child's parents. One of them, usually the first, is used in daily life while the others are solely for official documents, such as birth, death and marriage certificates. </p>
      <p>Traditionally, most people were given names from the <a href="/wiki/Romanian_Orthodox_Church" title="Romanian Orthodox Church">Romanian Orthodox</a> calendar of saints. Common names of this type are <i>Ion</i> or <i>Andrei</i> for males and <i>Maria</i> or <i>Elena</i> for females. Given names with a Christian lineage have an identifiable English equivalent: <i>Andrei</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Andrew" title="Andrew">Andrew</a>)</i>, <i>Constantin</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Constantine_(name)" title="Constantine (name)">Constantine</a>)</i>, <i>Cristian</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Christian_(given_name)" title="Christian (given name)">Christian</a></i>), <i>Daniel</i>/<i>Dan</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Daniel" title="Daniel">Daniel</a></i>/<i><a href="/wiki/Dan_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Dan (disambiguation)">Dan</a></i>), <i>Gheorghe</i>/<i>George</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/George_(given_name)" title="George (given name)">George</a></i>), <i>Grigore</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Gregory_(given_name)" title="Gregory (given name)">Gregory</a></i>), <i>Ilie</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Elijah" title="Elijah">Elijah</a></i>), <i>Ion</i>/<i>Ioan</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/John_(given_name)" title="John (given name)">John</a></i>), <i>Iacob</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Jacob" title="Jacob">Jacob</a></i>/<i><a href="/wiki/James_(name)" title="James (name)">James</a></i>), <i>Laurențiu</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Lawrence_(given_name)" title="Lawrence (given name)">Lawrence</a></i>), <i>Luca</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Luke_(name)" title="Luke (name)">Luke</a></i>), <i>Marcu</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Mark_(given_name)" title="Mark (given name)">Mark</a></i>), <i>Matei</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Matthew_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Matthew (name)">Matthew</a></i>), <i>Mihail</i>/<i>Mihai</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Michael" title="Michael">Michael</a></i>), <i>Nicolae</i>/<i>Niculaie</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Nicholas" title="Nicholas">Nicholas</a></i>), <i>Pavel</i>/<i>Paul</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Paul_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Paul (name)">Paul</a></i>), <i>Petru</i>/<i>Petre</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Peter_(given_name)" title="Peter (given name)">Peter</a></i>), <i>Ștefan</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Stephen" title="Stephen">Stephen</a></i>), <i>Vasile</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Basil_(name)" title="Basil (name)">Basil</a></i>). The most common name, <i>Maria</i><sup id="cite_ref-gandul2007_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gandul2007-1">[1]</a></sup> is the equivalent of <i><a href="/wiki/Mary_(name)" title="Mary (name)">Mary</a></i>. <i>Maria</i> has led to many closely related names such as <i>Mariana, Marioara, Maricica, Maricela, Măriuca, Mara, Marina, Marilena, Marieta, Marinela, Marisa, Marița, Marusia, Mia, Mioara.</i> </p>
      <p>Roman heritage is reflected in Romance given names such as <i>Traian</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Trajan" title="Trajan">Trajan</a></i>), <i>Titus</i>, <i>Marius</i>, <i>Octavian</i>, <i>Ovidiu</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a></i>), <i>Aurel</i> (<i>Aurelius</i>), <i>Cornel</i> (<i>Cornelius</i>) etc. Such names are common especially in <a href="/wiki/Transylvania" title="Transylvania">Transylvania</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dacia" title="Dacia">Dacian</a> heritage is reflected through the name <i>Decebal</i> (from king <a href="/wiki/Decebalus" title="Decebalus">Decebalus</a>). </p>
      <p>Some names are inspired from nature, such as <i>Sorin/Sorina</i> (<i>soare</i>, "sun"), <i>Codruț/Codruța</i> or <i>Codrin/Codrina</i> (<i>codru</i>, "woods"), or flowers: <i>Crin/Crina</i>, <i>Narcis/Narcisa</i>, <i>Viorel/Viorica</i>, <i>Anemona</i>, <i>Brândușa</i>, <i>Camelia</i>, <i>Iolanda</i>, <i>Lăcrămioara</i> etc. The word <i>floare</i> ("flower") has led to several names such as <i>Florin/Forina</i>, <i>Florentin/Florentina</i>, <i>Florian/Floriana</i>, <i>Florica</i>, <i>Floarea</i>, . </p>
      <p>Traditional Romanian names which come from Romanian words include <i><a href="/wiki/Doina_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Doina (disambiguation)">Doina</a></i> which means "<a href="/wiki/Doina" title="Doina">doina</a>", a traditional Romanian musical tune style, or <i><a href="/wiki/Lumini%C8%9Ba_(name)" title="Luminița (name)">Luminița</a></i>, meaning "little light", from the word "lumină" (light). The name <i>Lăcrămioara</i> refers to the name of a flower (<a href="/wiki/Lily_of_the_valley" title="Lily of the valley">lily of the valley</a>), but also means "little tear", from the word "lacrimă" (tear). <i>Crenguța</i> means "little branch", from the word "creangă" (branch). </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Slavic_influence_on_Romanian" title="Slavic influence on Romanian">Slavic influence on Romanian</a> is present at all linguistic levels, including names. These include names containing the Slavic root <i>-mir</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHasdeǔ1898clxxi_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHasdeǔ1898clxxi-3">[3]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHasdeǔ1898clxxi_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHasdeǔ1898clxxi-3">[3]</a></sup> Examples of Slavic names in Romanian include<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFelecan2011_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFelecan2011-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHasdeǔ1898clxxi_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHasdeǔ1898clxxi-3">[3]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Bogdan" title="Bogdan">Bogdan</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Drago%C8%99_(given_name)" title="Dragoș (given name)">Dragoș</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mircea_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Mircea (disambiguation)">Mircea</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Radu_(given_name)" title="Radu (given name)">Radu</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Tihomir" title="Tihomir">Tihomir</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Vlad" title="Vlad">Vlad</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Vladislav" title="Vladislav">Vladislav</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Vladimir_(name)" title="Vladimir (name)">Vladimir</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Miroslav_(given_name)" title="Miroslav (given name)">Miroslav</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Casimir" title="Casimir">Casimir</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Anastasia" title="Anastasia">Anastasia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Irina" title="Irina">Irina</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Milena_(given_name)" title="Milena (given name)">Milena</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Olga_(name)" title="Olga (name)">Olga</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Raisa_(given_name)" title="Raisa (given name)">Raisa</a></i>. </p>
      <p>Some common names are the names born by historical rulers (<i>domnitori</i>/<i>voievozi</i>), such <i>Ștefan</i> (<a href="/wiki/%C8%98tefan_cel_Mare" class="mw-redirect" title="Ștefan cel Mare">Ștefan cel Mare</a>), <i>Mihai</i> (<a href="/wiki/Mihai_Viteazu" class="mw-redirect" title="Mihai Viteazu">Mihai Viteazu</a>), <i>Mircea</i> (<a href="/wiki/Mircea_cel_B%C4%83tr%C3%A2n" class="mw-redirect" title="Mircea cel Bătrân">Mircea cel Bătrân</a>), <i>Vlad</i> (<a href="/wiki/Vlad_%C8%9Aepe%C8%99" class="mw-redirect" title="Vlad Țepeș">Vlad Țepeș</a>), <i>Rareș</i> (<a href="/wiki/Petru_Rare%C8%99" title="Petru Rareș">Petru Rareș</a>), although not all parents make such associations, especially when the name in that of a <a href="/wiki/Christian_saint" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian saint">Christian saint</a>. Christian parents may also choose names with a specific connotation with regard to Romanian <a href="/wiki/Religious_history" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious history">religious history</a>, such as <i>Arsenie</i>, from <a href="/wiki/Arsenie_Boca" title="Arsenie Boca">Arsenie Boca</a>, or <i>Zian/Ziana</i>, the birth name of Arsenie Boca. </p>
      <p><i>Alexandru/Alexandra</i> are very common names. They also include the variants of <i>Alex</i>, <i>Alexia</i>, <i>Alexandrina</i>, or the 'foreign' variants of <i>Alessia</i>, <i>Alessandra</i>, <i>Alexa</i> (see below). </p>
      <p>In the 1990s, as <a href="/wiki/Telenovelas" class="mw-redirect" title="Telenovelas">telenovelas</a> started to be broadcast in Romania, Spanish/Latin American names have become popular; and the trend of giving children such names has been reinforced by the migration or travel of parents to <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> or <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. As such, names like <i>Mario</i>, <i>Antonio</i>, <i>Alberto</i>, <i>Esmeralda</i>, <i>Gianni</i>, <i>Giovanni</i>, <i>Alessia</i> etc. are relatively common. Indeed, <i>Mario</i>, <i>Antonio</i> and <i>Alessia</i> were in the top 50 as baby names in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The prevalence of given names follows <a href="/wiki/Fads_and_trends" class="mw-redirect" title="Fads and trends">trends</a>, with some names being popular in some years, and some considered definitely out-of-fashion. As an example, few children born since 1980 would bear the names of <i>Gheorghe</i>, <i>Vasile</i>, or <i>Ilie</i>, which are generally associated with the idea of an elderly man (while the name of <i>Gheorghe</i> is rare among the younger generation, the variant <i>George</i> is more common). However, such "old-fashioned" names are sometimes used as <a href="/wiki/Middle_name" title="Middle name">middle names</a>. By contrast, some names are associated with the younger generations: for example the feminine name <i><a href="/wiki/Andreea" title="Andreea">Andreea</a></i> become popular from the 1970s onwards, being one of the most common given names in the younger generations, ranking third in popularity among feminine names given to children born in 1989,<sup id="cite_ref-b365.ro_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-b365.ro-6">[6]</a></sup> second in 2009,<sup id="cite_ref-behindthename.com_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-behindthename.com-7">[7]</a></sup> and fourth in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-gandul.info_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gandul.info-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Compound given names are uncommon, with only one notable exception, i.e. <i>Ana-Maria</i> (sometimes spelled <i>Anamaria</i>). In that case this is not considered to be two separate given names. <a href="/wiki/Diminutives" class="mw-redirect" title="Diminutives">Diminutives</a> are often used as names (e.g. Ionuţ, Ionel, Ionela, Anişoara). <a href="/wiki/Ionu%C5%A3" class="mw-redirect" title="Ionuţ">Ionuţ</a>, a diminutive from Ion/Ioan, is one of the most common names in Romania. It ranked second in popularity among male names given to children born in 1989,<sup id="cite_ref-b365.ro_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-b365.ro-6">[6]</a></sup> third in 2009,<sup id="cite_ref-behindthename.com_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-behindthename.com-7">[7]</a></sup> and ninth in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-gandul.info_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gandul.info-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Romanian male given names end in a <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">consonant</a> (<i>Adrian</i>, <i>Ion</i>, <i>Paul</i>, <i>Ștefan</i>, <i>Victor</i>) or in any vowel other than <i>-a</i> (<i>Alexandru</i>, <i>Andrei</i>, <i>Mihai</i>), with some exceptions (<i>Mircea</i>, <i>Mihnea</i>),while almost all female names end in <i>-a</i> (<i>Ana</i>, <i>Elena</i>, <i>Ioana</i>, <i>Maria</i>), with only very few exceptions such as <i>Carmen</i>. This is most easily seen in the male-female name pairs: <i>Gabriel</i>-<i>Gabriela</i>, <i>Ioan</i>-<i>Ioana</i>, <i>George</i>-<i>Georgiana</i>, <i>Mihai</i>-<i>Mihaela</i>, <i>Nicolae</i>-<i>Nicoleta</i>, etc. </p>
      <p>The most common Romanian name is <i>Maria</i>, with approximately 1.38 million females having it as one of their given names.<sup id="cite_ref-gandul2007_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gandul2007-1">[1]</a></sup> Also, almost 1.37 million Romanians have <i>Ion</i>, <i>Ioan</i> and <i>Ioana</i> as one of their given names.<sup id="cite_ref-gandul2007_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gandul2007-1">[1]</a></sup> The most common names are:<sup id="cite_ref-auto_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hotnews.ro_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hotnews.ro-9">[9]</a></sup> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>For males: Alexandru, Adrian, Andrei, Mihai, Ionuţ, Florin, Daniel, Marian, Marius, Cristian for all males.<sup id="cite_ref-name-statistics.org_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-name-statistics.org-10">[10]</a></sup> For boys born in 2014 they are: Andrei, David, Alexandru, Gabriel, Mihai, Cristian, Ştefan, Luca, Ionuţ, Darius.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-2">[2]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>For females: Ana-Maria, Mihaela, Andreea, Elena, Alexandra, Cristina, Daniela, Alina, Maria, Ioana for all females.<sup id="cite_ref-name-statistics.org_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-name-statistics.org-10">[10]</a></sup> For girls born in 2014, they are: Maria, Elena, Ioana, Andreea, Sofia, Alexandra, Antonia, Daria, Ana, Gabriela.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
      </ul> 
      <p>The given name can be changed on request, but it is necessary to prove a legitimate interest for the change (usually that the current name is a cause of mockery etc.). </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surnames">Surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Romanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Like in most of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, in Romania it is customary for a child to take its father's surname, and a wife her husband's surname. In cases where <a href="/wiki/Paternity_(law)" class="mw-redirect" title="Paternity (law)">paternity</a> is not established, the child takes the mother's surname. The law however is flexible, allowing for the couple to choose their family name, and thus the surname they would use for all their children. Typically it is the father's surname (in keeping with the tradition), but parents may also opt to use the mother's surname; or for both of the spouses to have both surnames; or for one spouse to use a <a href="/wiki/Double-barrelled_name" title="Double-barrelled name">double-barrelled name</a>. Romanian law does not require any of the spouses to change their surname, but in practice in most families both spouses will have the husband's original surname. If parents have different surnames, a child will have either the surname of one of them, or both surnames. Romanian surnames remain the same regardless of the sex of the person. After a <a href="/wiki/Divorce" title="Divorce">divorce</a>, the spouse who changed the surname (usually the wife) will generally revert to the original surname. However, the married surname can be retained, either with the consent of both spouses, or by court order. If the other spouse does not consent to the retaining of the surname, the spouse who wants to retain it can petition the court and ask it for permission. The relevant laws are Art. 282,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Art. 383<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Art. 449 <sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Art. 450.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Code_of_Romania" title="Civil Code of Romania">Civil Code of Romania</a>. </p>
      <p>Until the 19th century, the names were primarily of the form "[given name] [father's name] [grandfather's name]". The few exceptions are usually famous people or the nobility (<a href="/wiki/Boyar" title="Boyar">boyars</a>). The name reform introduced around 1850 had the names changed to a western style consisting of a given name followed by a family name (surname). As such, the name is called <b>prenume</b>, while the family name is called <b>nume</b> or, when otherwise ambiguous, <b>nume de familie</b> ("family name"). Middle names (second given names) are also fairly common. </p>
      <p>Many Romanian names are derivative forms obtained by the addition of some traditional Romanian <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffixes</a>, such as <i>-escu</i>, <i>-ăscu</i>, <i>-eanu</i>, <i>-anu</i>, <i>-an</i>, <i>-aru</i>, <i>-atu</i>, or <i>-oiu</i>. These uniquely Romanian suffixes strongly identify ancestral nationality. </p>
      <p>Historically, when the family name reform was introduced in the mid-19th century, the default was to use a <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronym</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Matronymic" title="Matronymic">matronym</a> when the father was dead or unknown. A typical derivation was to append the suffix <i>-escu</i> to the father's name, e.g. <i>Ionescu</i> ("Ion's child") and <i>Petrescu</i> ("Petre's child"). The <i>-escu</i> is derived from Latin <i>-iscum</i>, and cognate with Italian <i>-esco</i> and French <i>-esque</i>, but its pervasiveness in Romanian may have come from Slavic influence, by way of Old Slavonic -ьskъ (which is in fact cognate to Latin <i>-iscum</i> via Proto-Indo-European). </p>
      <p>Another common derivation was to append the suffix <i>-eanu</i> or the simpler forms <i>-anu</i> and <i>-an</i> to the name of a place, river, village, or region, e.g. <i>Ardeleanu</i> (from <i><a href="/wiki/Transylvania" title="Transylvania">Ardeal</a></i>), <i>Moldoveanu</i> (from <i><a href="/wiki/Moldavia" title="Moldavia">Moldova</a></i>), <i>Mureșanu</i> (from <i><a href="/wiki/Mure%C8%99_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Mureș River">Mureș</a></i>), <i>Sadoveanu</i> etc. (cognate to Italian <i>-(i)ano</i>). They may indicate a country or ethnic origin (e.g. <i>Grecu</i> - from Greece, <i>Ungureanu</i> - from Hungary, <i>Rusu</i> - from Russia). </p>
      <p>The suffix <i>-cea</i> (as in <i>Mihalcea</i>, <i>Grigorcea</i>, <i>Neculcea</i>, <i>Oncea</i>, etc.) is Slavic.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHasdeǔ1898clxxi_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHasdeǔ1898clxxi-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Furthermore, the Slavic influence on Romanian has manifested itself by way of the emergence of Romanian surnames of Slavic origin (as in <i>Bucinschi</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Chirilov" title="Chirilov">Chirilov</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Covaci" title="Covaci">Covaci</a></i>, <i>Levandovschi</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Marcovici" title="Marcovici">Marcovici</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Novac_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Novac (disambiguation)">Novac</a></i>, etc.). </p>
      <p>There are also descriptive family names derived from occupations or nicknames, e.g. <i>Ciobanu</i>/<i>Păcuraru</i> ("shepherd"/"pitch-maker/pitch-vendor"), <i>Croitoru</i> ("tailor"), <i>Fieraru</i> ("smith"), <i>Moraru</i> ("miller"), <i>Bălan</i> ("blond"), <i>Țăranu</i> ("villager") etc. Also some Romanian surnames come from various animals and plants, most probably being former nicknames, with or without the addition of various suffixes, e.g. <i>Bourean(u)</i> ("ox"), <i>Căpreanu</i> ("goat"), <i>Jderoiu</i> ("marten"), <i>Lupu</i> ("wolf"), <i>Ursu</i> ("bear"), <i>Zimbrean</i> (<a href="/wiki/European_bison" title="European bison">"bison"</a>), <i>Vidraru</i> ("otter"). Some surnames come from colors: e.g. <i>Roșu</i> (<i>Red</i>), <i>Negru</i> (<i>Black</i>), <i>Albu</i> (<i>White</i>). </p>
      <p>The most common Romanians surnames in 2007 were <a href="/wiki/Popa_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Popa (disambiguation)">Popa</a> (191,938 people), meaning <i>priest</i> and <a href="/wiki/Popescu" title="Popescu">Popescu</a> (147,784 people), meaning <i>priest's son/daughter</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Nume_la_români_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nume_la_români-15">[15]</a></sup> </p>
