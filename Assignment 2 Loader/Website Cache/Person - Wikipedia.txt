   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Person</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub" class="noprint">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div> 
    <div id="contentSub"></div> 
    <div id="jump-to-nav"></div> 
    <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a> 
    <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a> 
    <div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">
       A being that has certain capacities or attributes such as reason, morality, consciousness or self-consciousness
      </div> 
      <p class="mw-empty-elt"> </p> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       For other uses, see 
       <a href="/wiki/Person_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Person (disambiguation)">Person (disambiguation)</a>.
      </div> 
      <p>A <b>person</b> is a being that has certain capacities or attributes such as <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">reason</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morality" title="Morality">morality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Consciousness" title="Consciousness">consciousness</a> or <a href="/wiki/Self-consciousness" title="Self-consciousness">self-consciousness</a>, and being a part of a culturally established form of social relations such as <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ownership" title="Ownership">ownership</a> of <a href="/wiki/Property" title="Property">property</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Legal_obligation" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal obligation">legal responsibility</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The defining features of <a href="/wiki/Personhood" title="Personhood">personhood</a> and consequently what makes a person count as a person differ widely among cultures and contexts. </p>
      <p>In addition to the question of personhood, of what makes a being count as a person to begin with, there are further questions about <a href="/wiki/Personal_identity" title="Personal identity">personal identity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Self" title="Self">self</a>: both about what makes any particular person that particular person instead of another, and about what makes a person at one time the same person as they were or will be at another time despite any intervening changes. </p>
      <p>The common plural of "person", "<a href="/wiki/People" title="People">people</a>", is often used to refer to an entire <a href="/wiki/Nation" title="Nation">nation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnic group</a> (as in "a people"). The plural "persons" is often used in <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">legal</a> writing. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personhood">Personhood</span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Paul_Klee_WI_(In_Memoriam)_1938.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Paul_Klee_WI_%28In_Memoriam%29_1938.jpg/220px-Paul_Klee_WI_%28In_Memoriam%29_1938.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="240" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Paul_Klee_WI_%28In_Memoriam%29_1938.jpg/330px-Paul_Klee_WI_%28In_Memoriam%29_1938.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Paul_Klee_WI_%28In_Memoriam%29_1938.jpg/440px-Paul_Klee_WI_%28In_Memoriam%29_1938.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2216" data-file-height="2421"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Paul_Klee_WI_(In_Memoriam)_1938.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>An abstract painting of a person by 
         <a href="/wiki/Paul_Klee" title="Paul Klee">Paul Klee</a>. The concept of a person can be very challenging to define.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: 
       <a href="/wiki/Personhood" title="Personhood">Personhood</a>
      </div> 
      <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>The criteria for being a person... are designed to capture those attributes which are the subject of our most humane concern with ourselves and the source of what we regard as most important and most problematical in our lives.</p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        — 
        <cite>Harry G. Frankfurt</cite>
       </div> 
      </blockquote> 
      <p>Personhood is the status of being a person. Defining personhood is a controversial topic in <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>, and is closely tied to legal and <a href="/wiki/Political" class="mw-redirect" title="Political">political</a> concepts of <a href="/wiki/Citizenship" title="Citizenship">citizenship</a>, <a href="/wiki/Equality_before_the_law" title="Equality before the law">equality</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Liberty" title="Liberty">liberty</a>. According to common worldwide general legal practice, only a <a href="/wiki/Natural_person" title="Natural person">natural person</a> or <a href="/wiki/Legal_personality" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal personality">legal personality</a> has <a href="/wiki/Rights" title="Rights">rights</a>, protections, privileges, responsibilities, and <a href="/wiki/Legal_liability" title="Legal liability">legal liability</a>. Personhood continues to be a topic of international debate, and has been questioned during the abolition of <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slavery</a> and the fight for <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_rights" title="Women's rights">women's rights</a>, in debates about <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fetal_rights" title="Fetal rights">fetal rights</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Animal_rights" title="Animal rights">animal rights</a> advocacy.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Various debates have focused on questions about the personhood of different classes of entities. Historically, the personhood of animals, women, and slaves has been a catalyst of social upheaval. In most societies today, living adult humans are usually considered persons, but depending on the context, theory or definition, the category of "person" may be taken to include or not <a href="/wiki/Adultism" title="Adultism">children</a> or such <a href="/wiki/Non_human" class="mw-redirect" title="Non human">non-human</a> entities as <a href="/wiki/Animals" class="mw-redirect" title="Animals">animals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligences" class="mw-redirect" title="Artificial intelligences">artificial intelligences</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">extraterrestrial life</a>, as well as legal entities such as <a href="/wiki/Corporations" class="mw-redirect" title="Corporations">corporations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_states" class="mw-redirect" title="Sovereign states">sovereign states</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Polity" title="Polity">polities</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Estate_(law)" title="Estate (law)">estates</a> in <a href="/wiki/Probate" title="Probate">probate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_identity">Personal identity</span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:MontreGousset001.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/MontreGousset001.jpg/220px-MontreGousset001.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="192" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/MontreGousset001.jpg/330px-MontreGousset001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/MontreGousset001.jpg/440px-MontreGousset001.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="697"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:MontreGousset001.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>What does it take for individuals to persist from moment to moment – or in other words, for the same individual to exist at different moments?
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: 
       <a href="/wiki/Personal_identity" title="Personal identity">Personal identity</a>
      </div> 
      <p>Personal identity is the <a href="/wiki/Identity_(philosophy)" title="Identity (philosophy)">unique identity</a> of persons through time. That is to say, the necessary and sufficient conditions under which a person at one time and a person at another time can be said to be the <i>same</i> person, persisting through time. In the modern <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_mind" title="Philosophy of mind">philosophy of mind</a>, this concept of personal identity is sometimes referred to as the <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/diachronic" class="extiw" title="wikt:diachronic">diachronic</a></i> problem of personal identity. The <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/synchronic" class="extiw" title="wikt:synchronic">synchronic</a></i> problem is grounded in the question of what features or traits characterize a given person at one time. </p>
      <p>Identity is an issue for both <a href="/wiki/Continental_philosophy" title="Continental philosophy">continental philosophy</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Analytic_philosophy" title="Analytic philosophy">analytic philosophy</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A key question in continental philosophy is in what sense we can maintain the modern conception of identity, while realizing many of our prior assumptions about the world are incorrect.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>Proposed solutions to the problem of personal identity include continuity of the physical body, continuity of an immaterial mind or soul, continuity of consciousness or memory,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Bundle_theory" title="Bundle theory">bundle theory</a> of self,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> continuity of personality after the death of the physical body,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and proposals that there are actually no persons or selves who persist over time at all.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_the_concept">Development of the concept</span></h2> 
      <p>In ancient Rome, the word <i><a href="/wiki/Persona" title="Persona">persona</a></i> (Latin) or <i><a href="/wiki/Prosopon" title="Prosopon">prosopon</a></i> (πρόσωπον; Greek) originally referred to the masks worn by actors on stage. The various masks represented the various "personae" in the stage play.<sup id="cite_ref-CathEnc_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CathEnc-9">[9]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The concept of person was further developed during the <a href="/wiki/Trinitarianism" class="mw-redirect" title="Trinitarianism">Trinitarian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christology" title="Christology">Christological</a> debates of the 4th and 5th centuries in contrast to the word nature.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> During the theological debates, some philosophical tools (concepts) were needed so that the debates could be held on common basis to all theological schools. The purpose of the debate was to establish the relation, similarities and differences between the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc">Λóγος</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Ancient_Greek" class="mw-redirect" title="Romanization of Ancient Greek">romanized</a>:&nbsp;</small><i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek-language romanization">Lógos</i>/<i>Verbum</i> and God. The philosophical concept of person arose, taking the word "<a href="/wiki/Prosopon" title="Prosopon">prosopon</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc">πρόσωπον</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Ancient_Greek" class="mw-redirect" title="Romanization of Ancient Greek">romanized</a>:&nbsp;</small><i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek-language romanization">prósōpon</i>) from the <a href="/wiki/Theatre_of_ancient_Greece" title="Theatre of ancient Greece">Greek theatre</a>. Therefore, Christus (the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc">Λóγος</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Ancient_Greek" class="mw-redirect" title="Romanization of Ancient Greek">romanized</a>:&nbsp;</small><i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek-language romanization">Lógos</i>/<i>Verbum</i>) and God were defined as different "persons". This concept was applied later to the Holy Ghost, the angels and to all human beings. </p>
      <p>Since then, a number of important changes to the word's meaning and use have taken place, and attempts have been made to redefine the word with varying degrees of adoption and influence. </p>
