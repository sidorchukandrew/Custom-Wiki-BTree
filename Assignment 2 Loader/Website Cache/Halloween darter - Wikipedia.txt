      <p>The <b>Halloween darter</b> (<i>Percina crypta</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Freshwater_fish" title="Freshwater fish">freshwater fish</a> native to North America. It is found in <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> in the drainage basin of the <a href="/wiki/Apalachicola_River" title="Apalachicola River">Apalachicola River</a>, specifically in the <a href="/wiki/Flint_River_(Georgia)" title="Flint River (Georgia)">Flint River</a> system and the <a href="/wiki/Chattahoochee_River" title="Chattahoochee River">Chattahoochee River</a> system. It prefers shallow, fast-flowing areas with gravel bottoms in small and medium-sized rivers. It was first <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> in 2008, having not previously been distinguished from the <a href="/wiki/Blackbanded_darter" title="Blackbanded darter">blackbanded darter</a> (<i>P.&nbsp;nigrofasciata</i>), which occurs in the same watershed. The color is somewhat variable, being generally blackish dorsally, with some individuals having indistinct saddle-like barring. Males have orange and dark lateral striping while females have dark stripes and a yellowish-green belly. At a maximum <a href="/wiki/Fish_measurement" title="Fish measurement">standard length</a> of 101&nbsp;mm (4&nbsp;in), males are slightly larger than females, and both sexes develop distinctive orange barring on the edge of the first dorsal fin during the breeding season. </p>
      <p>The Halloween darter has a limited range and a fragmented distribution. The main threat faced by the species is a deterioration in the quality of its habitat. It has been classified as a "<a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">vulnerable species</a>" by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a>. It has been listed as "<a href="/wiki/Threatened" class="mw-redirect" title="Threatened">threatened</a>" in Georgia by the <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Department_of_Natural_Resources" title="Georgia Department of Natural Resources">Georgia Department of Natural Resources</a>, and is protected in Alabama under the Nongame Species Regulation of the <a href="/wiki/Alabama_Department_of_Conservation_and_Natural_Resources" title="Alabama Department of Conservation and Natural Resources">Alabama Department of Conservation and Natural Resources</a>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halloween_darter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The Halloween darter was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> as a new species in 2008 by Mary C. Freeman, Byron J. Freeman, and Noel M. Burkhead. The <a href="/wiki/Holotype" title="Holotype">holotype</a> had been collected in 1994 by B. J. Freeman, J. Devivo, J. W. Garrett, M. J. Zieg, R. E. Jenkins, J. S. Boyce, L. M. Hartle, and M. Flood from the <a href="/wiki/Chattahoochee_River" title="Chattahoochee River">Chattahoochee River</a> near <a href="/wiki/Sautee_Nacoochee,_Georgia" title="Sautee Nacoochee, Georgia">Sautee Nacoochee, Georgia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nora_Mill_Dam" title="Nora Mill Dam">Nora Mill Dam</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Specific_epithet_(zoology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Specific epithet (zoology)">species name</a> <i>crypta</i> is from the Greek root <i>crypt</i>, meaning "hidden" or "concealed". The authors chose this name to reference how the Halloween darter's existence as a separate species was concealed by its similar appearance to the <a href="/wiki/Blackbanded_darter" title="Blackbanded darter">blackbanded darter</a> (<i>P.&nbsp;nigrofasciata</i>); prior to the Halloween darter's description, it was thought that the blackbanded darter was the only species of <i><a href="/wiki/Percina" title="Percina">Percina</a></i> found in the area of the <a href="/wiki/Apalachicola_River" title="Apalachicola River">Apalachicola River</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">common name</a> "Halloween darter" was chosen to allude to the orange and black breeding coloration of individuals, as these colors are associated with the Western holiday <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Freeman_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Based on <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_DNA" title="Nuclear DNA">nuclear</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a>, the Halloween darter and blackbanded darter form a <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> with nine other members of <i>Percina</i>, identified as the <a href="/wiki/Subgenus" title="Subgenus">subgenus</a> <i>Hadropterus</i>. The other members of <i>Hadropterus</i> are the <a href="/wiki/Guadalupe_darter" title="Guadalupe darter">Guadalupe darter</a> (<i>P.&nbsp;apristis</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Goldline_darter" title="Goldline darter">goldline darter</a> (<i>P.&nbsp;aurolineata</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Dusky_darter" title="Dusky darter">dusky darter</a> (<i>P.&nbsp;sciera</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_darter" title="Bronze darter">bronze darter</a> (<i>P.&nbsp;palmaris</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Percina_kusha" title="Percina kusha">bridled darter</a> (<i>P.&nbsp;kusha</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Muscadine_darter" title="Muscadine darter">muscadine darter</a> (<i>P.&nbsp;smithvanizi</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Bankhead_darter" title="Bankhead darter">Bankhead darter</a> (<i>P.&nbsp;sipsi</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Freckled_darter" title="Freckled darter">freckled darter</a> (<i>P.&nbsp;lenticula</i>), and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Percina_westfalli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Percina westfalli (page does not exist)">Westfall's darter</a> (<i>P.&nbsp;westfalli</i>). The clade is a <a href="/wiki/Polytomy" title="Polytomy">polytomy</a>, however, meaning that clearer evolutionary relationships among members was not discernible based on the utilized methods.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> A 2018 study found no genetic distinction between the Halloween darter and Westfall's darter in the Chattahoochee and Flint Rivers.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The latter, described in 1942 from Florida,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> is sometimes considered a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of the blackbanded darter.<sup id="cite_ref-Freeman_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman-2">[2]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halloween_darter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Percina_nigrofasciata_FWS_20933.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Percina_nigrofasciata_FWS_20933.jpg/220px-Percina_nigrofasciata_FWS_20933.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Percina_nigrofasciata_FWS_20933.jpg/330px-Percina_nigrofasciata_FWS_20933.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Percina_nigrofasciata_FWS_20933.jpg/440px-Percina_nigrofasciata_FWS_20933.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2848"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Percina_nigrofasciata_FWS_20933.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>The closely related 
         <a href="/wiki/Blackbanded_darter" title="Blackbanded darter">blackbanded darter</a>, which is similar in appearance to the Halloween darter.

      <p>Halloween darters have terete (cylindrical or slightly tapering) bodies, with males longer than females. Males have a maximum <a href="/wiki/Fish_measurement" title="Fish measurement">standard length</a> of 101&nbsp;mm (4.0&nbsp;in), while females have a maximum standard length of 85&nbsp;mm (3.3&nbsp;in). Males and females also vary in coloration. Males' heads range in color from a dark, bluish-gray to black, with specks of iridescent green or gold concentrated on their cheeks and opercles (protective coverings of the gills), though also present elsewhere on the head. Its eyes are darkly colored, and the preorbital bar (band of color on the preorbital, or in front of the eye sockets) is black. Both the chin and the lower jaw are bluish-black. Its sides are pale orange with blackish lateral stripes, which may be tinged with iridescent green. Its back is either black or, when possessing distinct saddles, amber or orange with black saddles.<sup id="cite_ref-Freeman_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Females have dark olive-black tops of the head, with snouts that are a similar color but paler. Females have variable coloration of the cheek and opercle. They can be medium olive-black with yellow crescent-shaped markings or instead with pale yellow <a href="/wiki/Stippling" title="Stippling">stippling</a> (small dots) or mottling. Similar to males, they have iridescent green or gold speckling, though the speckles are concentrated on the opercles or <a href="/wiki/Cleithrum" title="Cleithrum">cleithrum</a> (bone that extends from pectoral fin up to the cranium above the gills). Females' eyes are also dark, and their upper and lower jaws range in color from olive-black to pale yellow. Their abdomens are pale yellowish-green, and their bellies are tinted pink. They also have olive-black lateral stripes and dorsal saddles; the color between the dorsal saddles is amber to pale yellow.<sup id="cite_ref-Freeman_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The Halloween darter can be distinguished from other members of its genus by possessing all the following traits: the branchiostegal membranes (membranes that connect the branchiostegal rays, which support the gill membranes) are slightly connected; the edge of the preopercle (bone between cheek and gill membrane) is not serrated; it usually has seven saddle-shaped color bands on its back; and males and females in breeding coloration have yellowish-orange or orange bands near the edge of their first dorsal fins.<sup id="cite_ref-Freeman_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman-2">[2]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halloween_darter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The Halloween darter is <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> to the watershed of the Apalachicola River. This is formed from the confluence of two rivers, the <a href="/wiki/Flint_River_(Georgia)" title="Flint River (Georgia)">Flint River</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Chattahoochee_River" title="Chattahoochee River">Chattahoochee River</a>. In the Flint River basin, the fish occurs both above and below the <a href="/wiki/Fall_line" title="Fall line">fall line</a>, being present in several tributaries including <a href="/wiki/Potato_Creek_(Flint_River_tributary)" title="Potato Creek (Flint River tributary)">Potato Creek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lazer_Creek" title="Lazer Creek">Lazer Creek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muckalee_Creek" title="Muckalee Creek">Muckalee Creek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ichawaynochaway_Creek" title="Ichawaynochaway Creek">Ichawaynochaway Creek</a>. In the Chattahoochee River basin, it occurs above <a href="/wiki/Lake_Lanier" title="Lake Lanier">Lake Lanier</a> in the Appalachian foothills of northeast Georgia, in the Chattahoochee itself, in the <a href="/wiki/Chestatee_River" title="Chestatee River">Chestatee River</a> and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sautee_Creek&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sautee Creek (page does not exist)">Sautee Creek</a>. It is also present in the <a href="/wiki/Uchee_Creek_(Georgia)" title="Uchee Creek (Georgia)">Uchee Creek</a>, a tributary that joins the Chattahoochee River near the fall line in Alabama.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">[1]</a></sup> This fish is typically found in fast-flowing sections of small and medium-sized rivers over bottoms composed of gravel, cobble or rock, often in areas where <a href="/wiki/Podostemum_ceratophyllum" title="Podostemum ceratophyllum">hornleaf riverweed</a> (<i>Podostemum ceratophyllum</i>) grows.<sup id="cite_ref-GA_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GA-6">[6]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology_and_ecology">Biology and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halloween_darter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Biology and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Medionidus_penicillatus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Medionidus_penicillatus.jpg/220px-Medionidus_penicillatus.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="219" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Medionidus_penicillatus.jpg/330px-Medionidus_penicillatus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Medionidus_penicillatus.jpg/440px-Medionidus_penicillatus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1416" data-file-height="1412"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">

          <a href="/wiki/File:Medionidus_penicillatus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>Shell of the 
         <a href="/wiki/Medionidus_penicillatus" title="Medionidus penicillatus">gulf moccasinshell</a> (
         <i>Medionidus penicillatus</i>), which uses the Halloween darter as a host for its larvae
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>It is <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a>, consuming a variety of aquatic insects such as <a href="/wiki/Mayfly" title="Mayfly">mayfly</a> nymphs and larvae of <a href="/wiki/Midge" title="Midge">midges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_fly" title="Black fly">black flies</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Caddisfly" title="Caddisfly">caddisflies</a>. Reproduction occurs in the spring, with <a href="/wiki/Spawn_(biology)" title="Spawn (biology)">spawning</a> occurring in April and May, probably in fast-flowing <a href="/wiki/Riffle" title="Riffle">riffle</a> habitats. Females have a clutch size of 23–335 eggs; clutch size increases with female body size. Ripe egg cells are approximately 1.5&nbsp;mm in diameter. Offspring hatch in June, with juveniles growing to more than half the maximum adult length by October. They reach sexual maturity at one year old. Lifespans likely do not exceed three years.<sup id="cite_ref-GA_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GA-6">[6]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The Halloween darter is a <a href="/wiki/Host_(biology)" title="Host (biology)">host</a> species for US federally endangered species of freshwater mussel, the <a href="/wiki/Medionidus_penicillatus" title="Medionidus penicillatus">gulf moccasinshell</a>. This mussel releases larvae called <a href="/wiki/Glochidium" title="Glochidium">glochidia</a> that lodge in the <a href="/wiki/Gill" title="Gill">gills</a> of suitable fish to develop into juveniles. Of twenty-four fish species in seven families tested as possible hosts for the gulf moccasinshell's larvae, successful <a href="/wiki/Metamorphosis" title="Metamorphosis">metamorphosis</a> of the larvae only occurred in the Halloween darter and three other darter species.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The Halloween darter is also considered a moderately successful host for the larvae of the <a href="/wiki/Purple_bankclimber" title="Purple bankclimber">purple bankclimber</a> mussel, with a metamorphosis success rate of approximately 34%.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_and_threats">Status and threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halloween_darter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Status and threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:USACE_West_Point_Dam_and_Lake.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/USACE_West_Point_Dam_and_Lake.jpg/220px-USACE_West_Point_Dam_and_Lake.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/USACE_West_Point_Dam_and_Lake.jpg/330px-USACE_West_Point_Dam_and_Lake.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/USACE_West_Point_Dam_and_Lake.jpg/440px-USACE_West_Point_Dam_and_Lake.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="1000"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:USACE_West_Point_Dam_and_Lake.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>The dam of 
         <a href="/wiki/West_Point_Lake" title="West Point Lake">West Point Lake</a> on the 
         <a href="/wiki/Chattahoochee_River" title="Chattahoochee River">Chattahoochee River</a>; dams along the watershed disrupt population 
         <a href="/wiki/Landscape_connectivity" title="Landscape connectivity">connectivity</a> of the Halloween darter
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>This fish has several separate populations, and a total area of occupancy of less than 2,000&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup> (772&nbsp;sq&nbsp;mi). The chief threat it faces is a deterioration in the quality of its habitat. It typically occurs in deep, fast-flowing sections of waterway, and damming of the rivers has altered the hydrological conditions and siltation.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">[1]</a></sup> In the US state of Georgia, its four main populations are isolated from each other due to artificial reservoirs. If any of the four populations were to become extinct, recolonization could not occur naturally from any of the other three.<sup id="cite_ref-GA_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GA-6">[6]</a></sup> Additionally, increased urbanization, water extraction and waste treatment are affecting the water quality of its habitat. For these reasons, the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> has assessed the conservation status of this fish as being "<a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">vulnerable</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 2010, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Fish_and_Wildlife_Service" title="United States Fish and Wildlife Service">United States Fish and Wildlife Service</a> was petitioned by a coalition of environmental organizations (Alabama Rivers Alliance, <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Biological_Diversity" title="Center for Biological Diversity">Center for Biological Diversity</a>, Clinch Coalition, <a href="/wiki/Dogwood_Alliance" title="Dogwood Alliance">Dogwood Alliance</a>, Gulf Restoration Network, Tennessee Forests Council, West Virginia Highlands Conservancy and activists Tierra Curry and Noah Greenwald) to list 404 aquatic species under the <a href="/wiki/Endangered_Species_Act_of_1973" title="Endangered Species Act of 1973">Endangered Species Act of 1973</a>, including the Halloween darter. They included the Halloween darter based on the perceived threats of habitat destruction, as well as insufficient regulatory measures currently in place to protect the species.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It is not included on the US Endangered Species Act as of 2019, and its listing status is under review.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In Georgia, the Halloween darter is listed as a threatened species.<sup id="cite_ref-GA_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GA-6">[6]</a></sup> In Alabama, it is protected under the Nongame Species Regulation of the <a href="/wiki/Alabama_Department_of_Conservation_and_Natural_Resources" title="Alabama Department of Conservation and Natural Resources">Alabama Department of Conservation and Natural Resources</a>. This makes it illegal to capture, trade, or kill the fish without a permit.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> </p>
