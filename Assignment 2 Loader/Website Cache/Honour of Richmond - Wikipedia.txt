      <p>The <b>Honour of Richmond</b> (or <a href="/wiki/English_feudal_barony" title="English feudal barony">English feudal barony</a> of Richmond) in north-west <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> was granted to Count <a href="/wiki/Alan_Rufus" title="Alan Rufus">Alan Rufus</a> (also known as Alain le Roux) by King <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="William I of England">William the Conqueror</a> sometime during 1069 to 1071, although the date is uncertain. It was gifted as thanks for his services at the Conquest. The extensive district was previously <a href="/wiki/Feudal_land_tenure_in_England" title="Feudal land tenure in England">held</a> by <a href="/wiki/Edwin,_Earl_of_Mercia" title="Edwin, Earl of Mercia">Edwin, Earl of Mercia</a> who died in 1071. The district is probably mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> of 1086 but its limits are uncertain.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Honour_(feudal_barony)" class="mw-redirect" title="Honour (feudal barony)">honour</a> comprised 60 <a href="/wiki/Knight%27s_fee" title="Knight's fee">knight's fees</a> and was one of the most important <a href="/wiki/Fiefdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiefdom">fiefdoms</a> in <a href="/wiki/Norman_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman dynasty">Norman</a> <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Butler_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Butler-2">[2]</a></sup> According to the 14th-century Genealogia of the lords of Richmond, Alan Rufus built a stronghold in the district. The buildings were later known as <a href="/wiki/Richmond_Castle" title="Richmond Castle">Richmond Castle</a> which is alluded to in the Domesday survey as forming a ‘castlery’.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>The district consisted of three main land divisions; the <a href="/wiki/Wapentakes" class="mw-redirect" title="Wapentakes">wapentakes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hang_West" title="Hang West">Hang</a>, of Gilling and of Hallikeld. The first two of these correspond to later medieval civil land divisions or wapentakes: the third is less easily defined. </p>
      <p>The Gilling territory consisted mainly of land which lay between the <a href="/wiki/River_Tees" title="River Tees">River Tees</a> and the <a href="/wiki/River_Swale" title="River Swale">River Swale</a>, with the Tees forming the northern border which separated the land from that granted to the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Durham" title="Bishop of Durham">Bishop of Durham</a>. The western border was the <a href="/wiki/Drainage_divide" title="Drainage divide">watershed</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">Pennine Hills</a> and the southern border was the watershed between the <a href="/wiki/River_Ure" title="River Ure">River Ure</a> and the Swale. The <a href="/wiki/River_Wiske" title="River Wiske">River Wiske</a> formed the eastern border. The manor of <a href="/wiki/Gilling" title="Gilling">Gilling</a>, close to the boundary, was the <i><a href="/wiki/Caput" title="Caput">caput</a></i> of the barony until Count Alan moved it to <a href="/wiki/Richmond_Castle" title="Richmond Castle">Richmond Castle</a>. The division of Hang, or Hangshire, had the River Swale as its northern boundary; its western boundary was the Pennine watershed and its southern boundary was the watershed with the <a href="/wiki/River_Wharfe" title="River Wharfe">River Wharfe</a> and the <a href="/wiki/River_Nidd" title="River Nidd">River Nidd</a>. The eastern border followed small streams and minor landmarks from the previous watershed to the Swale. The wapentake meeting place was situated on the Hang Beck in <a href="/wiki/Finghall" title="Finghall">Finghall</a> parish. The third part of the territory, Hallikeld, consisted of the parishes lying between the <a href="/wiki/River_Ure" title="River Ure">River Ure</a> and the River Swale until their <a href="/wiki/Confluence" title="Confluence">confluence</a> at <a href="/wiki/Ellenthorpe" title="Ellenthorpe">Ellenthorpe</a>. </p>
      <p>The Honour of Richmond, being 60&nbsp;km (37&nbsp;mi) from east to west and 45&nbsp;km (28&nbsp;mi) from north to south, comprised most of the land between the River Tees and the River Ure and ranged in its landscape from the bleak mountainous areas of the Pennines to the fertile lowlands of the <a href="/wiki/Vale_of_York" title="Vale of York">Vale of York</a>. </p>
      <p>Richmond castle was in ruins by 1540 but was restored centuries later and is now a tourist attraction.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_feudal_barons_of_Richmond">List of feudal barons of Richmond</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honour_of_Richmond&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: List of feudal barons of Richmond">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The feudal barons of Richmond were usually referred to as Lords of Richmond. The Honour of Richmond was sometimes held separately from the titles <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Richmond" title="Earl of Richmond">Earl of Richmond</a>, and later <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Richmond" title="Duke of Richmond">Duke of Richmond</a>. Grants were sometimes partial, and sometimes included or excluded Richmond Castle as noted in the list below. The descent of the barony was as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li><a href="/wiki/Alan_Rufus" title="Alan Rufus">Alan Rufus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Count_of_Brittany" class="mw-redirect" title="Count of Brittany">Count of Brittany</a> (d.1093), who died without progeny.</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Alan_the_Black" title="Alan the Black">Alan the Black</a> (d.1098), brother of Alan Rufus, who also died without progeny.</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Stephen,_Count_of_Tr%C3%A9guier" title="Stephen, Count of Tréguier">Stephen, Count of Tréguier</a> (d. 1135/6), brother.</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Alan,_1st_Earl_of_Richmond" title="Alan, 1st Earl of Richmond">Alan, 1st Earl of Richmond</a> (d.1146), (<i>alias</i> Alan The Black, or <i>le Noir</i>), younger son.</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Conan_IV,_Duke_of_Brittany" title="Conan IV, Duke of Brittany">Conan IV, Duke of Brittany</a> (d.1171), son, who left as his heir a daughter Constance (d.1201),<sup id="cite_ref-Sanders,_p.140_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanders,_p.140-6">[6]</a></sup> a ward of the king.</li> 
       <li>Held in wardship by King <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II</a> pending the marriage of his son Geoffrey to Constance, the daughter of Conan IV</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Constance,_Duchess_of_Brittany" title="Constance, Duchess of Brittany">Constance, Duchess of Brittany</a> (d.1201),<sup id="cite_ref-Sanders,_p.140_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanders,_p.140-6">[6]</a></sup> Conan's daughter and hereditary Countess of Richmond</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_II,_Duke_of_Brittany" title="Geoffrey II, Duke of Brittany">Geoffrey II, Duke of Brittany</a>(d.1186) first husband of Constance<sup id="cite_ref-Sanders,_p.140_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanders,_p.140-6">[6]</a></sup></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Ranulf_de_Blondeville,_6th_Earl_of_Chester" title="Ranulf de Blondeville, 6th Earl of Chester">Ranulph, Earl of Chester</a> (d.1232), second husband of Constance, whom she married in 1188 and whom she divorced in 1199.<sup id="cite_ref-Sanders,_p.140_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanders,_p.140-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[a]</a></sup></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Guy_of_Thouars" title="Guy of Thouars">Guy of Thouars</a> – third husband of Constance; forfeited to King John in 1203<sup id="cite_ref-Sanders,_p.140_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanders,_p.140-6">[6]</a></sup> upon taking up arms with the French to avenge the death of Arthur I</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Alix,_Duchess_of_Brittany" title="Alix, Duchess of Brittany">Alix, Duchess of Brittany</a> (d. 1221), Constance and Guy's elder daughter.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[b]</a></sup> 
        <ul>
         <li><a href="/wiki/Eleanor,_Fair_Maid_of_Brittany" title="Eleanor, Fair Maid of Brittany">Eleanor of Brittany</a>, Constance and Geoffrey's elder daughter.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[c]</a></sup> She ceased to be styled as Countess of Richmond in 1218.</li>
        </ul></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Peter_I,_Duke_of_Brittany" title="Peter I, Duke of Brittany">Peter I, Duke of Brittany</a> (alias <i>Pierre Mauclerc</i>), the husband of Alix of Thouars and the son-in-law of Guy of Thouars and Constance of Brittany. He declined an offer of the Honour and the Earldom of Richmond from King John (1199–1216) due to his loyalty to the French King.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> He received in 1218 from <a href="/wiki/William_Marshal,_1st_Earl_of_Pembroke" title="William Marshal, 1st Earl of Pembroke">William Marshal, 1st Earl of Pembroke</a> in name of King <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_England" title="Henry III of England">Henry III</a> a partial grant of the Honour, excepting 30 knight's fees south of the River Trent retained by the king,<sup id="cite_ref-Sanders,_p.140_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanders,_p.140-6">[6]</a></sup> His lands were forfeited to King Henry III when he paid homage to French King Louis IX. Thus his tenure of the barony varied according to his allegiance in the war between England and France.<sup id="cite_ref-Sanders,_p.140_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanders,_p.140-6">[6]</a></sup></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Peter_II,_Count_of_Savoy" title="Peter II, Count of Savoy">Peter II, Count of Savoy</a>, popularly known as Earl of Richmond, to whom the barony was granted by King Henry III in 1240.<sup id="cite_ref-Sanders,_p.140_6-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanders,_p.140-6">[6]</a></sup> He sought and received a royal confirmation of his holding in 1262. He had felt his tenure at risk due to the negotiations for the marriage of Beatrice, daughter of King Henry III to <a href="/wiki/John_II,_Duke_of_Brittany" title="John II, Duke of Brittany">John</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/John_I,_Duke_of_Brittany" title="John I, Duke of Brittany">John I, Duke of Brittany</a> (d.1286), a part of which marriage settlement stipulated that his father the duke should receive French lands equal in value to the honour of Richmond.<sup id="cite_ref-Sanders,_p.140_6-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanders,_p.140-6">[6]</a></sup> He lost control of the barony in 1264, although he received income from it until 1266, when the barony was granted to John of Brittany, husband of Beatrice.<sup id="cite_ref-Sanders,_p.140_6-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanders,_p.140-6">[6]</a></sup></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/John_II,_Duke_of_Brittany" title="John II, Duke of Brittany">John II, Duke of Brittany</a> (d.1305), husband of Princess Beatrice.<sup id="cite_ref-Sanders,_p.140_6-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanders,_p.140-6">[6]</a></sup></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/John_of_Brittany,_Earl_of_Richmond" title="John of Brittany, Earl of Richmond">John of Brittany, Earl of Richmond</a> (d.1334), son. He died unmarried without progeny.<sup id="cite_ref-Sanders,_p.140_6-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanders,_p.140-6">[6]</a></sup></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/John_III,_Duke_of_Brittany" title="John III, Duke of Brittany">John III, Duke of Brittany</a> (1286–1341), nephew</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/John_of_Montfort" title="John of Montfort">John de Montfort, Earl of Richmond</a> – upon his death, Earldom returned to the crown in 1342; it remained with the crown during most of the <a href="/wiki/Breton_War_of_Succession" class="mw-redirect" title="Breton War of Succession">Breton War of Succession</a> in part to ensure it would not fall into the hands of the King of France.</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/John_of_Gaunt" title="John of Gaunt">John of Gaunt</a> – surrendered the Earldom and Honour, at the insistence of the King, to pursue Kingship of Castille</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/John_IV,_Duke_of_Brittany" title="John IV, Duke of Brittany">John IV, Duke of Brittany</a>, son and heir of John of Montfort; forfeited twice, in 1381 (due to the First Treaty of Guerande) and 1384 upon paying homage to French King <a href="/wiki/Charles_VI_of_France" title="Charles VI of France">Charles VI</a>; the second forfeit represented the permanent loss of the Honour and the Earldom by the Dukes of Brittany.</li> 
       <li>Anne of Bohemia</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Ralph_de_Neville,_1st_Earl_of_Westmorland" class="mw-redirect" title="Ralph de Neville, 1st Earl of Westmorland">Ralph de Neville, 1st Earl of Westmorland</a> – Honour held for life without peerage Earl of Richmond</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/John_of_Lancaster,_1st_Duke_of_Bedford" title="John of Lancaster, 1st Duke of Bedford">John of Lancaster, 1st Duke of Bedford</a> and 1st Earl of Richmond</li> 
       <li>reverted to the Crown (1435–1450)</li> 
       <li>Ralph Neville, Earl of Westmoreland – partial grant of Richmond Castle in 1450</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Edmund_Tudor,_1st_Earl_of_Richmond" title="Edmund Tudor, 1st Earl of Richmond">Edmund Tudor, 1st Earl of Richmond</a> – father of Henry VII</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry Tudor</a> – forfeited to Edward IV</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/George_Plantagenet,_1st_Duke_of_Clarence" title="George Plantagenet, 1st Duke of Clarence">George Plantagenet, 1st Duke of Clarence</a> – granted Honour and castle without peerage Earl of Richmond by Edward IV in 1462</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Richard_III_of_England" title="Richard III of England">Richard, Duke of Gloucester</a> – retained upon becoming <a href="/wiki/Richard_III_of_England" title="Richard III of England">Richard III of England</a></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry Tudor</a> – regained the Honour upon winning the crown of England in contest with Richard III; Honour merged with the Crown</li> 
       <li>Held by members of Tudor and Stuart Dynasties</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Charles_Lennox,_1st_Duke_of_Richmond" title="Charles Lennox, 1st Duke of Richmond">Charles Lennox</a> – conferred on Lennox Family in 1675 by Charles Lennox's father <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a></li>
      </ul>
