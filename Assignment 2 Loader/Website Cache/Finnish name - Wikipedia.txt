   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Finnish name</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub" class="noprint">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div> 
    <div id="contentSub"></div> 
    <div id="jump-to-nav"></div> 
    <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a> 
    <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a> 
    <div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>In <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, a person must have a <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> and can have up to three <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given names</a>. Surnames are inherited either patrilineally or matrilineally, while given names are usually chosen by a person's parents. <b>Finnish names</b> come from a variety of dissimilar traditions that were consolidated only in the early 20th century. The first national act on names came into force in 1921, and it made surnames mandatory. Between 1930 and 1985, the Western Finnish tradition whereby a married woman took her husband's surname was mandatory. Previously in Eastern Finland, this was not necessarily the case. </p>
      <p>Finnish given names are often of Christian origin (e.g., <i>Jukka</i> from Greek <a href="/wiki/Johannes" title="Johannes">Johannes</a>), but Finnish and <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> origins are also common. </p>
      <p>In Finnish, the letter "j" denotes the <a href="/wiki/Approximant" class="mw-redirect" title="Approximant">approximant</a> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Finnish" title="Help:IPA/Finnish">[j]</a></span>, as in English <i>you</i>. For example, the two different names <i>Maria</i> and <i>Marja</i> are pronounced nearly identically. The letter "y" denotes the vowel <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[y]</span>, not found in English, but similar to German "ü" and French "u". "R" is rolled. The stress is always on the first syllable in Finnish. For example, <i>Yrjö Kääriäinen</i> is pronounced <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Finnish" title="Help:IPA/Finnish">[ˈyrjø ˈkæːri.æinen]</a></span>. Double letters always stand for a geminate or longer sound (e.g., <i>Marjaana</i> has a stressed short <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Finnish" title="Help:IPA/Finnish">[ɑ]</a></span> followed by an unstressed long <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Finnish" title="Help:IPA/Finnish">[ɑː]</a></span>). </p>
      <p>Pronunciation of Swedish names is similar, but long vowels are not doubled and the stress may be on any syllable. Finland has a long bilingual history and it is not unusual for Finnish speakers to have Swedish surnames or given names. Such names may be pronounced according to Finland–Swedish phonology or, depending on the person named, the person speaking and the language used, a <a href="/wiki/Fennicize" class="mw-redirect" title="Fennicize">Fennicized</a> variant. </p>
      <p>When writing Finnish names without the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_alphabet" class="mw-redirect" title="Finnish alphabet">Finnish alphabet</a> available (such as in e-mail addresses), the letters "ä" and "ö" are usually replaced with "a" and "o", respectively (e.g., <i>Pääkkönen</i> as <i>Paakkonen</i>). This is not the same, but visually recognizable. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surnames">Surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finnish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Kiianmies_Kyander_graves_Tampere_20090818.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Kiianmies_Kyander_graves_Tampere_20090818.JPG/220px-Kiianmies_Kyander_graves_Tampere_20090818.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Kiianmies_Kyander_graves_Tampere_20090818.JPG/330px-Kiianmies_Kyander_graves_Tampere_20090818.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Kiianmies_Kyander_graves_Tampere_20090818.JPG/440px-Kiianmies_Kyander_graves_Tampere_20090818.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Kiianmies_Kyander_graves_Tampere_20090818.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>

      <p>Finland has three predominant surname traditions: the <a href="/w/index.php?title=West_Finnish&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="West Finnish (page does not exist)">West Finnish</a>, the <a href="/wiki/East_Finnish" title="East Finnish">East Finnish</a> and that of the Swedish <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">nobility</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">bourgeoisie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a>. Until the early 20th century, Finland was a predominantly <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agrarian</a> society and the names of West <a href="/wiki/Finnish_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Finnish people">Finns</a> were based on their association with a particular area, <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farm</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Smallholding" title="Smallholding">homestead</a> (e.g. <i>Jaakko Jussila</i> 'Jaakko from the farm of Jussi'). Farm names typically had the suffix <i>-la</i>, <i>-lä</i>, and could refer to the husband (like <i>Jussila</i>) or describe the location (e.g. <i>Isoaho</i> 'large clearing'). This name could change every time the person moved to a different farm. Also, even if one had a surname, one would still be better known by the farm name. Farm names, patronyms and village names could be used to disambiguate between different people, but they were not true inherited surnames. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Aleksis_Kivi" title="Aleksis Kivi">Aleksis Kivi</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Seven_Brothers" class="mw-redirect" title="Seven Brothers">Seven Brothers</a></i> (1870) the character <i>Juhani</i> was officially summoned as <i>Juhani Juhanin-poika Jukola, Toukolan kylästä</i>, in English 'Juhani, son of Juhani, from Jukola farm, Toukola village'. </p>
      <p>On the other hand, the East Finnish surname tradition dates back to the 13th century. There, the <a href="/wiki/Savonian_people" title="Savonian people">Savonians</a> pursued <a href="/wiki/Slash-and-burn" title="Slash-and-burn">slash-and-burn</a> agriculture which necessitated moving several times during a person's lifetime. This in turn required the families to have surnames, which were in wide use among the common folk as early as the 13th century. By the mid-16th century, the East Finnish surnames had become hereditary. Typically, the oldest East Finnish surnames were formed from the first names of the patriarchs of the families (e.g. <i>Ikävalko</i>, <i>Termonen</i>, <i>Pentikäinen</i>). In the 16th, 17th and 18th centuries, new names were most often formed by adding the place name of the former or current place of living (e.g., <i>Puumalainen</i> &lt; <a href="/wiki/Puumala" title="Puumala">Puumala</a>). In the East Finnish tradition, women carried the family name of their fathers in feminine form indicated by the <i>-tar</i> suffix (e.g. <i>Puumalatar</i> &lt; <i>Puumalainen</i>). By the 19th century, this practice fell into disuse due to the influence of Western European surname tradition. Also, women did not change their surnames with marriage. </p>
      <p>In 1921, surnames became compulsory for all Finns. At this point, if there was no surname, the homestead names were usually adopted as surnames. Because the inhabitants often included farmhands and other non-family, holders of the same surname are not necessarily genetically related. A typical feature of such names is the addition of prefixes <i>Ala-</i> or <i>Ali-</i> ('Lower') and <i>Ylä-</i> or <i>Yli-</i> ('Upper'), giving the location of the holding along a waterway in relation of the main holding (e.g. <i>Yli-Ojanperä</i>, <i>Ala-Verronen</i>). In <a href="/wiki/Ostrobothnia_(historical_province)" title="Ostrobothnia (historical province)">Pohjanmaa</a>, there are similar prefixes <i>Rinta-</i> and <i>Latva-</i> ('downstream' and 'upstream' respectively). </p>
      <p>Common suffixes are <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-nen#Finnish" class="extiw" title="wikt:-nen">-nen</a></i> (in oblique form <i>-se-</i>; e.g., Miettinen&nbsp;: Miettisen "Miettinen's"), a <a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">diminutive</a> suffix usually meaning "small", and <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-la#Finnish" class="extiw" title="wikt:-la">-la</a></i>/<i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-l%C3%A4#Finnish" class="extiw" title="wikt:-lä">-lä</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Locative" class="mw-redirect" title="Locative">locative</a> suffix usually meaning "place of". The <i>-nen</i> suffix was freely interchanged with <i>-son</i> or <i>-poika</i> as late as the 16th century, but its meaning was ambiguous as it could refer not only to a "son", but any member of a patriarch's family, a farm or even a place. For example the surname <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuomonen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tuomonen (page does not exist)">Tuomonen</a> could mean "Son of Tuomo" or "Farm of Tuomo" or something else belonging to Tuomo. </p>
      <p>A third tradition of surnames was introduced in Finland by the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>-speaking upper and middle classes which used typical German and Swedish surnames. By custom, all Finnish-speaking persons who were able to get a position of some status in urban or learned society, discarded their Finnish name, adopting a Swedish, German or (in case of clergy) <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> surname.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In the case of <a href="/wiki/Swedish_allotment_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Swedish allotment system">enlisted soldiers</a>, the new name was given regardless of the wishes of the individual. The oldest noble surnames of Swedish origin were not original, but were derived from the charges in the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a>, sigil and flag of the family, for example with <i>Svärd</i> (Swedish: "sword"), <i>Kurki/Kurck</i> (Finnish: "crane") and <i>Kirves</i> (Finnish: "axe").<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Families of German origin would use the <i>von</i> suffix (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Von_Wright" title="Von Wright">von Wright</a></i>). </p>
      <p>In the late 19th and early 20th century, the overall modernization process and especially, the political movement of <a href="/wiki/Fennicization" class="mw-redirect" title="Fennicization">Fennicization</a> caused a movement for adoption of Finnish surnames. At that time, many persons with a Swedish or otherwise foreign surname changed their family name to a Finnish one. The features of nature with endings <i>-o/ö</i>, <i>-nen</i> (<i>Meriö</i> &lt; <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/meri#Finnish" class="extiw" title="wikt:meri">meri</a></i> "sea", <i>Nieminen</i> &lt; <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/niemi#Finnish" class="extiw" title="wikt:niemi">niemi</a></i> "peninsula") are typical of the names of this era, as well as more or less direct translations of Swedish names (<i>Helleranta</i> &lt; <i>Hällstrand</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Fennicizing one's name also concealed non-Finnish origin.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Martti_Ahtisaari" title="Martti Ahtisaari">Martti Ahtisaari</a>'s grandfather was <i>Adolfsen</i> from <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>. Nevertheless, Fennicization was not mandatory and thus it is common to find entirely Finnish-speaking families with Swedish surnames; having a Swedish name does not imply that one would speak Swedish. </p>
      <p>An effect of industrialization was that large numbers of people moved to the cities and towns and had to adopt a surname. Missing an inherited surname, they invented one from scratch. Initially, these were in Swedish, and they were not very stable; people called them "superfluous names" (<i>liikanimi</i>), and a person could change one's surname several times during their career. Later, Finnish became the preferred language, and themes were taken from nature. Some of the most common examples of this type are <i>Laine</i> "comber, wave", <i>Vainio</i> "cultivated field", <i>Nurmi</i> "grassland", and <i>Salo</i> "grove".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> When applicable, <i>-nen</i> or <i>-la</i>/<i>-lä</i> could be suffixed, such as in <i>Koskinen</i> "rapids + nen". </p>

      <p>In 21st-century Finland, the use of surnames follows the <a href="/wiki/German_name" title="German name">German model</a>. Every person is legally obliged to have a first and last name. At most, three first names are allowed. A Finnish married couple may adopt the surname that either spouse had before marriage, in which case this name will be the surname of their children. A spouse changing his or her name may decide to use a <a href="/wiki/Double-barrelled_name" title="Double-barrelled name">double-barrelled name</a> consisting of his or her former and current official surname. In the case where both spouses keep their names they may choose either name for their children, but all siblings must share the same surname.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>All persons have the right to change their surname once without any specific reason. A surname that is un-Finnish, contrary to the usages of the Swedish or Finnish languages, or in use by any person resident in Finland cannot be accepted as the new name, unless valid family reasons or religious or national customs give a reason for waiving this requirement. However, persons may change their surname to any surname that has ever been used by their ancestors, if they can prove such claim.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Surnames behave like regular words when forming grammatical cases. Thus, for example, the genitive of surname <i>Mäki</i> is <i>Mäen</i>, just like the regular word <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/m%C3%A4ki#Finnish" class="extiw" title="wikt:mäki">mäki</a></i> ("hill") becomes <i>mäen</i> in the genitive. For given names this is not always the case even if the word is a regular word; for example <i>Suvi</i> ("summer") becomes <i>Suvin</i> in the genitive, not <i>Suven</i>. </p>
      <p>In 1985, of the types of surnames, 38% of Finns had a <i>-nen</i> name, 8.9% <i>-la</i>, 7.4% with other derivative suffix (e.g., <i>-io/-iö</i>, as in <i>Meriö</i>, or <i>-sto/-stö</i>, as in <i>Niinistö</i>), 17.5% other names in Finnish, 14.8% non-Finnish (chiefly Swedish), 13.1% with compound word names (e.g., <i>Kivimäki</i> "stone hill", <i>Rautakoski</i> "iron rapids"). Only 0.3% had a double-barreled name (e.g., marriage of a <i>Forsius</i> to <i>Harkimo</i> giving <i>Forsius-Harkimo</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Patronymics">Patronymics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finnish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Patronymics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">Patronymics</a> were used in official documents until the late 19th century. Finns did not address each other by patronymics in colloquial speech. The natural Finnish way of referring to someone's parentage is the genitive: <i>Matin Olli</i> ("Matthew's Olaf") instead of the solemn <i>Olli Matinpoika</i> ("Olaf Matthew's son").<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> When patronymics were no longer required in documents, they quickly fell out of use.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> They are still perfectly legal, but very rare, often representing a deliberate archaism. Unlike in Swedish, Finnish patronymics were not transferred into hereditary family names. Thus, the Finnish situation differs considerably from, for instance, Sweden, which has hundreds of thousands of Johanssons and Anderssons. The Swedish patronymic-like surnames are treated as any other surname. Real patronymics are handled like additional first names, i.e., one must still have a surname.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> An exception is <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_nationality_law" title="Icelandic nationality law">Icelandic citizens</a> resident in Finland, who are allowed to follow the <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_name" title="Icelandic name">Icelandic name</a> tradition. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="First_names"><span id="given_name">First names</span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finnish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The native Finnish tradition of first names was lost during the early Christian period, and by the 16th century, only Christian first names were accepted. The popular names were usually the names of saints whose cult was widespread. This resulted in some differences between the Western and Eastern Finnish first names, as the names in Eastern Finland might have had forms derived from <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a> or Church-Slavic, instead of Swedish and Latin forms. For example, there are two Finnish cognates of George, <i>Yrjö</i> &lt; Swedish <i>Örjan</i> and <i>Jyri</i> &lt; Russian Юрий (<a href="/wiki/Yuri_(Slavic_name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Yuri (Slavic name)">Yuri</a>). The most important source for researching the name forms actually used by the Finns themselves in the 15th to 18th centuries are the surnames preserved in written sources, as these often are formed on the basis of a first name. The first names themselves are usually given in Swedish or Latin forms, as these are the languages used in the sources. The name actually used was a Fennicized form of the name, which might change as the person became older.<sup id="cite_ref-Sarilo,_M._Nimien_alkuperä_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sarilo,_M._Nimien_alkuperä-14">[14]</a></sup> For example, a person given the Swedish name <i>Gustaf</i> in the parish register might be called <i>Kustu</i> as a child, <i>Kusti</i> as an adolescent, <i>Kyösti</i> or <i>Köpi</i> as an adult and <i>Kustaa</i> as an old man.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the early 19th century, almost all Finnish first names were taken from the official <a href="/wiki/Almanac" title="Almanac">almanac</a>, published by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Turku" title="Royal Academy of Turku">Royal Academy of Turku</a>, later the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Helsinki" title="University of Helsinki">University of Helsinki</a>. The names were mostly names of the saints whose cult had been popular before the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a>, but the almanac also incorporated a number of names from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> and Swedish royalty, which were added to certain days during the 17th and 18th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> During the 19th century, the Finnish forms were gradually added to the Finnish almanac, while the Swedish and Latin forms were removed (the Swedish forms were retained in a separate Swedish almanac). At the same time, the vicars gradually started to use Finnish name forms in parish registers. This in turn, cemented the Finnish name forms used. </p>
      <p>Names with originally Finnish etymology were revived in the 19th century. In the absence of reliable information about ancient names, parents chose names of mythical characters from folklore (<i><a href="/wiki/Aino_(mythology)" title="Aino (mythology)">Aino</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Tapio_(name)" title="Tapio (name)">Tapio</a></i>), and many new names were created from Finnish words (<i>Seppo</i> "smith" or "skilled person", <i>Ritva</i> "birch twig"). Some clergymen initially refused to christen babies with such "pagan" names.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The first given name of Finnish origin, <i>Aino</i>, was accepted in the almanac in 1890, followed by numerous others in 1908. About 30% of Finns born in 1910–1939 received a name with Finnish etymology.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>By the 1930s, the use of Finnish names and name forms was stabilized and most of the popular names were noted in the almanac. Since then, the almanac has been gradually changed to include new, popular names.<sup id="cite_ref-Sarilo,_M._Nimien_alkuperä_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sarilo,_M._Nimien_alkuperä-14">[14]</a></sup> At present, all names which have at least 1,000 bearers are incorporated into the almanac of the University of Helsinki and given a "name day" (<a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a>: <i lang="fi">nimipäivä</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> At present, 792 of the 35,000 first names used in Finland are listed in the Finnish almanac.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The <i>nimipäivä</i> calendar follows the Medieval Catholic Saint calendar when applicable. </p>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_aspects">Legal aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finnish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legal aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>At present, the Names Act (<a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a>: <i lang="fi">Nimilaki</i>; <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>: <i lang="sv">Namnlagen</i>) of 1985 requires that all Finnish citizens and residents have at least one and at the most three first names. Persons who do not have a first name are obligated to adopt one when they are entered into the Finnish national population database. Parents of new-born children must name their child and inform the population registry within two months of the child's birth.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The name may be chosen freely, but it must not be </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>a name used primarily by persons of the other sex</li> 
       <li>a name foreign to the naming tradition in Finland</li> 
       <li>a surname, except a patronymic as last given name</li> 
       <li>a name already used by a sibling, if this is to be the only given name.</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>Waivers may be granted if valid family, religious or ethnic reasons give grounds to use a name contrary to these principles. Persons may change their first names once without a specific reason. For subsequent changes, valid reasons must be presented.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finnish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>As in general in European culture, the surname is seen as more formal and the first names as less formal. Strangers are expected to refer to each other by their surnames and using grammar in <a href="/wiki/T-V_distinction" class="mw-redirect" title="T-V distinction">formal plural</a>. The use of first names indicates familiarity, and children often refer to each other by first names only. However, in many workplaces familiarity between individuals working on the same site is assumed. </p>
      <p>In contrast to other European tradition, the use of titles such as <i>tohtori</i> "Doctor" with surnames is not very common and is found only in highly formal contexts, or it is considered old-fashioned. The titles equivalent to Mr., Mrs. and Miss are <i><a href="/wiki/Herra" title="Herra">herra</a></i>, <i>rouva</i> and <i>neiti</i>, respectively. Thus, for example, in formal contexts, <i>Matti Johannes Virtanen</i> can be referred to as <i>herra Virtanen</i> or <i>herra Matti Virtanen</i>, if several Virtanens are present. In most other contexts, simply one name, surname or first name, is used. As in Swedish culture, politeness is often expressed by indirect address, such that the use of names might even be deliberately avoided.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In spite of this, formal Finnish features various titles, particularly presidential citations such as <i><a href="/wiki/Vuorineuvos" title="Vuorineuvos">vuorineuvos</a></i> or <i>ministeri</i>. </p>
