      <p>A <b>Dharma name</b> is a new name acquired during a <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_initiation_ritual" title="Buddhist initiation ritual">Buddhist initiation ritual</a> in Mahayana Buddhism<sup id="cite_ref-khandro_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-khandro-1">[1]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Theravada#Ordination" title="Theravada">monk ordination</a> in Theravada Buddhism (where it is more proper to call it Dhamma or Sangha name). The name is traditionally given by a Buddhist monastic, and is given to newly ordained monks, nuns<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and laity.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Most of the well-known Buddhist teachers are known to have had many different Dharma names in the course of their careers, and often each name represents a stage of their career. For example, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Shotoku" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince Shotoku">Prince Shotoku</a> was also known as Prince Umayado and Prince Kamitsumiya. <a href="/wiki/Shinran" title="Shinran">Shinran</a>'s original name was Matsuwakamaru; he was also known as Hanen, Shakku, Zenshin, Gutoku Shinran and Kenshin Daeshi<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>. <a href="/wiki/Nichiren" title="Nichiren">Nichiren</a>'s original name was Zennichi and his dharma names were Zenshobo Rencho and Rissho Daishi. Similarly, the tradition of various Dharma names was also used by Zen monks, who also used art to promote Buddhism. The famous monk-painter <a href="/wiki/Hokusai" title="Hokusai">Hokusai</a> was also known as Shunro, Kako, Sori, Taito, Iitsu, Gakyojin and Manji.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Even the famous Samurai <a href="/wiki/Miyamoto_Musashi" title="Miyamoto Musashi">Miyamoto Musashi</a> had several names, including the Dharma name Niten Doraku and the birthname Miyamoto Masana. The zen monk <a href="/wiki/Thich_Nhat_Hanh" class="mw-redirect" title="Thich Nhat Hanh">Thich Nhat Hanh</a> also has used various Dharma names in the course of his career. </p>
      <p>If the student doesn't have a relationship with the monastic teacher and the ceremony is a public one with a congregation present, their new name will tend to reflect the lineage/tradition rather than the individual person.<sup id="cite_ref-khandro_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-khandro-1">[1]</a></sup> When it is given by a monastic who knows the disciple, however, the name is often tailor-made.<sup id="cite_ref-khandro_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-khandro-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Dharma names are generally given in the language of the particular <a href="/wiki/Sangha_(Buddhism)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sangha (Buddhism)">sangha</a> where the name is bestowed. People in the <a href="/wiki/Karma_Kagyu" title="Karma Kagyu">Karma Kagyu</a> tradition of Tibetan Buddhism are often given the name Karma.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In Burmese Buddhism, Dhamma names (<i>bwe</i>) are in <a href="/wiki/Pali" title="Pali">Pali</a> and chosen by the head monk of the monastery in which one is ordained. The traditional <a href="/wiki/Bamar#Naming" class="mw-redirect" title="Bamar">Burmese naming system</a>, in which the monastic's day of birth stipulates the first letter of one's name, is used to select the name. </p>
      <p>In China, ordained monks and nuns automatically revert to using the surname "Shi" (釋) as in <i>Shijiamouni</i> (釋迦牟尼), the Chinese transliteration of <a href="/wiki/Shakyamuni_Buddha" class="mw-redirect" title="Shakyamuni Buddha">Shakyamuni Buddha</a>. Vietnam also follows this tradition for its monks and nuns by changing their surname to "Thích" as in <i>Thích Ca Mâu Ni</i>, the Sino-Vietnamese name for Shakyamuni. Likewise for the Sino-Mahayana tradition of Buddhism, the dharma name given upon ordination can reflect the lineage passed from the teacher to the student, this can result in being given several dharma names: one for usage publicly, one used especially to reflect the transmitted lineage, and a second dharma name that can also be used. </p>
      <p>In Japan, other than the standard usage of dharma names for monastics and laity, it is also tradition for the deceased to receive a dharma name (戒名, kaimyō; literally "precept name") written in kanji from the priest. This name supposedly prevents the return of the deceased if his name is called. The length of the name depends also on either the virtue of the person's lifespan, or more commonly, the size of the donation of the relatives to the temple, which may range from a generally common name to the most elaborate names for 1 million yen or more. The high prices charged by the temples are a controversial issue in Japan, especially since some temples put pressure on families to buy a more expensive name. </p>
      <p>In the <a href="/wiki/Shaolin_Temple" class="mw-redirect" title="Shaolin Temple">Shaolin Temple</a>, each subsequent generation takes the first part of their given name from a 70-character poem written by <a href="/wiki/Xueting_Fuyu" title="Xueting Fuyu">Xueting Fuyu</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> For example, the 32nd character in the poem is "Xing" (行), and all Shaolin Temple monks and disciples of that generation have the name Shi Xing ___. </p>
