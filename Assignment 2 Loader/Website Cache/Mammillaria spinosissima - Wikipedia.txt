      <p><i><b>Mammillaria spinosissima</b></i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɛər/: 'are' in 'bare'">ɛər</span><span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span><span class="wrap"> </span><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>), also known as the <b>spiny pincushion cactus</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plant</a> in the cactus <a href="/wiki/Family_(botany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Family (botany)">family</a> Cactaceae, <a href="/wiki/Endemic" class="mw-redirect" title="Endemic">endemic</a> to the central Mexican states of <a href="/wiki/Guerrero" title="Guerrero">Guerrero</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morelos" title="Morelos">Morelos</a>, where they grow at elevations of approximately 1,600 to 1,900 metres (5,200 to 6,200&nbsp;ft). The species was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> in 1838 by <a href="/wiki/James_Forbes_(botanist)" title="James Forbes (botanist)">James Forbes</a>, gardener of the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Bedford" title="Duke of Bedford">Duke of Bedford</a>. Botanist <a href="/wiki/David_Hunt_(botanist)" title="David Hunt (botanist)">David Hunt</a> collected a specimen in 1971, when he located one near <a href="/wiki/Sierra_de_Tepoztlan" title="Sierra de Tepoztlan">Sierra de Tepoztlan</a>, Mexico. </p>
      <p>The cylindrical and elongated plants grow up to 30 centimetres (12&nbsp;in) tall and 10 centimetres (3.9&nbsp;in) wide. They reach full height after five to ten years. The spines are red-brown or white, with cream-colored radials and pink, funnel-shaped flowers that grow in a ring around the apex of the stem to approximately 2 centimetres (0.79&nbsp;in) long. It grows low to the ground in solitary or in clusters, and its flowers produce generally bright red berries that are club-shaped, smooth, and juicy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p><i>M. spinosissima</i> thrive in well-drained soils that are sandy or <a href="/wiki/Loam" title="Loam">loam</a>, with a <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> ranging from acidic to neutral. They prefer low humidity and full, filtered sun. Plants are typically watered once every two or three weeks, and kept nearly dry during the winter months. They require no pruning and make good patio and container plants. They are relatively disease resistant, but susceptible to pests such as <a href="/wiki/Mealybug" title="Mealybug">mealybugs</a>. Synonyms of <i>M. spinosissima</i> include <i>Mammillaria centraliplumosa</i>, <i>Mammillaria haasii</i>, and <i>Mammillaria virginis</i>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mammillaria_spinosissima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Mexico.Mor.Tepoztlan.01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A color picture of a mountain from town" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Mexico.Mor.Tepoztlan.01.jpg/220px-Mexico.Mor.Tepoztlan.01.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Mexico.Mor.Tepoztlan.01.jpg/330px-Mexico.Mor.Tepoztlan.01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Mexico.Mor.Tepoztlan.01.jpg/440px-Mexico.Mor.Tepoztlan.01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="480" data-file-height="640"></a>

      <p><a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> designated <i><a href="/wiki/Mammillaria" title="Mammillaria">Mammillaria</a></i> as a type <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> for <a href="/wiki/Cactus" title="Cactus">cactus</a> in 1753.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEButterworthWallace20041086_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEButterworthWallace20041086-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1838, <a href="/wiki/James_Forbes_(botanist)" title="James Forbes (botanist)">James Forbes</a>, gardener of the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Bedford" title="Duke of Bedford">Duke of Bedford</a>, listed and <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> a species he called <i>Echinocactus spinosissimas</i> from a group of cacti he had acquired in Europe three years earlier. <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Lord_Britton" title="Nathaniel Lord Britton">Nathaniel Lord Britton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Nelson_Rose" title="Joseph Nelson Rose">Joseph Nelson Rose</a> believe that Forbes was given that name by <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Karl_Georg_Pfeiffer" title="Ludwig Karl Georg Pfeiffer">Ludwig Karl Georg Pfeiffer</a>, but the plant was actually <i>Mammillaria spinosissima</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrittonRose1922179_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrittonRose1922179-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Pfeiffer had published the first infrageneric division of <i>Mammillaria</i> in 1837, dividing the genus into two groups based on distinct spine characteristics. In 1845, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_zu_Salm-Reifferscheidt-Dyck" title="Joseph zu Salm-Reifferscheidt-Dyck">Joseph zu Salm-Reifferscheidt-Dyck</a>&nbsp;– based on work by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Frederick_Scheer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Frederick Scheer (page does not exist)">Frederick Scheer</a>&nbsp;– expanded the classification into eight groups. With at least 145 recognized species, it is one of the largest and most morphologically variable genera in the cactus family.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEButterworthWallace20041086_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEButterworthWallace20041086-2">[2]</a></sup> Others estimate there are as many as two hundred species of <i>Mammillaria</i>, including sixty-two cultivated species from India. Though larger, the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Opuntia" title="Opuntia">Opuntia</a></i> is less popular with gardeners and landscapers.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMattagajasinghMukherjeeDas2006583_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMattagajasinghMukherjeeDas2006583-4">[4]</a></sup> <i>Mammillaria</i> was previously thought to be <a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">monophyletic</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">phylogenic</a> analysis indicates that <i>Mammilloydia</i> is "embedded within a 'core' group of <i>Mammillaria</i> species."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEButterworthWallace20041086_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEButterworthWallace20041086-2">[2]</a></sup> A specimen of <i>Mammillaria spinosissima</i> was collected by Botanist <a href="/wiki/David_Hunt_(botanist)" title="David Hunt (botanist)">David Hunt</a> in September 1971, when he located one in Mexico, near the Morelos Cautla-Cuernavaca toll road in <a href="/wiki/Sierra_de_Tepoztlan" title="Sierra de Tepoztlan">Sierra de Tepoztlan</a>, at an altitude of 1,600 meters (5,200&nbsp;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKew_Gardens:_Herbarium_catalogue_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKew_Gardens:_Herbarium_catalogue-5">[5]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span id="Similar_species.2C_subspecies_and_synonyms"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_species,_subspecies_and_synonyms">Similar species, subspecies and synonyms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mammillaria_spinosissima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Similar species, subspecies and synonyms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Species similar to <i>Mammillaria spinosissima</i> include <i><a href="/wiki/Mammillaria_backebergiana" title="Mammillaria backebergiana">Mammillaria backebergiana</a></i> and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mammillaria_meyranii&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mammillaria meyranii (page does not exist)">Mammillaria meyranii</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGraham2014105_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGraham2014105-6">[6]</a></sup> Subspecies include <i>M. spinosissima pilcayensis</i>, synonym: <i>Bravo</i> (D. R. Hunt), <i>M. spinosissima tepoxtlana</i> (D. R. Hunt), and <i>M. spinosissima spinosissima</i> (Lem.).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIntegrated_Taxonomic_Information_System_(a)_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIntegrated_Taxonomic_Information_System_(a)-1">[1]</a></sup> Synonyms of <i>M. spinosissima</i> include <i>Mammillaria centraliplumosa</i> (Fittkau), <i>Mammillaria haasii</i> (J. Meyrán), and <i>Mammillaria virginis</i> (Fittkau and Kladiwa).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIntegrated_Taxonomic_Information_System_(b)_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIntegrated_Taxonomic_Information_System_(b)-7">[7]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mammillaria_spinosissima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Mammillaria_spinosissima3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A color picture of a reddish-spined cactus" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Mammillaria_spinosissima3.jpg/220px-Mammillaria_spinosissima3.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="287" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Mammillaria_spinosissima3.jpg/330px-Mammillaria_spinosissima3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Mammillaria_spinosissima3.jpg/440px-Mammillaria_spinosissima3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="891" data-file-height="1164"></a>

      <p><i>Mammillaria spinosissima</i>, also known as spiny pincushion cactus, are cylindrical plants that grow up to 30 centimetres (12&nbsp;in) tall and 10 centimetres (3.9&nbsp;in) wide. They reach full height after five to ten years. The spines are red-brown or white, with cream-colored radials and pink, funnel-shaped flowers that grow in a ring around the apex of the stem to approximately 2 centimetres (0.79&nbsp;in) long.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoyal_Horticultural_Society_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoyal_Horticultural_Society-8">[8]</a></sup> The relatively small cacti are globular or elongated, and the flowers produce generally bright red berries that are club-shaped, smooth, and juicy.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMattagajasinghMukherjeeDas2006583_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMattagajasinghMukherjeeDas2006583-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>
      <p><i>Mammillaria</i> species tend to grow low to the ground in solitary or grouped in clusters. The genus is marked by its dimorphic <a href="/wiki/Areole" title="Areole">areoles</a>: the spine bearing, or vegetative, areoles are located on the apex of the tubercle, and flowering areoles are located inside the axils of the tubercles.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEButterworthWallace20041086_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEButterworthWallace20041086-2">[2]</a></sup> The stem is woolly and covered with bristles.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPienaar2000221_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPienaar2000221-9">[9]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Native_habitat">Native habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mammillaria_spinosissima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Native habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The genus <i>Mammillaria</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> native habitat ranges from Colombia and Venezuela to the Southwestern United States. Its diversity is greatest in Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEButterworthWallace20041086_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEButterworthWallace20041086-2">[2]</a></sup> Species have also been documented in the West Indies.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMattagajasinghMukherjeeDas2006583_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMattagajasinghMukherjeeDas2006583-4">[4]</a></sup> <i>M. spinosissima</i> is <a href="/wiki/Endemic" class="mw-redirect" title="Endemic">endemic</a> to central Mexico and concentrated in the states of <a href="/wiki/Guerrero" title="Guerrero">Guerrero</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morelos" title="Morelos">Morelos</a>, where they grow at elevations of approximately 1,600 to 1,900 meters (5,200 to 6,200&nbsp;ft); they prefer dry, tropical forests and xerophilous scrub.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEInternational_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature_and_Natural_Resources_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEInternational_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature_and_Natural_Resources-10">[10]</a></sup> The subspecies <i>pilcayensis</i> is named for its occurrence in the Barranca de Pilcaya, in Guerrero.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEggliNewton2004185_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEggliNewton2004185-11">[11]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mammillaria_spinosissima&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cultivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Britton and Rose believe that <i>M. spinosissima</i> has been in cultivation since at least 1835.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrittonRose1922179_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrittonRose1922179-3">[3]</a></sup> The species thrives in well-drained soils that are sandy or <a href="/wiki/Loam" title="Loam">loam</a>, with a <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> that is acidic, alkaline, or neutral. They prefer low humidity, and grow well under glass, with full, filtered sun from the south, north, and east. Plants are typically watered once every couple of weeks, and kept nearly dry during the winter months. Propagation is facilitated by offsets of seeds sown in early spring in 19 to 24&nbsp;°C (34 to 43&nbsp;°F) weather. They require no pruning and make good patio and container plants. They are disease resistant, but are susceptible to pests such as <a href="/wiki/Mealybugs" class="mw-redirect" title="Mealybugs">mealybugs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoyal_Horticultural_Society_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoyal_Horticultural_Society-8">[8]</a></sup> This plant has gained the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Horticultural_Society" title="Royal Horticultural Society">Royal Horticultural Society</a>’s <a href="/wiki/Award_of_Garden_Merit" title="Award of Garden Merit">Award of Garden Merit</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RHSPF_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RHSPF-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> </p>
