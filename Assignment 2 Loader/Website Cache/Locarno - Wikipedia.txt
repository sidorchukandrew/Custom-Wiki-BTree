      <p><b>Locarno</b> (<small>Italian:&nbsp;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian" title="Help:IPA/Italian">[loˈkarno]</a></span>, <small>German: </small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[loˈkarno]</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Ticinese_dialect" title="Ticinese dialect">Ticinese</a>: <i lang="lmo" title="Lombard language text">Locarn</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Lombard" title="Help:IPA/Lombard">[luˈkɑːrn]</a></span>; formerly in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Luggárus</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[luˈɡaːrʊs]</a></span>) is a southern <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Swiss</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_towns_in_Switzerland" class="mw-redirect" title="List of towns in Switzerland">town</a> and <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_of_Switzerland" title="Municipalities of Switzerland">municipality</a> in the district <a href="/wiki/Locarno_(district)" class="mw-redirect" title="Locarno (district)">Locarno</a> (and its capital), located on the northern shore of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Maggiore" title="Lake Maggiore">Lake Maggiore</a> at its northeastern tip in the <a href="/wiki/Cantons_of_Switzerland" title="Cantons of Switzerland">canton</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ticino" class="mw-redirect" title="Ticino">Ticino</a> at the southern foot of the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Alps" title="Swiss Alps">Swiss Alps</a>. It has a population of about 16,000 (proper), and about 56,000 for the agglomeration of the same name including <a href="/wiki/Ascona" title="Ascona">Ascona</a> besides other municipalities.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The town of Locarno is located on the northeastern part of <a href="/wiki/Maggia_(river)" title="Maggia (river)">Maggia</a>'s delta; across the river lies the town Ascona on the southwestern part of the delta. </p>
      <p>It is the 74th largest city in Switzerland by population<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and the third largest in the Ticino canton, after <a href="/wiki/Lugano" title="Lugano">Lugano</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bellinzona" title="Bellinzona">Bellinzona</a>. </p>
      <p>The official language of Locarno is <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>. </p>
      <p>The town is known for hosting the <a href="/wiki/Locarno_International_Film_Festival" class="mw-redirect" title="Locarno International Film Festival">Locarno International Film Festival</a> which takes place every year in August and involves open-air screenings at the main square, the Piazza Grande. It is also known for the <a href="/wiki/Locarno_Treaties" title="Locarno Treaties">Locarno Treaties</a>, a series of European territorial agreements negotiated here in October 1925. </p>
      <p>In January 2004, the <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historian</a> Marino Vigano speculated that <a href="/wiki/Castello_Visconteo_(Locarno)" class="mw-redirect" title="Castello Visconteo (Locarno)">Locarno's castle</a> may have been designed by <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistoric_Locarno">Prehistoric Locarno</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prehistoric Locarno">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In 1934 in the vicinity of today's Via S. Jorio, a <a href="/wiki/Necropolis" title="Necropolis">necropolis</a> with 14 <a href="/wiki/Urn#Cremation_urns" title="Urn">urn graves</a> from the Early <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> (about 14th century BC) were found. Some of the urns were directly buried, while others were placed in boxes of uncut stone. The urns contained, in addition to burned bones, <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> ornaments, which had some fire damage, including, bangles, hair pins with conical head and slightly thickened neck, rings and knives. Similar urns were also discovered in the district of S. Antonio, which was probably also a small cemetery. The ceramic and bronze objects date from the <a href="/wiki/Canegrate_culture" title="Canegrate culture">Canegrate culture</a> (named after a large necropolis in the province of Milan). However, no traces of the settlement have been discovered.<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_Pre_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Pre-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1935, a large necropolis was discovered at <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solduno&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Solduno (page does not exist)">Solduno</a>. The over 200 graves cover nearly a thousand years, from the <a href="/wiki/La_T%C3%A8ne_culture" title="La Tène culture">La Tène culture</a> to the 3rd century AD. Many of the La Tène era grave goods (particularly from the 3rd–1st centuries BC) are <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> style <a href="/wiki/Fibula_(brooch)" title="Fibula (brooch)">Fibulae</a> or brooches. These objects demonstrate a cultural influence from regions north of the Alps. However, the ceramic objects are indigenous to <a href="/wiki/Golasecca_culture" title="Golasecca culture">Golasecca culture</a> which spread into Ticino and <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">Lombardy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_Pre_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Pre-4">[4]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_era">Roman era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Roman era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Between 1946 and 1949, a number of <a href="/wiki/Switzerland_in_the_Roman_era" title="Switzerland in the Roman era">Roman era</a> tombs were discovered on the terrace between the churches of Santa Maria in Selva and San Giovanni Battista in Solduno. The Roman city that became Locarno was therefore between the <a href="/wiki/Vicus_(Rome)" class="mw-redirect" title="Vicus (Rome)">vicus</a> of Muralto and this cemetery. Unfortunately, intensive construction and agricultural activity have destroyed most traces of the ancient city. In 1995 and 1997, 57 graves were found in Via Valle Maggia. Nineteen were from the Roman period, which confirms that even in the <a href="/wiki/3rd_century" title="3rd century">3rd century</a> AD cremation and body burials were practiced side by side. Among other significant finds, a number of glass items were discovered. The Roman necropolis was used from the end of the prehistoric <a href="/wiki/La_T%C3%A8ne_culture" title="La Tène culture">La Tène era</a> until the middle of the 3rd century AD. The extensive <a href="/wiki/Romanization_(cultural)" title="Romanization (cultural)">Romanization</a> of Locarno wiped out much of the local culture and replaced it with ancient Roman elements. However, it appears that there was no Roman ruling class which could have dominated the local population.<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_early_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_early-5">[5]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Capitanei_di_Locarno">Capitanei di Locarno</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Capitanei di Locarno">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The <b>capitanei</b> were a group of prominent noble families who emerged in the early Middle Ages and led Locarno. The term is first mentioned in a document granting <a href="/wiki/Market_right" class="mw-redirect" title="Market right">market rights</a> to the town by the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Frederick_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick I, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick I</a> in 1164. This title was originally reserved only the direct <a href="/wiki/Vassals" class="mw-redirect" title="Vassals">vassals</a> of the king's <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">fief</a>. The lower vassals were known as <i>valvassores</i>, but could have been awarded the title of <i>capitanei</i> as a special concession. The original <i>capitanei</i> were probably descendants of the old <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">Lombard</a> noble family of Da Besozzo from the <a href="/wiki/County" title="County">county</a> of Seprio, a historic region of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Lombards" title="Kingdom of the Lombards">Lombard Italy</a> which comprised areas in southern Ticino and modern day Italian provinces of <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Varese" title="Province of Varese">Varese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Como" title="Province of Como">Como</a> on the western side of Lake Maggiore, and was centered in <a href="/wiki/Castelseprio_(archaeological_park)" title="Castelseprio (archaeological park)">Castelseprio</a>, some 20 kilometres south of Locarno. </p>
      <p>Around 1000, the family was granted a fief in Locarno by the schismatic <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Como" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Como">Bishop of Como</a> Landolfo da Carcano. The <i>capitanei</i> were given the right to manage the property of the Church entrusted to the local <a href="/wiki/Pieve" title="Pieve">pieve</a>, they had the rights of immunity and coercion, but were not owners of the village cooperatives' (<i>vicini</i>) land, with the exception of the churches and royal estates. They did not have the right of <a href="/wiki/High,_middle_and_low_justice" title="High, middle and low justice">high justice</a> so their political power was limited. However, they played an important role in the later conflicts in the 13th and 14th century between the <a href="/wiki/Guelphs_and_Ghibellines" title="Guelphs and Ghibellines">Guelphs and Ghibellines</a> and in the wars between Como and the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Milan" title="Duchy of Milan">Duchy of Milan</a>. </p>
      <p>In Locarno, during the <a href="/wiki/Reformation" title="Reformation">Reformation</a> period in the 16th century, two of the three great feudal families of capitanei, the Muralto and the Orelli families, left the town and moved to <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" title="Zürich">Zürich</a>. A branch of the Muraltos was established in <a href="/wiki/Bern" title="Bern">Bern</a>. The third great Locarno family, the Magoria, remained in Locarno. The <i>capitanei</i> retained a central role in Locarno's politics until 1798. In 1803, the lands and rights of the <i>capitanei</i> were integrated into the political municipality of Locarno.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Locarno">Early Locarno</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early Locarno">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Tessin2006_picture_049.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Tessin2006_picture_049.jpg/220px-Tessin2006_picture_049.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Tessin2006_picture_049.jpg/330px-Tessin2006_picture_049.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Tessin2006_picture_049.jpg/440px-Tessin2006_picture_049.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200"></a>

          <a href="/wiki/File:Tessin2006_picture_049.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>

        </div>

      <p>Starting in the Lombard period (after 569), the area around Locarno (and presumably the town) was part of the county Stazzona and later the <a href="/wiki/March_(territory)" title="March (territory)">Mark</a> of Lombardy. Locarno is first mentioned in 807 as <i>Leocarni</i>. In German, it came to be known as <i>Luggarus, Lucarius, Lucaris</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS-7">[7]</a></sup> It is likely that a market existed at or near the lake since the Roman era. The long history of the town and its location led to the creation of a royal court, which is first mentioned in 866. During the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> Locarno and Ascona formed a community, with several, separate neighborhoods. The community managed its <a href="/wiki/The_commons" class="mw-redirect" title="The commons">common goods</a> (alpine pastures, pastures, forests, churches) and tax officials and police. </p>
      <p>In the 10th century, <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Milan" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Milan">Bishop of Milan</a> began to consolidate more and more power to himself at the expense of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Germany" title="Kingdom of Germany">Kings of the Germans</a>. This expansion by Milan was countered by <a href="/wiki/Henry_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry II, Holy Roman Emperor">Henry II</a>, who incorporated Locarno in 1002/04 with the surrounding areas into the Diocese of Como. <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Barbarossa" class="mw-redirect" title="Friedrich Barbarossa">Friedrich Barbarossa</a> granted extensive <a href="/wiki/Market_town" title="Market town">market rights</a> to Locarno in 1164 and granted <a href="/wiki/Imperial_immediacy" title="Imperial immediacy">imperial immediacy</a> in 1186. Due to these privileges, Locarno developed substantial local autonomy, which assisted the development of municipal institutions. The nobles (<i>Nobili</i>) lost more and more rights to the citizens (<i>borghesi</i>). By 1224, the <i>borghesi</i> had their own administration and various privileges, including: market rights, the right to their own weights, maintenance of mills and grazing rights in Saleggi, in Colmanicchio (<i>Alp Vignasca</i>) and in the Magadino and Quartino valleys.<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_early_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_early-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Locarno was the administrative center of the <a href="/wiki/Parish" title="Parish">parish</a> of Locarno. The <i><a href="/wiki/Podest%C3%A0" title="Podestà">Podestà</a></i> or high government official, resided in the Casa della Gallinazza, which was burned in 1260 during the clashes between the Guelphs and Ghibellines. Several Locarno families, including members of the <i>Capitanei di Locarno</i> and Simone da Orello, played an important role in the battles between the two factions. In 1342, Luchino and Giovanni <a href="/wiki/House_of_Visconti" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Visconti">Visconti</a> conquered the area, which brought Locarno back under the power of <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>. In 1439, Count Franchino Rusca was awarded Locarno as a fief.<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_early_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_early-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1291, a <a href="/wiki/Humiliati" title="Humiliati">Humiliati</a> monastery was first mentioned in Locarno. St. Catherine's church, attached to the monastery, probably dates to the mid-14th century.<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_Religion_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Religion-8">[8]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Under_the_Swiss_Confederation">Under the Swiss Confederation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Under the Swiss Confederation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

      <p>The rule of the Rusca ended 1503, when the <a href="/wiki/Old_Swiss_Confederacy" title="Old Swiss Confederacy">Confederates</a> occupied Locarno, but failed to conquer the Visconti castle. After the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Novara_(1513)" title="Battle of Novara (1513)">battle of Novara</a> in 1513, the French King <a href="/wiki/Louis_XII_of_France" title="Louis XII of France">Louis XII</a> gave the Confederates the castle. In the Treaty of Freiburg in 1516, they received all of Locarno. The Twelve Cantons took turns appointing a governor (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <i lang="it">commissario</i>), to rule over Locarno. The governor had both civil and criminal jurisdiction, except in certain cases after 1578, where seven judges were elected by the locals to try the cases. On taking office, the Governor swore under oath to obey the statutes of Locarno. The Governor was supported by a local mayor, and criminal fines were usually given to local community.<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_early_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_early-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The rule of the Twelve Cantons also had an effect on the social and political relations of the town. In addition to the nobility (representing the three old noble families Orelli, Muralto and Magoria) and citizens, there was a third group. This third group, the <i>terrieri</i>, was a group of residents who had lived a long time in Locarno, but were not citizens. Each of the three groups were a <a href="/wiki/Statutory_corporation" title="Statutory corporation">statutory corporation</a>, with shared property and a governing body. Under the Confederation, each of these groups or corporations had representation in the <i>Consiglio Magnifico</i> which ruled Locarno. However, the town's dominance over the villages in the region, until 1798, is clearly shown in their representation in the Council. The three groups in Locarno had twelve aldermen (six nobles, four citizens and two <i>terrieri</i>), while all the rural communities together had only eight members.<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_early_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_early-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>During the Middle Ages, Locarno was centered around the intersection of Via Cittadella and Via S. Antonio. The town stretched up the slope of the mountain and behind the castle. Some houses lined the shore, and above the Contrada Borghese a small, almost rural settlement grew up. The center of town was dominated by town houses with their large courtyards and gardens. The members of the upper class also owned small country houses with <a href="/wiki/Vineyards" class="mw-redirect" title="Vineyards">vineyards</a>, which went up the hill behind Locarno as well as in Solduno and <a href="/wiki/Cugnasco" title="Cugnasco">Cugnasco</a>. Locarno also had several noble <a href="/wiki/Estate_(land)" title="Estate (land)">estates</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">Late Middle Ages</a>, including the so-called Cittadella. The buildings of the 17th and 18th Centuries, including the Casa Simona (16th to 18th century), the Casa Rusca from the first half of the 18th century (now the seat of the municipal art gallery) and the Casa del Negromante, were built on older structures and didn't change the layout of the town.<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_early_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_early-5">[5]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span id="Locarno.27s_castles"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Locarno's_castles">Locarno's castles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Locarno's castles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

      <p>There were several castles from the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Middle Ages</a> in Locarno. One was destroyed by the Milanese in 1156. Another, first mentioned in 1210 as Orelli castle, was occupied in 1342 occupied by the Visconti and enlarged. This castle came to be known as Visconti Castle. In 1531, the Confederates demolished much of the castle, leaving only the central core. The castle was used as the residence of the governor, but in the following centuries, it began to fall apart. From 1804 to 1909, the castle was the seat of the administrative governor and the District Court. After 1909, it came into the possession of the city and was renovated in 1921–28. Since 1920, it houses the city and Archaeological Museum. In 1507, a defensive bastion or <a href="/wiki/Ravelin" title="Ravelin">ravelin</a> was added to protect the castle entrance. It is likely that Leonardo da Vinci designed this structure.<sup id="cite_ref-Swissinfo_11_Aug_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Swissinfo_11_Aug-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HDS_early_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_early-5">[5]</a></sup> North-east of the castle is Palazzo Casorella (Casa degli Orelli) from the 16th century. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_Locarno">Early modern Locarno</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Early modern Locarno">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:View_from_Monte_Ceneri_road_toward_Locarno.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/View_from_Monte_Ceneri_road_toward_Locarno.jpg/220px-View_from_Monte_Ceneri_road_toward_Locarno.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/View_from_Monte_Ceneri_road_toward_Locarno.jpg/330px-View_from_Monte_Ceneri_road_toward_Locarno.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/View_from_Monte_Ceneri_road_toward_Locarno.jpg/440px-View_from_Monte_Ceneri_road_toward_Locarno.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4032" data-file-height="3024"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:View_from_Monte_Ceneri_road_toward_Locarno.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>

      </div> 
      <p>During the Middle Ages, the economy of Locarno and the whole region was closely linked with the nobles who owned the market, fishing, alpine and grazing rights and <a href="/wiki/Tithe" title="Tithe">tithes</a>. Starting in the 13th century, some of these privileges went to the citizens corporation. The hill areas were dominated by <a href="/wiki/Viticulture" title="Viticulture">viticulture</a>, while grain was raised in the plains. Within the town, vegetables and fruits were grown. Artisans and stores were concentrated in the interior of the town. The repeatedly flooded shorelines and the alpine pastures were used for grazing. In the early modern period, Locarno developed into a thriving commercial center on an important road that linked the major cities of <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">Lombardy</a>, with German-speaking Switzerland and Germany. Cereals and salt came from the Lombardy and <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">Piedmont</a> to Locarno, while Locarno and its hinterland (especially the <a href="/wiki/Valle_Maggia" title="Valle Maggia">Maggia Valley</a>) provided large quantities of wood and cattle to Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_early_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_early-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parish church</a> of Locarno, S. Antonio Abate, was first built in 1353–54. It was replaced by the current building in 1664. The decoration of the church was financed, primarily, by the grain traders at the end of the 17th century, and the chapel <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">frescoes</a> are by Giuseppe Antonio Felice Orelli from 1742. The citizens of Locarno had the right to appoint priests at S. Antonio Abate, S. Maria in Selva (consecrated in 1424, since 1884 only the <a href="/wiki/Choir" title="Choir">choir</a> and bell tower remain) and SS Trinità dei Monti (consecrated 1621). The church of San Francesco, together with the adjoining convent, were built in the early 13th century. The church's first consecration was probably in 1230, but the first documented consecration of the church was in 1316. It was rebuilt and enlarged between 1538–1675 using construction material from Visconti Castle. San Francesco hosted the meetings of the nobility and the citizens. Starting in the 16th century, the appointed Governor swore his oath in the church.<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_Religion_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Religion-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the 16th century, the <a href="/wiki/Humiliati" title="Humiliati">Humiliati</a> order was suppressed and St. Catherine's church and monastery closed. All three ruling groups of Locarno agreed to convert the church and monastery into the Hospital S. Carlo. The Hospital remained until 1854 when it closed, due to financial reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_Religion_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Religion-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the 16th century, Locarno's population declined as a result of exodus of <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestants</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Plague_(disease)" title="Plague (disease)">plague</a> in 1576–77. After a recovery in the 17th century, the population declined considerably in the 18th century again. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protestant_Reformation">Protestant Reformation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Protestant Reformation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>By 1535, there was a <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant</a> community known as the <i>ecclesia christiana locarnensis</i> in Locarno. They owed their existence to the work of Giovanni Beccaria, several notables of the town (including Taddeo Duni) and religious refugees from <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a> and Piedmont. To counteract the tensions, the Protestant community and the Catholic clergy met for a debate on 8 May 1549. At the end of the debate, Beccaria was imprisoned. However, because of protests against his arrest, he was immediately released. In 1550, Locarno declared itself to be a Catholic town, but a large group continued to practice the Protestant faith. However, in 1554, the Diet of Baden issued an ultimatum to the Protestants, either renounce new faith or go into exile. On 3 March 1555, over 100 people left Locarno to emigrate to <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" title="Zürich">Zürich</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_Religion_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Religion-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the course of <a href="/wiki/Counter-Reformation" title="Counter-Reformation">Counter-Reformation</a> in the 17th century, many religious buildings in Locarno were renewed or rebuilt. These include the 1604 church dedicated to SS Sebastiano e Rocco with the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Friars_Minor_Capuchin" title="Order of Friars Minor Capuchin">Capuchin</a> monastery and the church of S. Maria Assunta (Chiesa Nuova).<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_Religion_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Religion-8">[8]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Canton_of_Ticino">The Canton of Ticino</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The Canton of Ticino">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Helvetic_Republic" title="Helvetic Republic">Helvetic Republic</a> (1798–1803) Locarno was part of the <a href="/wiki/Canton_of_Lugano" title="Canton of Lugano">Canton of Lugano</a>. Following the collapse of the Helvetic Republic, the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Mediation" title="Act of Mediation">Act of Mediation</a>, in 1803, created the Canton of Ticino with Locarno as an independent municipality. The Helvetic Republic was very liberal and attempted to reform much of Swiss society. However, the changes were too great and the Republic collapsed. The compromise Act of Mediation changed some aspects of society but left others unchanged. The new municipality of Lucarno was no longer ruled by three different <i><a href="/wiki/B%C3%BCrgergemeinde" title="Bürgergemeinde">patriziati</a></i>, which had emerged from the three groups (nobles, <i>borghesi</i> and <i>terrieri</i>), but until the mid-19th century there were institutions that reached back to the <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_Switzerland" title="Early Modern Switzerland">Ancien Régime</a>. For example, the community of Locarno and Ascona was dissolved in 1805. Nevertheless, a "committee of the representatives of the former communities of Locarno" managed, for several decades, the S. Carlo Hospital and schools, which had been shared by the <i>patriziati</i> of the old, combined community. However, the power of the old <i>patriziati</i> gradually weakened. In 1859, the <i>terrieri</i> decided to set aside their corporation. The nobles corporation distributed its assets in 1866–67 to its members and dissolved the archive, but retained until about 1920, the fishing rights. Only the citizens corporation has preserved its status as a civil community.<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_Modern_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Modern-10">[10]</a></sup> </p> 
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Locarno_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Locarno_1.jpg/220px-Locarno_1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Locarno_1.jpg/330px-Locarno_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Locarno_1.jpg/440px-Locarno_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2560" data-file-height="1920"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Locarno_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>Piazza Grande
        </div>

      </div> 
      <p>The Constitution of 1814, established Locarno, Bellinzona and <a href="/wiki/Lugano" title="Lugano">Lugano</a> as the capitals of the canton, in a six-year rotation. Locarno was the capital of the canton in 1821–27, 1839–45, 1857–63 and 1875–81. In 1838–39, on the initiative of a group of notables, a government building was built. It was sold in 1893 to private company. It then became the headquarters of Credito Ticinese and since 1917 it has been the headquarters of the <a href="/wiki/Electricity_Company_of_Sopraceneri" class="mw-redirect" title="Electricity Company of Sopraceneri">Electricity Company of Sopraceneri</a>. Locarno was repeatedly the scene of political clashes. In 1839 and 1841, uprisings against the government broke out. In 1855, a murder in a coffee house was used as a pretext for a coup of radicals (<i>pronunciamento</i>). Another coup, the Ticino coup of 1890, did nothing to change the balance of power between the parties in the city. After a liberal <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_Locarno" class="mw-redirect" title="Mayor of Locarno">mayor</a> ruled for 35 consecutive years (1865–80), a conservative mayor ruled for another 36 years (1880–1916).<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_Modern_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Modern-10">[10]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>To break the conservative hegemony of the late 19th century, the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Switzerland" title="Social Democratic Party of Switzerland">Social Democrats</a>, allied with the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Switzerland" title="Liberal Party of Switzerland">Liberals</a> in 1916 elections. They succeeded to a majority in the city government (which then consisted of nine members, but in 1987 was limited to seven), which they kept in the following decades.<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_Modern_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Modern-10">[10]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the 19th century, the population in Locarno grew noticeably compared to neighboring communities. As early as 1836, the immigrant population was over 16% of the population. Between 1860–80, the population fell slightly mainly because of emigration to <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. In the following decades, the growth rates were below those of other population centers of the canton, which, unlike Locarno, benefited directly from the <a href="/wiki/Gotthard_railway" title="Gotthard railway">Gotthard railway</a>. </p>
      <p>In 1816, the special rights and title of the old mother church of San Vittore in <a href="/wiki/Muralto" title="Muralto">Muralto</a> went over to S. Antonio Abate. In 1863, snow collecting on the roof of the church caused the <a href="/wiki/Vault_(architecture)" title="Vault (architecture)">vault</a> to collapse, killing 40 people. The citizens collective or <i>borghesi</i> lacked the resources to repair the church. Therefore, in 1866, the ownership and maintenance of the churches of S. Antonio and S. Maria in Selva were given to the town as a whole. For financial reasons, the collapsed church was not completely repaired. Only the façade and central vault were rebuilt and the choir was expanded.<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_Religion_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_Religion-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Between 1863 and '74, church of San Francesco had to be used for services, and after 1798, the Assemblies of the neighborhoods, the city and the county were held in the church. In 1814, the church of San Francesco was <a href="/wiki/Secularized" class="mw-redirect" title="Secularized">secularized</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Franciscans" title="Franciscans">Franciscans</a> had to leave. The church and convent served from 1821 until 1827 as the State Government offices. From 1848–63, the church was closed for worship, and in 1874 it was converted into a barracks and a salt storage. In 1924, it was converted back into a church and used by Benedictines for <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Catholic</a> services delivered in the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German language</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Jesuits" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesuits">Jesuits</a> took over this task from 1947 until 1992. The monastery was secularized in 1848, and after the 1893–94 renovation, it housed the first high school. Then in 1930 it was used for teacher training. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Locarno">Modern Locarno</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Modern Locarno">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Between 1935 and '92, the newspaper, <i>L'Eco di Locarno</i> was printed in Locarno. In 1992, it merged with the official newspaper of the Liberal party <i>Il dovere</i> to create the daily newspaper <i>La Regione</i>. Since 1987, the only German language newspaper in Ticino, the <i>Tessiner Zeitung</i>, is published three times each week in Locarno. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

         </div>Locarno
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>Locarno has an area, as of 1997<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, of 19.27 square kilometers (7.44&nbsp;sq&nbsp;mi). Of this area, 8.39&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup> (3.24&nbsp;sq&nbsp;mi) or 43.5% is used for agricultural purposes, while 5.97&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup> (2.31&nbsp;sq&nbsp;mi) or 31.0% is forested. Of the rest of the land, 4.92&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup> (1.90&nbsp;sq&nbsp;mi) or 25.5% is settled (buildings or roads), 1.25&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup> (0.48&nbsp;sq&nbsp;mi) or 6.5% is either rivers or lakes and 0.91&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup> (0.35&nbsp;sq&nbsp;mi) or 4.7% is unproductive land. </p>
      <p>Of the built up area, industrial buildings made up 1.4% of the total area while housing and buildings made up 10.3% and transportation infrastructure made up 7.6%. Power and water infrastructure as well as other special developed areas made up 2.6% of the area while parks, green belts and sports fields made up 3.6%. Out of the forested land, 28.3% of the total land area is heavily forested and 2.7% is covered with orchards or small clusters of trees. Of the agricultural land, 31.2% is used for growing crops, while 2.1% is used for orchards or vine crops and 10.3% is used for alpine pastures. Of the water in the municipality, 0.8% is in lakes and 5.7% is in rivers and streams. Of the unproductive areas, 4.6% is unproductive vegetation.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The municipality is the capital of its district. Locarno is located on the left shore of Lake Maggiore. The city is made up of the old town (historic settlement center), the new town (Nuovo quartiere) toward the lake and the land district (quartiere Campagna) toward Solduno. The area of the municipality extends from the lake (elevation 209&nbsp;m (686&nbsp;ft)) to the mountains above the city (Monti della SS Trinità, Bre, Cardada and Cimetta, highest point at 1,474&nbsp;m (4,836&nbsp;ft)). It includes a large part of the Magadino valley along with the right side of the <a href="/wiki/Ticino_(river)" title="Ticino (river)">Ticino river</a>, and stretches from the Bolle di Magadino to Monda Contone. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Locarno averages 103 days of precipitation per year and on average receives 1,668&nbsp;mm (65.7&nbsp;in) of <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>. The wettest month is May during which time Locarno receives an average of 194&nbsp;mm (7.6&nbsp;in) of precipitation. During this month there are 12.8 days with measurable precipitation. The driest month of the year is December with an average of 61&nbsp;mm (2.4&nbsp;in) of precipitation over 5.7 days. Locarno averages 2155 hours of sunshine or 56% of possible sunshine.<sup id="cite_ref-MeteoSwiss_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MeteoSwiss-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen-Geiger_climate_classification_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Köppen-Geiger climate classification system">Köppen-Geiger climate classification system</a> classifies its climate as on the border between temperate <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic</a> (Cfb) and <a href="/wiki/Humid_subtropical" class="mw-redirect" title="Humid subtropical">humid subtropical</a> (<i>Cfa</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

      <p>Locarno has a population (as of December 2018<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>) of 15,826.<sup id="cite_ref-Stat2018_TI_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stat2018_TI-15">[15]</a></sup> As of 2008<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, 33.9% of the population are resident foreign nationals.<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_superweb_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_superweb-16">[16]</a></sup> Over the last 10 years (1997–2007) the population has changed at a rate of 3.7%. </p>
      <p>Most of the population (as of 2000<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>) speaks Italian (76.6%), with German being second most common (10.5%) and <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Serbo-Croatian language">Serbo-Croatian</a> being third (3.1%).<sup id="cite_ref-STAT2000_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-STAT2000-17">[17]</a></sup> Of the Swiss national languages (as of 2000<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>), 1,528 speak German, 189 people speak <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, 11,153 people speak Italian, and 27 people speak <a href="/wiki/Romansh_language" title="Romansh language">Romansh</a>. The remainder (1,664 people) speak another language.<sup id="cite_ref-Lang_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lang-18">[18]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>As of 2008<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the gender distribution of the population was 46.5% male and 53.5% female. The population was made up of 4,421 Swiss men (29.1% of the population), and 2,636 (17.4%) non-Swiss men. There were 5,654 Swiss women (37.2%), and 2,474 (16.3%) non-Swiss women.<sup id="cite_ref-Resident_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Resident-19">[19]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 2008<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> there were 83 live births to Swiss citizens and 45 births to non-Swiss citizens, and in same time span there were 115 deaths of Swiss citizens and 33 non-Swiss citizen deaths. Ignoring immigration and emigration, the population of Swiss citizens decreased by 32 while the foreign population increased by 12. There were 14 Swiss men and 3 Swiss women who immigrated back to Switzerland. At the same time, there were 87 non-Swiss men and 78 non-Swiss women who immigrated from another country to Switzerland. The total Swiss population change in 2008 (from all sources, including moves across municipal borders) was an increase of 159 and the non-Swiss population change was an increase of 55 people. This represents a <a href="/wiki/Population_growth#Population_growth_rate" title="Population growth">population growth rate</a> of 1.4%.<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_superweb_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS_superweb-16">[16]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The age distribution, as of 2009<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, in Locarno is; 1,205 children or 7.9% of the population are between 0 and 9 years old and 1,454 teenagers or 9.6% are between 10 and 19. Of the adult population, 1,791 people or 11.8% of the population are between 20 and 29 years old. 2,002 people or 13.2% are between 30 and 39, 2,442 people or 16.1% are between 40 and 49, and 1,979 people or 13.0% are between 50 and 59. The senior population distribution is 1,767 people or 11.6% of the population are between 60 and 69 years old, 1,541 people or 10.1% are between 70 and 79, there are 1,004 people or 6.6% who are over 80.<sup id="cite_ref-Resident_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Resident-19">[19]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>As of 2000<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were 6,730 private households in the municipality, and an average of 2.1 persons per household.<sup id="cite_ref-SFSO_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-20">[20]</a></sup> In 2000<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> there were 904 single family homes (or 42.1% of the total) out of a total of 2,147 inhabited buildings. There were 252 two family buildings (11.7%) and 480 multi-family buildings (22.4%). There were also 511 buildings in the municipality that were multipurpose buildings (used for both housing and commercial or another purpose).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The vacancy rate for the municipality, in 2008<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, was 0.63%. In 2000<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> there were 8,647 apartments in the municipality. The most common apartment size was the 3 room apartment of which there were 3,068. There were 856 single room apartments and 877 apartments with five or more rooms.<sup id="cite_ref-09.02.02_Abitazioni_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-09.02.02_Abitazioni-22">[22]</a></sup> Of these apartments, a total of 6,709 apartments (77.6% of the total) were permanently occupied, while 1,695 apartments (19.6%) were seasonally occupied and 243 apartments (2.8%) were empty.<sup id="cite_ref-09.02.02_Abitazioni_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-09.02.02_Abitazioni-22">[22]</a></sup> As of 2007<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the construction rate of new housing units was 10.5 new units per 1000 residents.<sup id="cite_ref-SFSO_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-20">[20]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>As of 2003<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the average price to rent an average apartment in Locarno was 1046.19 <a href="/wiki/Swiss_franc" title="Swiss franc">Swiss francs</a> (CHF) per month (US$840, £470, €670 approx. exchange rate from 2003). The average rate for a one-room apartment was 611.61 CHF (US$490, £280, €390), a two-room apartment was about 816.64 CHF (US$650, £370, €520), a three-room apartment was about 1007.47 CHF (US$810, £450, €640) and a six or more room apartment cost an average of 1896.51 CHF (US$1520, £850, €1210). The average apartment price in Locarno was 93.7% of the national average of 1116 CHF.<sup id="cite_ref-BFS_rent_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BFS_rent-23">[23]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Heritage_sites_of_national_significance">Heritage sites of national significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Heritage sites of national significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>There are nine Swiss <a href="/wiki/Swiss_inventory_of_cultural_property_of_national_and_regional_significance" class="mw-redirect" title="Swiss inventory of cultural property of national and regional significance">heritage site of national significance</a> in Locarno. Three of the sites are churches; the church of S. Francesco and former convent, the church of S. Maria Assunta (new church) and house of the <a href="/wiki/Canon_(priest)" title="Canon (priest)">canons</a> and the church of S. Maria in Selva with Cemetery. The <i>Castello Visconteo</i> complex (part of which may have been designed by Leonardo da Vinci<sup id="cite_ref-Swissinfo_11_Aug_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Swissinfo_11_Aug-9">[9]</a></sup>) is on the list. Two schools, the Ai Saleggi primary school and the Secondary School at via Dr. G. Varesi 30, as well as the Cantonal Library are also listed. The last two are the Pinacoteca comunale Casa Rusca at piazza Sant’Antonio and the Casorella at Via Bartolomeo Rusca 5 make up the rest of the list. The entire city of Locarno is listed on the <a href="/wiki/Inventory_of_Swiss_Heritage_Sites" title="Inventory of Swiss Heritage Sites">Inventory of Swiss Heritage Sites</a><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> </p>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Astrovia_Locarno">Astrovia Locarno</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Astrovia Locarno">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The Astrovia Locarno is a 1&nbsp;: 1,000,000,000 scale model of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>. The Sun can be found at the end of Via Gioacchino Respini where the cycle path, which runs alongside the river Maggia, starts. <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a>, the final planet in the model, can be found 6 kilometres (4 miles) away from this starting point in the village of Tegna. </p> 
      <h3><span id="Madonna_del_Sasso.2C_Cardada_and_Cimetta"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Madonna_del_Sasso,_Cardada_and_Cimetta">Madonna del Sasso, Cardada and Cimetta</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Madonna del Sasso, Cardada and Cimetta">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The sanctuary of <a href="/wiki/Madonna_del_Sasso,_Switzerland" title="Madonna del Sasso, Switzerland">Madonna del Sasso</a> in <a href="/wiki/Orselina" title="Orselina">Orselina</a> above the city is the principal sight and goal of pilgrimage in the city. </p>
      <p>The founding of the sanctuary goes back to a vision of the Virgin Mary that the <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" class="mw-redirect" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> brother Bartolomeo d'Ivrea experienced in the night of 14/15 August 1480. The interior is highly decorated, and a platform has views of the city. </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Locarno%E2%80%93Madonna_del_Sasso_funicular" title="Locarno–Madonna del Sasso funicular">Locarno–Madonna del Sasso funicular</a> links Locarno city centre with the Madonna del Sasso sanctuary and Orselina. From Orselina, a <a href="/wiki/Aerial_lift" title="Aerial lift">cable car</a> operates to the top of <a href="/wiki/Cardada" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardada">Cardada</a> (el. 1,340&nbsp;m (4,400&nbsp;ft)), and a <a href="/wiki/Chair_lift" class="mw-redirect" title="Chair lift">chair lift</a> goes further to the top of <a href="/wiki/Cimetta" title="Cimetta">Cimetta</a> (el. 1,671&nbsp;m (5,482&nbsp;ft)). </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Castello_Visconteo">Castello Visconteo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Castello Visconteo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Visconteo_Castle_(Locarno)" class="mw-redirect" title="Visconteo Castle (Locarno)">Castello Visconteo</a>, on the edge of the old town, was built in the 12th century, probably as the residence of a Captain Orelli, who remained true to the Emperor. In 1260, it fell into the hands of the <a href="/wiki/Ghibelline" class="mw-redirect" title="Ghibelline">Ghibellines</a>. In 1342 the Visconti of Milan, for whom it is now named, attacked the castle from both the land and the lake side and took it. It first came into the hands of the <i><a href="/wiki/Old_Swiss_Confederation" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Swiss Confederation">Eidgenossen</a></i> in 1503. Today, only a fifth of the original structure remains. Most of that dates from the 15th and 16th centuries. Only the foundation remains from the original structure. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In the 2007 <a href="/wiki/Swiss_federal_election,_2007" class="mw-redirect" title="Swiss federal election, 2007">federal election</a> the most popular party was the <a href="/wiki/FDP.The_Liberals" title="FDP.The Liberals">FDP</a> which received 34.88% of the vote. The next three most popular parties were the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Switzerland" title="Social Democratic Party of Switzerland">SP</a> (19.72%), the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_People%27s_Party_of_Switzerland" title="Christian Democratic People's Party of Switzerland">CVP</a> (18.75%) and the <a href="/wiki/Ticino_League" title="Ticino League">Ticino League</a> (9.54%). In the federal election, a total of 3,303 votes were cast, and the <a href="/wiki/Voter_turnout" title="Voter turnout">voter turnout</a> was 40.5%.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the 2007<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Ticino#Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Ticino">Gran Consiglio</a></i> election, there were a total of 8,555 registered voters in Locarno, of which 4,291 or 50.2% voted. 90 blank ballots and 15 null ballots were cast, leaving 4,186 valid ballots in the election. The most popular party was the <a href="/wiki/FDP.The_Liberals" title="FDP.The Liberals">PLRT</a> which received 913 or 21.8% of the vote. The next three most popular parties were; the SSI (with 822 or 19.6%), the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Switzerland" title="Social Democratic Party of Switzerland">PS</a> (with 790 or 18.9%) and the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_People%27s_Party_of_Switzerland" title="Christian Democratic People's Party of Switzerland">PPD</a>+GenGiova (with 703 or 16.8%).<sup id="cite_ref-Election_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Election-26">[26]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the 2007<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> <i>Consiglio di Stato</i> election, 66 blank ballots and 23 null ballots were cast, leaving 4,202 valid ballots in the election. The most popular party was the PS which received 946 or 22.5% of the vote. The next three most popular parties were; the PLRT (with 837 or 19.9%), the SSI (with 768 or 18.3%) and the PPD (with 714 or 17.0%).<sup id="cite_ref-Election_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Election-26">[26]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>As of &nbsp;2007<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Locarno had an unemployment rate of 5.93%. As of 2005<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were 86 people employed in the <a href="/wiki/Primary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Primary sector of the economy">primary economic sector</a> and about 23 businesses involved in this sector. 2,385 people were employed in the <a href="/wiki/Secondary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Secondary sector of the economy">secondary sector</a> and there were 158 businesses in this sector. 7,338 people were employed in the <a href="/wiki/Tertiary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Tertiary sector of the economy">tertiary sector</a>, with 920 businesses in this sector.<sup id="cite_ref-SFSO_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-20">[20]</a></sup> There were 6,688 residents of the municipality who were employed in some capacity, of which females made up 46.6% of the workforce. </p>
      <p>In 2000<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were 7,550 workers who commuted into the municipality and 2,864 workers who commuted away. The municipality is a net importer of workers, with about 2.6 workers entering the municipality for every one leaving. About 12.1% of the workforce coming into Locarno are coming from outside Switzerland, while none of the locals counted in the census, commute out of Switzerland for work.<sup id="cite_ref-commuter_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-commuter-27">[27]</a></sup> Of the working population, 10.5% used public transportation to get to work, and 44.1% used a private car.<sup id="cite_ref-SFSO_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-20">[20]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In Locarno about 57.1% of the population (between age 25-64) have completed either non-mandatory <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Switzerland#Secondary" title="Education in Switzerland">upper secondary education</a> or additional higher education (either university or a <i><a href="/wiki/Fachhochschule" title="Fachhochschule">Fachhochschule</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-SFSO_20-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-20">[20]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In Locarno there were a total of 2,210 students (as of 2009<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>). The Ticino <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Switzerland" title="Education in Switzerland">education system</a> provides up to three years of non-mandatory <a href="/wiki/Kindergarten" title="Kindergarten">kindergarten</a> and in Locarno there were 292 children in kindergarten. The primary school program lasts for five years and includes both a standard school and a special school. In the village, 648 students attended the standard primary schools and 65 students attended the special school. In the lower secondary school system, students either attend a two-year middle school followed by a two-year pre-apprenticeship or they attend a four-year program to prepare for higher education. There were 512 students in the two-year middle school and 10 in their pre-apprenticeship, while 203 students were in the four-year advanced program. </p>
      <p>The upper secondary school includes several options, but at the end of the upper secondary program, a student will be prepared to enter a trade or to continue on to a university or college. In Ticino, vocational students may either attend school while working on their internship or apprenticeship (which takes three or four years) or may attend school followed by an internship or apprenticeship (which takes one year as a full-time student or one and a half to two years as a part-time student).<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> There were 146 vocational students who were attending school full-time and 293 who attend part-time. </p>
      <p>The professional program lasts three years and prepares a student for a job in engineering, nursing, computer science, business, tourism and similar fields. There were 41 students in the professional program.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>As of 2000<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were 1,484 students in Locarno who came from another municipality, while 405 residents attended schools outside the municipality.<sup id="cite_ref-commuter_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-commuter-27">[27]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Locarno is home to the <i>Biblioteca Cantonale Locarno</i> library. The library has (as of 2008<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>) 122,115 books or other media, and loaned out 97,667 items in the same year. It was open a total of 264 days with average of 44 hours per week during that year.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crime">Crime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Locarno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Crime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In 2014 the crime rate, of the over 200 crimes listed in the <a href="/wiki/Strafgesetzbuch_(Switzerland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Strafgesetzbuch (Switzerland)">Swiss Criminal Code</a> (running from murder, robbery and assault to accepting bribes and election fraud), in Locarno was 77.9 per thousand residents, which was slightly higher than the national average (64.6). During the same period, the rate of drug crimes was 27.2 per thousand residents. This rate is 138.6% greater than the rate in the district, 209.1% greater than the cantonal rate and 174.7% greater than the national rate. The rate of violations of immigration, visa and work permit laws was 2.6 per thousand residents. This rate is lower than average, only 72.2% of the rate in the canton and only 53.1% of the rate for the entire country.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> </p>
