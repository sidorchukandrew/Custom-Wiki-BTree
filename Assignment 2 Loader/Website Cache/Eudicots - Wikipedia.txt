      <p>The <b>eudicots</b>, <b>Eudicotidae</b> or <b>eudicotyledons</b> are a <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> of <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plants</a> that had been called <b>tricolpates</b> or <b>non-<a href="/wiki/Magnoliid" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnoliid">magnoliid</a> dicots</b> by previous authors. The botanical terms were introduced in 1991 by evolutionary botanist <a href="/w/index.php?title=James_A._Doyle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="James A. Doyle (page does not exist)">James A. Doyle</a> and paleobotanist Carol L. Hotton to emphasize the later evolutionary divergence of tricolpate <a href="/wiki/Dicotyledon" title="Dicotyledon">dicots</a> from earlier, less specialized, dicots.<sup id="cite_ref-Endress_2002_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Endress_2002-1">[1]</a></sup> The close relationships among flowering plants with tricolpate pollen grains was initially seen in morphological studies of <a href="/wiki/Synapomorphy" class="mw-redirect" title="Synapomorphy">shared derived characters</a>. These plants have a distinct trait in their pollen grains of exhibiting three colpi or grooves paralleling the polar axis<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>. Later <a href="/wiki/Molecular" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular">molecular</a> evidence confirmed the genetic basis for the evolutionary relationships among flowering plants with tricolpate pollen grains and dicotyledonous traits. The term means "true dicotyledons", as it contains the majority of plants that have been considered dicots and have <a href="/wiki/Phenotypic_trait" title="Phenotypic trait">characteristics</a> of the dicots. The term "eudicots" has subsequently been widely adopted in <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botany</a> to refer to one of the two largest clades of <a href="/wiki/Angiosperm" class="mw-redirect" title="Angiosperm">angiosperms</a> (constituting over 70% of the angiosperm species), <a href="/wiki/Monocots" class="mw-redirect" title="Monocots">monocots</a> being the other. The remaining angiosperms include <a href="/wiki/Magnoliids" title="Magnoliids">magnoliids</a> and what are sometimes referred to as <a href="/wiki/Basal_angiosperms" title="Basal angiosperms">basal angiosperms</a> or paleodicots, but these terms have not been widely or consistently adopted, as they do not refer to a <a href="/wiki/Monophyletic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monophyletic">monophyletic</a> group. </p>
      <p>The other name for the eudicots is <b>tricolpates</b>, a name which refers to the grooved structure of the <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a>. Members of the group have tricolpate pollen, or forms derived from it. These pollens have three or more pores set in furrows called colpi. In contrast, most of the other <a href="/wiki/Seed_plants" class="mw-redirect" title="Seed plants">seed plants</a> (that is the <a href="/wiki/Gymnosperms" class="mw-redirect" title="Gymnosperms">gymnosperms</a>, the monocots and the paleodicots) produce monosulcate pollen, with a single pore set in a differently oriented groove called the sulcus. The name "tricolpates" is preferred by some botanists to avoid confusion with the dicots, a nonmonophyletic group.<sup id="cite_ref-Judd04_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Judd04-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Numerous familiar plants are eudicots, including many common food plants, trees, and ornamentals. Some common and familiar eudicots include members of the <a href="/wiki/Asteraceae" title="Asteraceae">sunflower family</a> such as the common <a href="/wiki/Dandelion" class="mw-redirect" title="Dandelion">dandelion</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Forget-me-not" class="mw-redirect" title="Forget-me-not">forget-me-not</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cabbage" title="Cabbage">cabbage</a> and other members of <a href="/wiki/Brassicaceae" title="Brassicaceae">its family</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buttercup" class="mw-redirect" title="Buttercup">buttercup</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maple" title="Maple">maple</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Macadamia" title="Macadamia">macadamia</a>. Most leafy trees of midlatitudes also belong to eudicots, with notable exceptions being magnolias and tulip trees which belong to <a href="/wiki/Magnoliids" title="Magnoliids">magnoliids</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Ginkgo_biloba" title="Ginkgo biloba">Ginkgo biloba</a></i>, which is not an <a href="/wiki/Angiosperm" class="mw-redirect" title="Angiosperm">angiosperm</a>. </p>
      <p>The name "eudicots" (plural) is used in the <a href="/wiki/APG_system" title="APG system">APG system</a>, of 1998, and <a href="/wiki/APG_II_system" title="APG II system">APG II system</a>, of 2003, for classification of angiosperms. It is applied to a <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a>, a monophyletic group, which includes most of the (former) dicots. </p>
      <p><b>"Tricolpate"</b> is a synonym for the "<a href="/wiki/Eudicot" class="mw-redirect" title="Eudicot">Eudicot</a>" <a href="/wiki/Monophyletic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monophyletic">monophyletic</a> group, the "true <a href="/wiki/Dicotyledons" class="mw-redirect" title="Dicotyledons">dicotyledons</a>" (which are distinguished from all other flowering plants by their <b>tricolpate</b> <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a> structure). The number of <a href="/wiki/Pollen_grain" class="mw-redirect" title="Pollen grain">pollen grain</a> <i>furrows</i> or <i>pores</i> helps classify the <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plants</a>, with <a class="mw-selflink selflink">eudicots</a> having three <a href="/wiki/Colpus" class="mw-redirect" title="Colpus">colpi</a> (<b>tricolpate</b>), and other groups having one <a href="/wiki/Sulcus_(anatomy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sulcus (anatomy)">sulcus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Judd04_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Judd04-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Pollen <a href="/wiki/Aperture_(botany)" title="Aperture (botany)">apertures</a> are any modification of the wall of the pollen grain. These modifications include thinning, ridges and pores, they serve as an exit for the pollen contents and allow shrinking and swelling of the grain caused by changes in moisture content. The elongated apertures/ furrows in the pollen grain are called <i>colpi</i> (singular <i>colpus</i>), which, along with pores, are a chief criterion for identifying the pollen classes.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subdivisions">Subdivisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eudicots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Subdivisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The eudicots can be divided into two groups: the basal eudicots and the core eudicots.<sup id="cite_ref-worberg2007_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-worberg2007-6">[6]</a></sup> Basal eudicot is an informal name for a <a href="/wiki/Paraphyletic" class="mw-redirect" title="Paraphyletic">paraphyletic</a> group. The core eudicots are a monophyletic group.<sup id="cite_ref-soltis2005_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soltis2005-7">[7]</a></sup> A 2010 study suggested the core eudicots can be divided into two clades, <a href="/wiki/Gunnerales" title="Gunnerales">Gunnerales</a> and a clade called <b>Pentapetalae</b>, comprising all the remaining core eudicots.<sup id="cite_ref-MoorSoltBellBurl10_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoorSoltBellBurl10-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The Pentapetalae can be then divided into three clades: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li><a href="/wiki/Dilleniales" title="Dilleniales">Dilleniales</a></li> 
       <li>superrosids consisting of <a href="/wiki/Saxifragales" title="Saxifragales">Saxifragales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rosids" title="Rosids">rosids</a> (the <a href="/wiki/APG_IV_system" title="APG IV system">APG IV system</a> includes the Vitales in the rosids)</li> 
       <li>superasterids consisting of <a href="/wiki/Santalales" title="Santalales">Santalales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berberidopsidales" title="Berberidopsidales">Berberidopsidales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caryophyllales" title="Caryophyllales">Caryophyllales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asterids" title="Asterids">asterids</a></li>
      </ul>
