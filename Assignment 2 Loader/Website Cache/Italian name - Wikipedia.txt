   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Italian name</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub" class="noprint">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div> 
    <div id="contentSub"></div> 
    <div id="jump-to-nav"></div> 
    <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a> 
    <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a> 
    <div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>A <b>name in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a></b> consists of a <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> (<i>nome</i>) and a <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> (<i>cognome</i>). Surnames are normally written after given names. In official documents, the surname may be written before given names. In speech, the use of given name before family name is standard in an educated style, but bureaucratic influence caused the opposite to be formerly common. </p>
      <p>Italian names, with their fixed <i>nome</i> + <i>cognome</i> structure, have little to do with the ancient <a href="/wiki/Roman_naming_conventions" title="Roman naming conventions">Roman naming conventions</a>, which used a tripartite system of <a href="/wiki/Praenomen" title="Praenomen">given name</a> + <a href="/wiki/Roman_naming_conventions#Nomen" title="Roman naming conventions">gentile name</a> + <a href="/wiki/Cognomen" title="Cognomen">hereditary or personal name or names</a>. For example, the Italian <i>nome</i> is not analogous to the ancient Roman <i>nomen</i> since the former is the given name (distinct between siblings), and the latter the gentile name (inherited, thus shared by all belonging to a <a href="/wiki/Gens" title="Gens">gens</a>). Female naming traditions and name changing rules after adoption for both sexes also differ between Roman antiquity and modern Italian use. Moreover, the low number and the steady decline of importance and variety of Roman <i>praenomina</i> starkly contrast with the current number of Italian given names.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In Italy, one's name may also be determined by the <a href="/wiki/Name_day" title="Name day">name day</a>, referred to as his or her "<a href="/wiki/Onomastico" class="mw-redirect" title="Onomastico">Onomastico</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&nbsp;[<a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onomastico" class="extiw" title="it:Onomastico">it</a>]</span>". Name days are determined according to the <i><a href="/wiki/Sanctorale" title="Sanctorale">Sanctorale</a></i>, a cycle found in the <a href="/wiki/General_Roman_Calendar" title="General Roman Calendar">General Roman Calendar</a> giving almost each day a few saints, so different names may celebrated on the same day.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Traditionally, parents fix the name day of their child at <a href="/wiki/Infant_baptism" title="Infant baptism">christening</a>, according to the favourite saint in case of different ones (on different days) with the same name, and the child will carry it all along its life. In the case of multiple given names, the child will celebrate only one, usually the first. </p>

      <p>A few names end with an accented vowel, for instance <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2" title="Niccolò">Niccolò</a> and <a href="/wiki/Giosu%C3%A8_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Giosuè (disambiguation)">Giosuè</a>. </p>
      <p>Almost every base name can have a diminutive form ending with -ino/-ina or -etto/etta as in Paolino/Paoletto and Paolina/Paoletta from Paolo and Paola, -ello/-ella, as in Donatello/Donatella from Donato and Donata, or -uccio/-uccia, as in Guiduccio from Guido. The forms -uzzo/-uzza, as in Santuzza from Santa, are typical of <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">Sicilian language</a>. </p>

      <p>Since the ancient Romans had a very limited stock of given names (<i>praenomina</i>), very few modern Italian given names (<i>nomi</i>) are derived directly from the classical ones. A rare example would be <i><a href="/wiki/Marco_(given_name)" title="Marco (given name)">Marco</a></i> (from <a href="/wiki/Marcus_(given_name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Marcus (given name)">Marcus</a>). </p>
      <p>Some <i>nomi</i> were taken from classical clan names (<i>nomina</i>)—for their meanings or because they are euphonic, such as <a href="/wiki/Emilio_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Emilio (disambiguation)">Emilio</a>/<a href="/wiki/Emilia_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Emilia (name)">Emilia</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Aemilia_(gens)" title="Aemilia (gens)">Aemilius</a>), <a href="/wiki/Valerio" title="Valerio">Valerio</a>/<a href="/wiki/Valeria_(given_name)" title="Valeria (given name)">Valeria</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Valerius" class="mw-redirect" title="Valerius">Valerius</a>), <a href="/wiki/Claudio_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Claudio (disambiguation)">Claudio</a>/<a href="/wiki/Claudia_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Claudia (disambiguation)">Claudia</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Claudius_(gens)" class="mw-redirect" title="Claudius (gens)">Claudius</a>), Orazio (from <a href="/wiki/Horatius_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Horatius (disambiguation)">Horatius</a>), <a href="/wiki/Fabio" title="Fabio">Fabio</a> (from the <i>cognomen</i> <a href="/wiki/Fabius" class="mw-redirect" title="Fabius">Fabius</a>), <a href="/wiki/Flavio" title="Flavio">Flavio</a>/<a href="/wiki/Flavia_(name)" title="Flavia (name)">Flavia</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Flavius" class="mw-redirect" title="Flavius">Flavius</a>) and Fulvio from Fulvius. </p>
      <p>When combined with a second given name, <i>Giovanni</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pietro" title="Pietro">Pietro</a></i> are commonly contracted to <i>Gian-</i> and <i>Pier-</i>, as in <i><a href="/wiki/Giancarlo" title="Giancarlo">Giancarlo</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Gianfranco" title="Gianfranco">Gianfranco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gianluca" title="Gianluca">Gianluca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gianluigi" title="Gianluigi">Gianluigi</a>, Gianmaria, Giampaolo (Gianpaolo), Giampiero (Gianpiero), Giambattista, Pierangelo, Pierantonio, Pierfranco, Pierluigi, Piermaria, Pierpaolo, </i> and so on. </p>
      <p>Italian <a href="/wiki/Unisex_name" title="Unisex name">unisex names</a> are very rare (e.g. <i>Celeste</i>), but the feminine name <i><a href="/wiki/Maria_(given_name)" title="Maria (given name)">Maria</a></i> is common as a masculine second name, as in <i>Gianmaria</i>, <i>Carlo Maria</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Antonio" title="Antonio">Anton</a> Maria</i> etc. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surnames">Surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Italy has the largest collection of surnames (<i>cognomi</i>) of any country in the world, with over 350,000.<sup id="cite_ref-Corriere_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Corriere-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Men—except slaves—in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Rome</a> always had hereditary surnames, i.e., <i>nomen</i> (clan name) and <i>cognomen</i> (side-clan name). However, the multi-name tradition was lost by the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. Outside the aristocracy, where surnames were often <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> or those of manors or fiefs, most Italians began to assume hereditary surnames around 1450. </p>
      <p>Registration of baptisms and marriages became mandatory in parishes after the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trento" class="mw-redirect" title="Council of Trento">Council of Trento</a> in 1564.<sup id="cite_ref-world_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-world-8">[8]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Suffixes">Suffixes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Suffixes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>A large number of Italian surnames end in <i>i</i>, due to the medieval Italian habit of identifying families by the name of the ancestors in the plural (which have an <i>-i</i> suffix in Italian). For instance, Filippo from the Ormanno family (<i>gli Ormanni</i>) would be called "signor Filippo degli Ormanni" ("Mr. Filippo of the Ormannos"). In time, the middle possessive portion ("of the") was dropped, but surnames became permanently pluralized and never referred to in the singular, even for a single person. Filippo Ormanno would therefore be known as Filippo Ormanni.<sup id="cite_ref-Hall_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hall-9">[9]</a></sup> Some families, however, opted to retain the possessive portion of their surnames, for instance <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_de%27_Medici" title="Lorenzo de' Medici">Lorenzo de' Medici</a> literally means "Lorenzo of the Medici" (<i>de'</i> is a contraction of <i>dei</i>, also meaning "of the"; c.f. <a href="/wiki/The_Medicis" class="mw-redirect" title="The Medicis">The Medicis</a>). </p>
      <p>Some common suffixes indicate endearment (which may also become pluralized and receive an <i>-i</i> ending), for example: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li><i>-ello/illo/etto/ino</i> (<a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">diminutive</a> "little"), e.g., Bernardello, Vettorello, Iannuccillo, Bortoletto, Bernardino, Ravellino, Verdino</li> 
       <li><i>-one/ne</i> (<a href="/wiki/Augmentative" title="Augmentative">augmentative</a> "big"), e.g., Mangione, Bellone, Capone, Pastene, Mantone, Vallone</li> 
       <li><i>-accio/azzo/asso</i> (<a href="/wiki/Pejorative" title="Pejorative">pejorative</a><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>), e.g., Boccaccio, Terrazzo, Varasso</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>Other endings are characteristic of certain regions:<sup id="cite_ref-Corriere_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Corriere-6">[6]</a></sup> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li><a href="/wiki/Veneto" title="Veneto">Veneto</a>: <i>-asso</i>, <i>-ato/ati</i>, and consonants (<i>l</i>, <i>n</i>, <i>r</i>); <i>-on</i>: Bissacco, Zoccarato, Cavinato, Brombal, Bordin, Meneghin, Perin, Vazzoler, Peron, Francescon, Zanon, Fanton</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>: <i>-aro</i>, <i>-isi</i> and "osso": Cavallaro, Cherisi, Rosi, Rosso (Sicily, Piedmont and Veneto)</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">Lombardy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Piemont" class="mw-redirect" title="Piemont">Piemont</a>: <i>-ago/ghi</i> (of <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic</a> derivation), <i>-engo/enghi</i> (of <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic</a> derivation): Salmoiraghi, Ornaghi, Vernengo, Martinengo, Giordanengo, Lambertenghi</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">Lombardy</a>: <i>-ate/ati/atti</i>: Lunati, Bonatti, Moratti, Orsatti</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">Piedmont</a>: <i>-ero</i>, <i>-audi</i>, <i>-asco</i>,<i>-zzi</i>, <i>-anti</i>, <i>-ini</i>: Ferrero, Rambaudi, Comaco, Bonazzi, Santi, Baldovini</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Friuli" title="Friuli">Friuli</a>: <i>-otti/utti</i> and <i>-t</i>: Bortolotti, Pascutti, Codutti, Rigonat, Ret</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a>: <i>-ai</i> and <i>-aci/ecci/ucci</i>: Bollai, Balducci, Martaci</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a>: <i>-u</i>, <i>-as</i> and <i>-is</i>, derived from the <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_language" title="Sardinian language">Sardinian language</a>: Pusceddu, Cadeddu, Schirru, Marras, Argiolas, Floris, Melis, Abis</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Calabria" title="Calabria">Calabria</a>: <i>-ace</i>: Storace, Versace</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Campania" title="Campania">Campania</a>: <i>-iello</i>: Borriello, Aiello, Manganiello</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Abruzzo" title="Abruzzo">Abruzzo</a>: <i>-us</i>, <i>-is</i> and <i>-iis</i> that stem from traditional Latin names: Fidelibus, De Sanctis, De Laurentiis<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
      </ul> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>As in most other European naming traditions, <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymics</a> are common. Originally they were indicated by a possessive, e.g., Francesco de Bernardo, meaning "Francis (the son) of Bernard". <i>De Luca</i> ("[son] of Luke") remains one of the most common Italian surnames. However, <i>de</i> ("of") was often dropped and suffixes added, hence <i>de Bernardo</i> evolved to be <i>Bernardo</i> and eventually pluralized as <i>Bernardi</i> (see <a href="#Suffixes">Suffixes</a> above). </p>

      <p>Nicknames, referring to physical attributes or mannerism, also gave rise to some family names, e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Rossi_(surname)" title="Rossi (surname)">Rossi</a></i> (from <i><a href="/wiki/Rosso_(surname)" title="Rosso (surname)">rosso</a></i> "<a href="/wiki/Red_hair" title="Red hair">redhead</a>"), <i>Basso</i> ("short"), <i>Caporaso</i> ("shaved or bald head"), <i>Pappalardo</i> ("lard-eater", originally an abusive nickname for one who professed himself a devout person but ate meat and fatty dishes in forbidden times),<sup id="cite_ref-cognomi_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cognomi-12">[12]</a></sup> and <i>Barbagelata</i> ("frozen beard"). </p>
      <p>A few family names are still in the original <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, like <i>Santorum</i>, <i>De Juliis</i> and <i>De Laurentiis</i>, reflecting that the family name has been preserved from <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> sources as a part of their business or household documentation or church records. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Articles">Articles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Italian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Articles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The traditional rule, which is the common usage especially in Tuscany, is that in referring to people by their surnames alone, the definite article should be used (<i>il</i> for most parts, <i>lo</i> before some consonants and consonant clusters and <i>l<span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span></i> before vowels).<sup id="cite_ref-Hall_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hall-9">[9]</a></sup> <i>Mario Russo</i>, therefore, is called <i>il Russo</i> ("the Russo"). Now, some prefer to use the article only or chiefly for historical surnames ("l'Ariosto", "il Manzoni", etc.) </p>
      <p>Male given names are never preceded by an article except in popular northern regional usage. </p>
      <p>However, in Tuscany and the rest of Northern Italy, given names of females are usually preceded by articles (<i>la Maria</i>, <i>la Gianna</i>) unless one is speaking of a woman who is personally unknown (such as <i>Cleopatra</i>, <i>Maria Stuarda</i>, with no article).<sup id="cite_ref-Meyer-Lübke_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meyer-Lübke-13">[13]</a></sup> That is also the traditional grammar rule. </p>
      <p>Articles are also used (more often than with those of men) with the surnames of women: <i>Gianni Rossi</i> can be called <i>il Rossi</i> or (especially nowadays) simply <i>Rossi</i>, but <i>Maria Bianchi</i> is usually <i>la Bianchi</i> (also <i>la Maria Bianchi</i>). </p>
      <p>Placing the surname before the name is considered incorrect except in bureaucratic usage and is often stigmatised as a shibboleth of illiteracy. </p>
      <p>Names that are derived from possessions of noble families normally never had articles preceding them such as the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Farnese" title="House of Farnese">House of Farnese</a> (from a territorial holding) and the <a href="/wiki/Cornaro_family" title="Cornaro family">Cornaro family</a> (from a <a href="/wiki/Prince-bishop" title="Prince-bishop">prince-bishopric</a>). Articles were omitted also for surnames with an identifiable foreign origin (including Latin ones) such as <i>Cicerone</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hall_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hall-9">[9]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>That practice somewhat resembles the Greek custom of placing definite articles before <i>all</i> names (see <a href="/wiki/Greek_name" title="Greek name">Greek names</a>). The Greco-Italian practice even spread to French in the 17th century, especially in writings regarding figures in literature and painting such as <i>le <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Poussin" title="Nicolas Poussin">Poussin</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hall_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hall-9">[9]</a></sup> For example, some Italian surnames of Greek sound descent: <i>Papasidero</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-cognomi_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cognomi-12">[12]</a></sup> <i>Papadopulo</i>. </p>
