      <p class="mw-empty-elt"> </p>
      <p>The <b>naming customs of Hispanic America</b> are similar to the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_naming_customs" title="Spanish naming customs">Spanish naming customs</a> practiced in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, with some modifications to the surname rules. Many Hispanophones in the countries of <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_America" title="Hispanic America">Hispanic America</a> have two given names, plus a paternal surname (<i>primer apellido</i> or <i>apellido paterno</i>) and a maternal surname (<i>segundo apellido</i> or <i>apellido materno</i>). </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Colonial_Hispanic_America">Colonial Hispanic America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_customs_of_Hispanic_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Colonial Hispanic America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In the colonial period and nineteenth century, it was common to have between one and three given names followed by a second name with a "de" (from) in front. For example, the Saint <a href="/wiki/Teresa_of_the_Andes" title="Teresa of the Andes">Teresa de Los Andes</a> whose real name is Juana Enriqueta Josefina de los Sagrados Corazones Fernández del Solar. Where "Juana", "Enriqueta" and "Josefina" are her first names, followed by the second name "de los Sagrados Corazones". Her paternal surname is "Fernández" and her maternal surname is "del Solar". </p>
      <p>Another form of second name can be preceded by a "de" particle, which can be varied to "del" or "de los". Examples are "José del Pilar", "Rosa del Carmen", "Fidelina de las Mercedes". These second names are only used in formal occasions, and in many cases only registered in the birth, marriage and death certificates. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_day">Modern day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_customs_of_Hispanic_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modern day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Since the late 20th century, owing to US influence, many Hispanic Americans have been baptized with English rather than Spanish given names<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. </p>
      <p>Children who are not recognized by their father or to be raised separately have been legally treated in two ways, changing from time to time according to the civil registration norms. One way is to be registered with only a first surname that is the mother's surname. The second way is to have the mother's surname as first surname and second surname. </p>
      <p>Another case is to only register the father's surname and not giving reference to the mother, in accordance with US naming customs. This can be done to avoid legal and clerical complications in the future. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Argentina">Argentina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_customs_of_Hispanic_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Argentina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Generally speaking, just the paternal surname is used in Argentinian names.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> However, due to the large number of descendants of Spaniards, many Argentines use both surnames. In Argentina, people usually don't replace their surnames by the spouse's one at marriage, but it's pretty common that women add the husband's surname after theirs with a <i>de</i> (of) (María López de Martínez). </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chile">Chile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_customs_of_Hispanic_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Instead of <i>primer apellido</i> (first surname) and <i>segundo apellido</i> (second surname), legally, the following expressions are used: <i>apellido paterno</i> (paternal surname) and <i>apellido materno</i> (maternal surname). Both surnames are equally important and having two surnames is obligation for any person in birth registrations, the use of them are mandatory for any official document.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In Chile people never replace their surnames by the spouse’s ones at marriage. Spouse’s name adoption is not socially practiced and the possibility of so doing is not even contemplated by the law. Although a woman may socially use the marital conjunction <i>de</i>, it is omitted in her legal name. For example, former first lady <a href="/wiki/Marta_Larraechea" title="Marta Larraechea">Marta Larraechea</a> very often is called Marta Larraechea de Frei, but her full legal name remains Marta Larraechea Bolívar. As another example, <a href="/wiki/Soledad_Alvear" title="Soledad Alvear">Soledad Alvear</a> is almost never called Soledad Alvear de Martínez; her full legal name is María Soledad Alvear Valenzuela. This social practice, though, has long ago begun to fall into disfavor and very few women would these days accept to be referred to in this manner. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colombia">Colombia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_customs_of_Hispanic_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Colombia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In Colombia, the use is two surnames: first the paternal surname and then the maternal surname. Married women used to change their second last name for their husband first last name adding the preposition "de" between the two last names. However, in recent years, married women do not change their original family names for their husband's. Children who are not recognized by their father are registered with the two maternal surnames.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span id="Cuba.2C_Dominican_Republic.2C_Nicaragua.2C_and_Puerto_Rico"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuba,_Dominican_Republic,_Nicaragua,_and_Puerto_Rico">Cuba, Dominican Republic, Nicaragua, and Puerto Rico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_customs_of_Hispanic_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cuba, Dominican Republic, Nicaragua, and Puerto Rico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In Cuba, the Dominican Republic, Nicaragua, and Puerto Rico, both men and women carry their two family names (first their father's, and second their mother's). Both are equally important and are mandatory for any official document. Married women never change their original family names for their husband's. Even when they migrate to other countries where this is a common practice, many prefer to adhere to their heritage and keep their maiden name. They also use "de", as explained below. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecuador">Ecuador</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_customs_of_Hispanic_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ecuador">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In Ecuador, a couple can choose the order of their children's surnames. Most choose the traditional order (e.g., <i>Guerrero García</i> in the example above), but some invert the order, putting the mother's paternal surname first and the father's paternal surname last (e.g., <i>García Guerrero</i> from the example above). Such inversion, if chosen, must be consistent for all children of the marriage. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Uruguay">Uruguay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_customs_of_Hispanic_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Uruguay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Uruguayans carry two surnames, as is the practice in most Spanish-speaking countries. Such custom has been recognized under Uruguayan laws No. 15.462<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and 19.075<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>. </p>
      <p>Regarding names, it is a common practice for Uruguayans to carry two names. Under Section 5 of Law No. 15.462, it is forbidden to the Officers of Public Registrars to register "<i>names that are extravagant, ridicule, immoral or that may provoke a misunderstanding regarding the sex of the child to whom it is being imposed.</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Regarding surnames, according to those laws, if no agreement has been reached, the first surname shall be the father's surname (paternal surname), and the second surname shall be the mother's surname (maternal surname, or maiden surname). </p>
      <p>Women do not change their surnames upon marriage in Uruguay. In some instances, such as high society meetings, the partner's surname can be added after the person's surnames using the preposition de (of), but it is not a practice officially or legally provided, recognized or accepted. </p>
      <p>Since 2013, parents may invert this order by mutual agreement, at the naming of the first child of the couple. Subsequent children must be named following the same order, since once the order of the surnames has been established it cannot be changed. If there is no agreement on the order, the rule shall apply depending on the type of couple: in case of heterosexual couples, the order shall be as in general practice (first surname shall be the paternal surname and the second surname shall be the maternal surname). Same-sex parents may choose the order of both surnames of the children (either from birth or adoption) by mutual agreement. In case of disagreement the order of the surnames is determined by draw.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>For example, <a href="/wiki/Natalia_Oreiro" title="Natalia Oreiro">Natalia Marisa Oreiro Iglesias</a> is the daughter of Carlos Florencio Oreiro Poggio and Mabel Cristina Iglesias Bourié. Note that the marriage between her parents did not mean that the mother lost her maiden surnames. </p>
      <p>In Uruguay, foreigners may retain use of their cultural naming customs, yet upon being granted the Uruguayan national identification document called <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9dula_de_Identidad" class="mw-redirect" title="Cédula de Identidad">Cédula de Identidad</a>, they are legally obliged to assume Spanish-style names (a name or two, and two surnames). If the naturalised person is from a one-surname culture (paternal surname), the maiden's name of the mother needs to be obtained, and if such cannot be evidenced, the surname is then duplicated. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Venezuela">Venezuela</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_customs_of_Hispanic_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Venezuela">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In August 2007, a draft law<sup id="cite_ref-CNE_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNE-9">[9]</a></sup> by the Venezuelan <a href="/wiki/National_Electoral_Council_(Venezuela)" title="National Electoral Council (Venezuela)">National Electoral Council</a> thus sought to change the national Venezuelan naming customs: </p> 
      <blockquote>
       <p>'<i>Civil Registry Organic Law Project: Limitation upon the inscription of names</i> Article 106 "...[civil registrars] will not permit... [parents] to place names [upon their children] that expose them to ridicule; that are extravagant or difficult to pronounce in the official language; that contain familiar and colloquial variants that denote a confused identification, or that generate doubts about the determination of the sex. In these cases, the registrar will offer, as reference, a listing of the most common names and surnames... The names of boys, girls, or adolescents of the country's indigenous ethnic groups and the names of foreigners' children are excepted from this disposition...." </p>
      </blockquote> 
      <p>Popular complaint against the naming-custom-limiting Article 106 compelled the Venezuelan National Electoral Council to delete it from the <i>Civil Registry Organic Law Project</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-CNE_Retira_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNE_Retira-10">[10]</a></sup> It could be said that common names like Elvio Lado (which can be pronounced as "el violado", meaning "the raped one") or Mónica Galindo (which can be pronounced as "Moni caga lindo", meaning "Moni shits prettily") would count as an example of violation to this law. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="de"><i>de</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_customs_of_Hispanic_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: de">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In some instances, such as high society meetings, and with no legal value (except in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>), the husband’s surname can be added <i>after</i> the woman’s surnames using the <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjunction" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammatical conjunction">conjunction</a> <b>de</b> ("of"). Thus <i>Leocadia Blanco Álvarez</i>, married to a <i>Pedro Pérez Montilla</i>, may be addressed as <i>Leocadia Blanco de Pérez</i> or as <i>Leocadia Blanco Álvarez de Pérez</i>. This format is not used in everyday settings and has no legal value.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In other nations doing so is frowned upon. The contemporary naming custom now practises the wife retaining her <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a>. The use of the husband’s surname by a wife is typically encountered in social situations where the connection to the husband is being stressed. Her full formal married-name (<i>Ángela López Sáenz de Portillo</i>) is the documentary convention in only some Latin American countries. Where it exists, the custom provides her with <a href="/wiki/Ceremony" title="Ceremony">ceremonial</a> life and death wife-names, <i>Ángela López, Sra. de Portillo</i> (Ángela López, Wife of Portillo) wherein <i>Sra.</i> (<i>señora</i>, "Mrs") connotes "wife"; and <i>Ángela López Sáenz, vda. de Portillo</i> (Ángela López Sáenz, Widow of Portillo), wherein <b>vda.</b> (<i>viuda</i>, "widow") denotes widowhood. </p>
      <p>Some names have the <i>de</i> conjunction without association to marriage at all. Instead they may reflect the geographical origin of the individual or that of the individual ancestors. Thus there are men named <a href="/wiki/Juan_Ponce_de_Le%C3%B3n" title="Juan Ponce de León">Juan Ponce de León</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_de_Guzm%C3%A1n_Ben%C3%ADtez" title="José de Guzmán Benítez">José de Guzmán Benítez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cristi%C3%A1n_de_la_Fuente" title="Cristián de la Fuente">Cristián de la Fuente</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oscar_de_la_Renta" title="Oscar de la Renta">Oscar de la Renta</a>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_implications">Legal implications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_customs_of_Hispanic_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Legal implications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The Hispanic preference for using the <i>first</i> surname from the father over the second surname from the mother occasionally results in serious legal problems in English-speaking countries such as the United States, whose laws operate on the assumption (based upon <a href="/wiki/English_name" title="English name">English names</a>) that each person has a first name, an optional middle name, and a last name, where the last name is normally inherited solely from the father and is assumed to be the <i>only</i> surname. </p>
      <p>For example, in a 2006 decision from one of the <a href="/wiki/California_Courts_of_Appeal" title="California Courts of Appeal">California Courts of Appeal</a>, it was held that a creditor had failed to perfect its <a href="/wiki/Security_interest" title="Security interest">security interest</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Strawberry" title="Strawberry">strawberry</a> crop of a debtor whose full true name was "Armando Muñoz Juárez."<sup id="cite_ref-corona_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-corona-12">[12]</a></sup> In accordance with Mexican naming convention, he frequently went by Armando Muñoz, and signed documents by that name, and the creditor's financing statement therefore referred to him as "Armando Muñoz."<sup id="cite_ref-corona_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-corona-12">[12]</a></sup> The court ruled: "Debtor's last name did not change when he crossed the border into the United States. The 'naming convention' is legally irrelevant[.]"<sup id="cite_ref-corona_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-corona-12">[12]</a></sup> In other words, under the California implementation of the <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Commercial_Code" title="Uniform Commercial Code">Uniform Commercial Code</a>, the debtor's "true last name" was Juárez (his maternal surname).<sup id="cite_ref-corona_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-corona-12">[12]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indexing">Indexing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_customs_of_Hispanic_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Indexing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Manual_of_Style" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago Manual of Style">Chicago Manual of Style</a>, Spanish and Hispanophone names are <a href="/wiki/Subject_indexing" title="Subject indexing">indexed</a> by the family name. In case there are two family names, the indexing is done under the father's family name; this would be the first element of the surname if the father's and mother's or husband's family names are joined by a <i>y</i>. Depending upon the person involved, the particle <i>de</i> may be treated as a part of a family name or it may be separated from a family name. The indexing of Hispanophone names differs from that of Portuguese of Lusophone names, where the indexing occurs from the final element of the name.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> </p>
