      <p>The <b>United States National Library of Medicine</b> (<b>NLM</b>), operated by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">United States federal government</a>, is the world's largest <a href="/wiki/Medical_library" title="Medical library">medical library</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Located in <a href="/wiki/Bethesda,_Maryland" title="Bethesda, Maryland">Bethesda, Maryland</a>, the NLM is an institute within the <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a>. Its collections include more than seven million <a href="/wiki/Book" title="Book">books</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scientific_journal" title="Scientific journal">journals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Technical_report" title="Technical report">technical reports</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manuscript" title="Manuscript">manuscripts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microform" title="Microform">microfilms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Photograph" title="Photograph">photographs</a>, and images on medicine and related sciences, including some of the world's oldest and rarest works. </p>
      <p>The current director of the NLM is <a href="/wiki/Patricia_Flatley_Brennan" title="Patricia Flatley Brennan">Patricia Flatley Brennan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-welcomes_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-welcomes-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publications_and_informational_resources">Publications and informational resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_National_Library_of_Medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Publications and informational resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Since 1879, the National Library of Medicine has published the <a href="/wiki/Index_Medicus" title="Index Medicus">Index Medicus</a>, a monthly guide to articles, in nearly five thousand selected journals. The last issue of Index Medicus was printed in December 2004, but this information is offered in the freely accessible <a href="/wiki/PubMed" title="PubMed">PubMed</a>, among the more than fifteen million <a href="/wiki/MEDLINE" title="MEDLINE">MEDLINE</a> journal article references and abstracts going back to the 1960s and 1.5 million references going back to the 1950s.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The National Library of Medicine runs the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Biotechnology_Information" title="National Center for Biotechnology Information">National Center for Biotechnology Information</a>, which houses <a href="/wiki/Biological_database" title="Biological database">biological databases</a> (PubMed among them) that are freely accessible on the Internet through the <a href="/wiki/Entrez" title="Entrez">Entrez</a> search engine <sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lister_Hill_National_Center_For_Biomedical_Communications&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lister Hill National Center For Biomedical Communications (page does not exist)">Lister Hill National Center For Biomedical Communications</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> As the United States National Release Center for <a href="/wiki/SNOMED_CT" title="SNOMED CT">SNOMED CT</a>, NLM provides SNOMED CT data and resources to licensees of the NLM <a href="/wiki/UMLS" class="mw-redirect" title="UMLS">UMLS</a> Metathesaurus.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> NLM maintains <a href="/wiki/ClinicalTrials.gov" title="ClinicalTrials.gov">ClinicalTrials.gov</a> registry for human interventional and observational studies. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicology_and_environmental_health">Toxicology and environmental health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_National_Library_of_Medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Toxicology and environmental health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The Toxicology and Environmental Health Program was established at the National Library of Medicine in 1967 and is charged with developing computer databases compiled from the medical literature and from the files of governmental and nongovernmental organizations.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The program has implemented several information systems for chemical emergency response and public education, such as the <a href="/wiki/Toxicology_Data_Network" class="mw-redirect" title="Toxicology Data Network">Toxicology Data Network</a>, <a href="/wiki/TOXMAP" title="TOXMAP">TOXMAP</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tox_Town&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tox Town (page does not exist)">Tox Town</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wireless_Information_System_for_Emergency_Responders" title="Wireless Information System for Emergency Responders">Wireless Information System for Emergency Responders</a>, Toxmystery, and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Household_Products_Database&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Household Products Database (page does not exist)">Household Products Database</a>. These resources are accessible without charge on the internet. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiation_exposure">Radiation exposure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_National_Library_of_Medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Radiation exposure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The United States National Library of Medicine Radiation Emergency Management System<sup id="cite_ref-remm_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-remm-11">[11]</a></sup> provides: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Guidance for health care providers, primarily physicians, about clinical diagnosis and treatment of radiation injury during radiological and nuclear emergencies</li> 
       <li>Just-in-time, evidence-based, usable information with sufficient background and context to make complex issues understandable to those without formal radiation medicine expertise</li> 
       <li>Web-based information that may be downloaded in advance, so that it would be available during an emergency if the Internet were not accessible</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>Radiation Emergency Management System is produced by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Health_and_Human_Services" title="United States Department of Health and Human Services">United States Department of Health and Human Services</a>, Office of the Assistant Secretary for Preparedness and Response, Office of Planning and Emergency Operations, in cooperation with the <a href="/wiki/National_Library_of_Medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="National Library of Medicine">National Library of Medicine</a>, Division of Specialized Information Services, with subject matter experts from the <a href="/wiki/National_Cancer_Institute" title="National Cancer Institute">National Cancer Institute</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention" title="Centers for Disease Control and Prevention">Centers for Disease Control and Prevention</a>, and many U.S. and international consultants.<sup id="cite_ref-remm_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-remm-11">[11]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extramural_division">Extramural division</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_National_Library_of_Medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Extramural division">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The Extramural Division provides grants to support research in medical information science and to support planning and development of computer and communications systems in medical institutions. Research, publications, and exhibitions on the history of medicine and the life sciences also are supported by the History of Medicine Division. In April 2008 the current exhibition <a href="/wiki/Against_the_Odds:_Making_a_Difference_in_Global_Health" title="Against the Odds: Making a Difference in Global Health">Against the Odds: Making a Difference in Global Health</a> was launched. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Center_for_Biotechnology_Information_division">National Center for Biotechnology Information division</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_National_Library_of_Medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: National Center for Biotechnology Information division">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Biotechnology_Information" title="National Center for Biotechnology Information">National Center for Biotechnology Information</a> is an intramural division within National Library of Medicine that creates public databases in molecular biology, conducts research in <a href="/wiki/Computational_biology" title="Computational biology">computational biology</a>, develops software tools for analyzing molecular and genomic data, and disseminates biomedical information, all for the better understanding of processes affecting human health and disease. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_National_Library_of_Medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>For details of the pre-1956 history of the Library, see <a href="/wiki/Library_of_the_Surgeon_General%27s_Office" title="Library of the Surgeon General's Office">Library of the Surgeon General's Office</a>.</i>
       </dd>
      </dl> 
      <p>The precursor of the National Library of Medicine, established in 1836, was the <a href="/wiki/Library_of_the_Surgeon_General%27s_Office" title="Library of the Surgeon General's Office">Library of the Surgeon General's Office</a>, a part of the office of the <a href="/wiki/Surgeon_General_of_the_United_States_Army" title="Surgeon General of the United States Army">Surgeon General of the United States Army</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_Institute_of_Pathology" title="Armed Forces Institute of Pathology">Armed Forces Institute of Pathology</a> and its Medical Museum were founded in 1862 as the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_Health_and_Medicine" title="National Museum of Health and Medicine">Army Medical Museum</a>. Throughout their history the Library of the Surgeon General's Office and the Army Medical Museum often shared quarters. From 1866 to 1887, they were housed in <a href="/wiki/Ford%27s_Theatre" title="Ford's Theatre">Ford's Theatre</a> after production there was stopped, following the assassination of President <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a>. </p>
      <p>In 1956, the library collection was transferred from the control of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">U.S. Department of Defense</a> to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Public_Health_Service" title="United States Public Health Service">Public Health Service</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Health_and_Human_Services" title="United States Department of Health and Human Services">Department of Health, Education, and Welfare</a> and renamed the National Library of Medicine, through the instrumentality of <a href="/wiki/Frank_Bradway_Rogers" title="Frank Bradway Rogers">Frank Bradway Rogers</a>, who was the director from 1956 to 1963. The library moved to its current quarters in <a href="/wiki/Bethesda,_Maryland" title="Bethesda, Maryland">Bethesda, Maryland</a>, on the campus of the National Institutes of Health, in 1962. </p>
