      <p>The <b>1983 United Kingdom general election</b> was held on Thursday, 9 June 1983. It gave the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> the most decisive election victory since that of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> in <a href="/wiki/1945_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="1945 United Kingdom general election">1945</a>. </p>
      <p>Thatcher's first four years as <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a> had not been an easy time.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[1]</a></sup> Unemployment increased during the first three years of her premiership and the economy went <a href="/wiki/Early_1980s_recession" title="Early 1980s recession">through a recession</a>. However, the British victory in the <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a> led to a recovery of her personal popularity; the economy had also returned to growth. By the time Thatcher called the election in May 1983, the Conservatives were most people's firm favourites to win the general election. The resulting win earned the Conservatives their biggest parliamentary majority of the post-war era, and their second-biggest majority as a single-party government, behind only the <a href="/wiki/1924_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="1924 United Kingdom general election">1924 election</a> (they earned even more seats in the <a href="/wiki/1931_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="1931 United Kingdom general election">1931 election</a>, but were part of the <a href="/wiki/National_Government_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="National Government (UK)">National Government</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-bbcoverview_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbcoverview-3">[2]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The Labour Party had been led by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Foot" title="Michael Foot">Michael Foot</a> since the resignation of former Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/James_Callaghan" title="James Callaghan">James Callaghan</a> in 1980. They had fared well in opinion polls and local elections during this time, but issues developed which would lead directly to their defeat. Labour adopted a platform that was considered more <a href="/wiki/Left-wing" class="mw-redirect" title="Left-wing">left-wing</a> than usual.<sup id="cite_ref-bbcoverview_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbcoverview-3">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> Several moderate Labour MPs had defected from the party to form the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_(UK)" title="Social Democratic Party (UK)">Social Democratic Party</a> (SDP); they then formed the <a href="/wiki/SDP%E2%80%93Liberal_Alliance" title="SDP–Liberal Alliance">SDP–Liberal Alliance</a> with the existing <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal Party</a>. </p>
      <p>The opposition vote split almost evenly between the Alliance and Labour. With its worst electoral performance since <a href="/wiki/1918_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="1918 United Kingdom general election">1918</a>, the Labour vote fell by over 3 million votes from 1979 and this accounted for both a national swing of almost 4% towards the Conservatives and their larger parliamentary majority of 144 seats, even though the Conservatives' total vote fell by almost 700,000. This was the last general election where a governing party increased its number of seats until <a href="/wiki/2015_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="2015 United Kingdom general election">2015</a>. </p>
      <p>The Alliance finished in third place but came within 700,000 votes of out-polling Labour; by gaining 25.4% of the vote it won the largest percentage for any third party since <a href="/wiki/1923_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="1923 United Kingdom general election">sixty years prior</a>. Despite this, they won only 23 seats, whereas Labour won 209. The Liberals argued that a proportional electoral system would have given them a more representative number of MPs. Changing the electoral system had been a long-running Liberal Party campaign plank and would later be adopted by the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a>. </p>
      <p>The election night was broadcast live on the BBC, and was presented by <a href="/wiki/David_Dimbleby" title="David Dimbleby">David Dimbleby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sir_Robin_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Robin Day">Sir Robin Day</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Snow" title="Peter Snow">Peter Snow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> It was also broadcast on ITV, and presented by <a href="/wiki/Alastair_Burnet" title="Alastair Burnet">Alastair Burnet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Sissons" title="Peter Sissons">Peter Sissons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martyn_Lewis" title="Martyn Lewis">Martyn Lewis</a>. </p>
      <p>Three future leaders of the Labour Party (<a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Brown" title="Gordon Brown">Gordon Brown</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Corbyn" title="Jeremy Corbyn">Jeremy Corbyn</a>) were first elected at this election – two went on hold the office of Prime Minister, whilst Corbyn became Labour leader in 2015. Former Labour Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Harold_Wilson" title="Harold Wilson">Harold Wilson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Williams" title="Shirley Williams">Shirley Williams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Rodgers,_Baron_Rodgers_of_Quarry_Bank" title="Bill Rodgers, Baron Rodgers of Quarry Bank">Bill Rodgers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joan_Lestor,_Baroness_Lestor_of_Eccles" class="mw-redirect" title="Joan Lestor, Baroness Lestor of Eccles">Joan Lestor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tony_Benn" title="Tony Benn">Tony Benn</a> left Parliament as a result of this election, although Benn would return in a <a href="/wiki/1984_Chesterfield_by-election" title="1984 Chesterfield by-election">by-election the following year</a>, and Lestor at the following general election. In addition, two future Leaders of the Liberal Democrats were first elected (<a href="/wiki/Paddy_Ashdown" title="Paddy Ashdown">Paddy Ashdown</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Kennedy" title="Charles Kennedy">Charles Kennedy</a>), and one of the Conservative Party (<a href="/wiki/Michael_Howard" title="Michael Howard">Michael Howard</a>). </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_campaign">Background and campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1983_United_Kingdom_general_election&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Michael_Foot" title="Michael Foot">Michael Foot</a> was <a href="/wiki/1980_Labour_Party_leadership_election_(UK)" title="1980 Labour Party leadership election (UK)">elected leader</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> in 1980, replacing <a href="/wiki/James_Callaghan" title="James Callaghan">James Callaghan</a>. The election of Foot signalled that the core of the party was swinging to the left and the move exacerbated divisions within the party. In 1981 a group of senior figures including <a href="/wiki/Roy_Jenkins" title="Roy Jenkins">Roy Jenkins</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Owen" title="David Owen">David Owen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Rodgers,_Baron_Rodgers_of_Quarry_Bank" title="Bill Rodgers, Baron Rodgers of Quarry Bank">Bill Rodgers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Williams" title="Shirley Williams">Shirley Williams</a> left Labour to found the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_(UK)" title="Social Democratic Party (UK)">Social Democratic Party</a> (SDP). The SDP agreed to a pact with the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberals</a> for the 1983 election and stood as "<a href="/wiki/SDP%E2%80%93Liberal_Alliance" title="SDP–Liberal Alliance">The Alliance</a>". </p>
      <p>The campaign displayed the huge divisions between the two major parties. Thatcher had been highly unpopular during her first two years in office until the swift and decisive victory in the <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a>, coupled with an improving economy, considerably raised her standings in the polls. The Conservatives' key issues included employment, economic growth and defence. Labour's campaign manifesto involved leaving the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">European Economic Community</a>, abolishing the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>, abandoning the United Kingdom's nuclear deterrent by cancelling <a href="/wiki/Trident_nuclear_programme" class="mw-redirect" title="Trident nuclear programme">Trident</a> and removing cruise missiles—a programme dubbed by Labour MP <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Kaufman" title="Gerald Kaufman">Gerald Kaufman</a> "<a href="/wiki/The_longest_suicide_note_in_history" title="The longest suicide note in history">the longest suicide note in history</a>"; "Although, at barely 37 pages, it only seemed interminable", noted <a href="/wiki/Roy_Hattersley" title="Roy Hattersley">Roy Hattersley</a>. Pro-Labour political journalist <a href="/wiki/Michael_White_(journalist)" title="Michael White (journalist)">Michael White</a>, writing in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, commented: "There was something magnificently brave about <a href="/wiki/Michael_Foot" title="Michael Foot">Michael Foot</a>'s campaign but it was like the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Somme" title="Battle of the Somme">Battle of the Somme</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Results">Results</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1983_United_Kingdom_general_election&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Results">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Further information: 
       <a href="/wiki/Results_of_the_1983_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="Results of the 1983 United Kingdom general election">Results of the 1983 United Kingdom general election</a>
      </div> 
      <p>The election saw a <a href="/wiki/Landslide_victory" title="Landslide victory">landslide victory</a> for the Conservatives, achieving their best results since <a href="/wiki/1935_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="1935 United Kingdom general election">1935</a>. Although there was a slight drop in their share of the vote, they made significant gains at the expense of Labour. The night was a disaster for the Labour Party; their share of the vote fell by over 9%, which meant they were only 700,000 votes ahead of the newly-formed <a href="/wiki/Third_party_(politics)" title="Third party (politics)">third party</a>, the SDP–Liberal Alliance. The massive increase of support for the Alliance at the expense of Labour meant that, in many seats,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (June 2018)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> the collapse in the Labour vote allowed the Conservatives to gain. Despite winning over 25% of the national vote, the Alliance got fewer than 4% of seats, 186 fewer than Labour. The most significant Labour loss of the night was <a href="/wiki/Tony_Benn" title="Tony Benn">Tony Benn</a>, who was defeated in the revived <a href="/wiki/Bristol_East_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Bristol East (UK Parliament constituency)">Bristol East</a> seat. SDP President <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Williams" title="Shirley Williams">Shirley Williams</a>, then a prominent leader in the Social Democratic Party, lost her <a href="/wiki/Crosby_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Crosby (UK Parliament constituency)">Crosby</a> seat which she had won in a <a href="/wiki/1981_Crosby_by-election" title="1981 Crosby by-election">by-election in 1981</a>. <a href="/wiki/William_Rodgers,_Baron_Rodgers_of_Quarry_Bank" class="mw-redirect" title="William Rodgers, Baron Rodgers of Quarry Bank">Bill Rodgers</a>, another leading figure in the Alliance (like Williams, one of the "<a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_(UK)" title="Social Democratic Party (UK)">Gang of Four</a>") also failed to win his old seat that he previously held as a Labour MP. </p>
      <p>In Scotland, both Labour and the Tories sustained modest losses to the Alliance. Labour remained by far the largest party, with 41 seats to 21 for the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Conservatives" title="Scottish Conservatives">Scottish Conservatives</a>. The Scottish Conservatives have been unable to match their 1983 Westminster seat total since, although they did record a slightly larger share of the Scottish vote in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2017_(Scotland)" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom general election, 2017 (Scotland)">2017</a>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1983_United_Kingdom_general_election&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 40em; -webkit-column-width: 40em; column-width: 40em; list-style-type: lower-alpha;"> 
       <ol class="references"> 
        <li id="cite_note-boundary_change-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-boundary_change_1-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-boundary_change_1-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-boundary_change_1-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">Indicates boundary change—so this is a nominal figure.</span> </li> 
        <li id="cite_note-7"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-7">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Includes official Liberal candidates who were not given national Alliance endorsement in three constituencies: <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_Broadgreen_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Liverpool Broadgreen (UK Parliament constituency)">Liverpool Broadgreen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hackney_South_and_Shoreditch_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Hackney South and Shoreditch (UK Parliament constituency)">Hackney South and Shoreditch</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hammersmith_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Hammersmith (UK Parliament constituency)">Hammersmith</a>.</span> </li> 
        <li id="cite_note-8"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-8">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The <a href="/wiki/SDP%E2%80%93Liberal_Alliance" title="SDP–Liberal Alliance">SDP–Liberal Alliance</a> vote is compared with the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal Party</a> vote in the 1979 election.</span> </li> 
        <li id="cite_note-9"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-9">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The Independent Unionist elected in the <a href="/wiki/1979_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="1979 United Kingdom general election">1979 election</a> defended and held his seat for the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Popular_Unionist_Party" title="Ulster Popular Unionist Party">Ulster Popular Unionist Party</a>. The <a href="/wiki/United_Ulster_Unionist_Party" title="United Ulster Unionist Party">United Ulster Unionist Party</a> dissolved and its sole MP did not re-stand.</span> </li> 
        <li id="cite_note-10"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-10">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The <a href="/wiki/Frank_Maguire_(politician)" title="Frank Maguire (politician)">Independent Republican</a> elected in the <a href="/wiki/1979_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="1979 United Kingdom general election">1979 election</a> died in 1981. In the ensuring by-election the seat was won by <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Sands" title="Bobby Sands">Bobby Sands</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Anti-H-Block/Armagh_Political_Prisoner" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-H-Block/Armagh Political Prisoner">Anti-H-Block/Armagh Political Prisoner</a> who then died and was succeeded by an <a href="/wiki/Anti-H-Block_Proxy_Political_Prisoner" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-H-Block Proxy Political Prisoner">Anti-H-Block Proxy Political Prisoner</a> candidate <a href="/wiki/Owen_Carron" title="Owen Carron">Owen Carron</a>. He defended and lost his seat standing for <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn Féin">Sinn Féin</a> who contested seats in Northern Ireland for the first time since <a href="/wiki/1959_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="1959 United Kingdom general election">1959</a>.</span> </li> 
        <li id="cite_note-11"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-11">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">This election was fought under revised boundaries. The changes reflect those comparing to the notional results on the new boundaries. One significant change was the increase in the number of seats allocated to <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> from 12 to 17.</span> </li> 
       </ol>
