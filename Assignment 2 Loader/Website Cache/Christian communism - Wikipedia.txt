      <p><b>Christian communism</b> is a form of <a href="/wiki/Religious_communism" title="Religious communism">religious communism</a> based on <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>. It is a theological and political theory based upon the view that the teachings of <a href="/wiki/Jesus_in_Christianity" title="Jesus in Christianity">Jesus Christ</a> compel <a href="/wiki/Christians" title="Christians">Christians</a> to support <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communism</a> as the ideal <a href="/wiki/Social_system" title="Social system">social system</a>. Although there is no universal agreement on the exact date when Christian communism was founded, many Christian communists assert that evidence from the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> (in the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">Acts of the Apostles</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-Bible_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bible-1">[1]</a></sup> suggests that the first Christians, including the <a href="/wiki/Apostles" title="Apostles">apostles</a>, established their own small communist society in the years following Jesus' death and resurrection.<sup id="cite_ref-Bible_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bible-1">[1]</a></sup> As such, many advocates of Christian communism argue that it was taught by Jesus and practiced by the apostles themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Some independent historians confirm it.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Montero2017_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Montero2017-14">[14]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basis">Basis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_communism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
      <p> Christian communists regard biblical texts in Acts 2 and 4 as evidence that the first Christians lived in a communist society. </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>Acts 2:44-45, "All who believed were together and had all things in common; 45 they would sell their possessions and goods and distribute the proceeds to all, as any had need."<br><br> Acts 4:32-35, "Now the whole group of those who believed were of one heart and soul, and no one claimed private ownership of any possessions, but everything they owned was held in common. ... 34 There was not a needy person among them, for as many as owned lands or houses sold them and brought the proceeds of what was sold. 35 They laid it at the apostles' feet, and it was distributed to each as any had need."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>Montero offers anthropological evidence that the practices recounted in Acts 4:32–35 were historical and were practiced widely and taken seriously during at least the first two centuries of Christianity.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Other biblical evidence of <a href="/wiki/Anti-capitalism" title="Anti-capitalism">anti-capitalistic</a> belief-systems include Matthew 6:24: "No one can serve two masters. Either you will hate the one and love the other or you will be devoted to the one and despise the other. You cannot serve both God and money."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The slogan "Each according to his abilities" has biblical origins too. Act 11:29 states: "29 Then the disciples, every man according to his ability, determined to send relief unto the brethren which dwelt in Judaea". The phrase <a href="/wiki/From_each_according_to_his_ability,_to_each_according_to_his_needs" title="From each according to his ability, to each according to his needs">"To each according to his needs"</a> also has a biblical basis - Acts 4:35: "[...] to the emissaries to distribute to each according to his need". <i>Preaching</i> by Thomas Wharton Collens (1812-1879) describes biblical sources supporting a common-property society.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (January 2019)">page&nbsp;needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>Christian communism does not depend merely on the principles of the early apostles. In fact, Christian communists claim that <a href="/wiki/Anti-capitalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-capitalist">anti-capitalist</a> ideals are deeply rooted in the Christian faith. While modern <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> had not yet formed in the time of Christ, his message was overwhelmingly against the love of money (greed) and in support of the poor. Christian communists see the principles of Christ as staunchly anti-capitalist in nature. Since "the love of money is a root of all kinds of evil" (1 Tim 6:10 NRSV), it seems natural for Christians to oppose a social system founded - as Christian communists claim - entirely on the love of money. In fact, Christian opposition to the emergence of such a system largely delayed <a href="/wiki/History_of_capitalism" title="History of capitalism">capitalist development</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and capitalism did not gather popular support until <a href="/wiki/John_Calvin" title="John Calvin">John Calvin</a> (1509-1564) endorsed capitalist practice from a religious perspective.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The ideals of Christian communism are pre-Calvinist and as such seek to return Christianity to its anti-capitalist roots by progressing beyond it into <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a> and finally to <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communism</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_communism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atheism_and_communism">Atheism and communism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_communism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Atheism and communism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Contemporary communism, including contemporary Christian communism, owes much to <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxist thought</a>—particularly <a href="/wiki/Marxian_economics" title="Marxian economics">Marxist economics</a>. Not all communists are in full agreement with Marxism, but it is difficult to find any communists today who do not agree at least with the Marxist critique of capitalism. Marxism includes a complex array of views that cover several different fields of human knowledge and one may easily distinguish between <a href="/wiki/Marxist_philosophy" title="Marxist philosophy">Marxist philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marxist_sociology" title="Marxist sociology">Marxist sociology</a> and Marxist economics. Marxist sociology and Marxist economics have no connection to religious issues and make no assertions about such things. On the other hand, Marxist philosophy is famously <a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">atheistic</a>, although some Marxist scholars, both Christian and non-Christian, have insisted that Marxist philosophy and the philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">Friedrich Engels</a> are significantly different from one another and that this difference needs recognition. In particular, Jose Porfirio Miranda found Marx and Engels to be consistently opposed to deterministic materialism and broadly sympathetic towards Christianity and towards the text of the Bible, although disbelieving in a supernatural deity.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Vladimir Lenin</a> also allowed Christians in the <a href="/wiki/Bolshevik_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="Bolshevik Party">Bolshevik Party</a>. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Establishing_Christian_communism">Establishing Christian communism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_communism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Establishing Christian communism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Jesus_Revolution.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Jesus_Revolution.svg/220px-Jesus_Revolution.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="236" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Jesus_Revolution.svg/330px-Jesus_Revolution.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Jesus_Revolution.svg/440px-Jesus_Revolution.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="719" data-file-height="772"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Jesus_Revolution.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>Jesus as a revolutionary
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>There is also the question of how a <a href="/wiki/Communist_society" title="Communist society">communist society</a> should be actually achieved. While orthodox communists advocate a form of violent <a href="/wiki/Revolution" title="Revolution">revolution</a>, Christian communists almost universally insist on <a href="/wiki/Nonviolence" title="Nonviolence">nonviolent</a> means, such as <a href="/wiki/Nonviolent_resistance" title="Nonviolent resistance">passive resistance</a> or winning <a href="/wiki/Election" title="Election">elections</a>. Some groups are <a href="/wiki/Pacifism" title="Pacifism">pacifists</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Hutterites" title="Hutterites">Hutterites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bruderhof_Communities" title="Bruderhof Communities">Bruderhof</a>, who regard all force as wrong, including forcing a new society on someone.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Regarding the issue of the <a href="/wiki/Nationalization" title="Nationalization">nationalization</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Means_of_production" title="Means of production">means of production</a>, Christian communists argue that capitalism itself is a form of institutionalized theft in the manner that capitalist owners exploit their workers by not paying them the full value of their labour. However, not all Christian communists seek to achieve large-scale social change. Some believe that rather than attempting to transform the politics and economics of an entire country, Christians should instead establish communism at a local or regional level only. </p>
      <p>According to theologians such as Leonardo Boff, the Latin American branch of Christian communist <a href="/wiki/Liberation_theology" title="Liberation theology">liberation theology</a> is rooted in the concept that "prudence is the understanding of situations of radical crisis".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Among Christian communists, <a href="/wiki/Historical_materialism" title="Historical materialism">historical materialism</a> is utilized as a methodology of analysis to define the nature of the crisis in question as a product of political-economic dynamics and modalities derived from the workings of what is termed "the late capitalist/imperialist mode of production". According to this subset of liberation theology, the challenge for the Christian communist is then to define what it means (in context of "a concrete analysis of the concrete social reality") to affirm a "preferential option for the poor and oppressed" as <a href="/wiki/Praxis_(process)" title="Praxis (process)">praxis</a> (active theory)<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and as commanded by an <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a> allegedly "rooted in the beatidic teachings of Jesus". Christian communist liberation theology is not about <a href="/wiki/Evangelism" title="Evangelism">evangelization</a> per se, but rather about developing an <a href="/wiki/Orthopraxis" class="mw-redirect" title="Orthopraxis">orthopraxis</a> (ethical action, i.e. the condition of coming to the light by doing the works of God)<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> that aims to reconcile the "beatidic ethics" of Jesus as espoused in the <a href="/wiki/Sermon_on_the_Mount" title="Sermon on the Mount">Sermon on the Mount</a> with existing social struggles against what is termed "<a href="/wiki/Neo-colonialism" class="mw-redirect" title="Neo-colonialism">neo-colonialism</a>" or "<a href="/wiki/Late_capitalism" title="Late capitalism">late capitalism</a>". Both Christian communism and liberation theology stress orthopraxis over <a href="/wiki/Orthodoxy" title="Orthodoxy">orthodoxy</a>. A narrative of the nature of contemporary social struggles is developed via materialist analysis utilizing historiographic concepts developed by Karl Marx. A concrete example are the Paraguayan <i>Sin Tierra</i> (landless) movement,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> who engage in direct land seizures and the establishment of socialized agricultural cooperative production in <i>asentamientos</i>. The contemporary Paraguayan <i>Sin Tierra</i> operate in a very similar manner as that of the reformation era <a href="/wiki/Diggers" title="Diggers">Diggers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> For <a href="/wiki/Camilo_Torres_Restrepo" title="Camilo Torres Restrepo">Camilo Torres</a> (the founder of the <a href="/wiki/National_Liberation_Army_(Colombia)" title="National Liberation Army (Colombia)">Colombian guerrilla group ELN</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> developing this orthopraxis meant celebrating the <a href="/wiki/Eucharist_in_the_Catholic_Church" title="Eucharist in the Catholic Church">Catholic Eucharist</a> only among those engaged in armed struggle against the army of the Colombian state while fighting alongside them.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> </p>
